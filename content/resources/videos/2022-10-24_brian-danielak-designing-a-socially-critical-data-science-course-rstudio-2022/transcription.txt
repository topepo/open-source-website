<h3>Act 2: Data analysis for social action</h3>

<p><a data-t="330">So when I was teaching at Michigan State, a very local crisis was getting international attention, and it had to do with the poisoning of the water supply in Flint, Michigan.</a> <a data-t="345">Flint's about an hour away from East Lansing, which is where I was teaching.</a> <a data-t="351">The super short version is that to save money, the city of Flint decided to stop importing water from the Great Lakes and divert the Flint River instead, and the Flint River's toxic corrosiveness leached lead out of the pipes.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="367">This was happening while we were teaching the course, and a data set had just come out from folks at Virginia Tech who had sent at-home testing kits to 270 families in Flint to get readings on their water levels.</a> <a data-t="384">And we had students analyze this and write about it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="388">Here's one example of a student analysis where what they've done is try to compute the number of samples that exceed the EPA's action limit.</a> <a data-t="397">So the EPA says if more than 10% of your samples are above this threshold, which happens to be .015 milligrams per liter, I think, you've got a problem.</a> <a data-t="408">And a student wrote some code to count up how many of the raw samples there are that exceed that amount, as well as the percentage that exceed that amount.</a> <a data-t="417">The other thing I want to point out to you is it's difficult to see, perhaps, but that distribution has a real long tail.</a> <a data-t="424">And there are some folks out there at .2 or, yeah, sorry, .12, .14, six, eight times the legal limit for lead.</a> <a data-t="433">That's a huge problem.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="435">So we had our students write to the governor of Michigan with their analysis results.</a> <a data-t="441">And here's an example student one.</a> <a data-t="443">I constructed a histogram.</a> <a data-t="444">I also wrote a simple program to compute the number of samples and the percentage above the limit.</a> <a data-t="450">Forty-five samples exceeded the EPA limit, making this 16.6% of the samples above the action limit.</a> <a data-t="457">This indicates the seriousness of this problem and exactly why we need to urgently take action to help Flint.</a></p>

<h3>Act 3: Resources to help you teach</h3>

<p><a data-t="476">You might be asking, what can I do?</a> <a data-t="480">So I have three pieces of advice that I'm going to go into detail on.</a> <a data-t="483">The first is try to choose data and activities that question systems of power.</a> <a data-t="491">The second is do what you can to make bias issues in your data known and check for them.</a> <a data-t="500">And the third, which I think is arguably the most important, is to adopt a critical framework to analyze and expose oppression.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="510">So first up, you should use the opportunity of being able to choose data sets for your class to analyze to ask what issues demand our attention socially.</a> <a data-t="522">Now, I don't want to stand here and say that every activity that you do must always be focused on improving the social good.</a> <a data-t="528">But I think even if you started with one activity, you'd find it has a ripple impact, and it gets you thinking about more data that can do this, and it gets your students thinking about more ways they can try and help society through the newfound powers they're learning in your courses.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="548">Second, you can check data for biases.</a> <a data-t="553">You can ask who is being disadvantaged in this data.</a> <a data-t="560">And so a tool for that is IBM's AI360.</a> <a data-t="563">I've got the link down there, but also I'll have a QR code you can scan, which has all the links that are related to this presentation, so don't panic.</a> <a data-t="568">The AI360 has some demo data that you can take a look at, and this is an example of what it can produce for you.</a> <a data-t="575">This data was analyzed and has a privileged group, which happen to be females, and an unprivileged group, which happen to be males, because we have representational issues of unequal weighting.</a></p>
