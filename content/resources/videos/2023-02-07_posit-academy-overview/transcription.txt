<h3>The forgetting problem</h3>

<p><a data-t="454">So I thought, the world looks pretty good, but I still have a background in psychology and I started exploring educational psychology, the literature of psychology that's related to education, and there are some disturbing signals in there, and I'll share some of those with you.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="471">For example, the best we could tell based on research is that students only remember about 56%, so about half of the content, that you tell them in a lecture.</a> <a data-t="487">And that's if you test them immediately after the lecture.</a> <a data-t="492">And we expect that this drops off from there as time goes by.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="502">Not only is this disturbing, the students who are being tested here are undergraduates at college who we might think of as professional learners.</a> <a data-t="510">If they can only manage to remember half of what they learned in a lecture, then the distracted adult let's go right back to all their other work responsibilities, probably go learn even less.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="520">Next other studies, here's a study that looks at procedural instruction.</a> <a data-t="524">So this is more like teaching someone how to do a procedure, whether that's using a machine or fitting a certain model or something that you do, it's a behavior.</a> <a data-t="536">And they find the same numbers immediately after the training, where the students are doing the thing, only 60% of them can reproduce it.</a> <a data-t="546">And then six months later, that drops to 40%.</a> <a data-t="543">One year later, that drops to 30% and so on.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="547">And most trainers I discovered weren't aware of this at all.</a> <a data-t="553">But you can look at the students being trained and kind of get the sense that this is exactly what is happening.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="567">I went back to my workshops, and I continued to teach, and I continued to get rave reviews.</a> <a data-t="571">And I realized what all the knobs were to make student satisfaction really high in a workshop.</a> <a data-t="578">But I also realized they don't really relate to the stickiness of what the student's learning.</a> <a data-t="582">And anecdotally, I started to check back in on some of my students, and I realized, even though they love the workshop, and they could do lots of great things in the workshop, they weren't doing those things a year later or six months later.</a> <a data-t="600">To me, that seems like a training failure.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="600">To me, that seems like a training failure.</a> <a data-t="609">And I'm not going to speak for everyone, but I almost feel like it's a dirty secret of the training industry that you don't get terrific outcomes as a trainer.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="609">And I'm not going to speak for everyone, but I almost feel like it's a dirty secret of the training industry that you don't get terrific outcomes as a trainer.</a> <a data-t="625">I've heard people talk about marketing and say, you know, we know half of what we do works.</a> <a data-t="629">We just don't know which half.</a> <a data-t="630">Well, it seems like that might be the case for training as well.</a> <a data-t="634">But we're dealing with a field where people aren't trained to be trainers.</a> <a data-t="638">So I think there's lots of upside to do much, much better.</a> <a data-t="642">And that's what we set out to do at RStudio, which is now Posit.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="648">And the way we fix this is by looking into why this happens.</a> <a data-t="652">So some of you may have seen this graph before.</a> <a data-t="655">It's called the forgetting curves or the ebbing house curves based on the gentleman who first postulated it.</a> <a data-t="662">This summarizes a body of research that goes all the way back to the 1800s, has been verified in many different fields.</a> <a data-t="669">It's just a fact of human life.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="671">The way to look at the graph is these blue lines are someone's ability to do something, be that to do a task, to remember vocabulary, to take a test.</a> <a data-t="683">It's just their ability to do that thing that they learned.</a> <a data-t="686">And the first blue line shows how that ability changes over time.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="692">What happens consistently is your ability to do something drops off after you first learn it.</a> <a data-t="703">And especially if you never use it again, your brain basically cannibalizes the resources that devoted that ability and uses them to do other more important things.</a> <a data-t="712">And eventually you can't do that thing anymore.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="719">But you don't have to settle for that outcome.</a> <a data-t="719">If you wait until that thing starts to get difficult for you, and then you practice it and do it again, you're essentially relearning it through your practice.</a> <a data-t="728">And you do it again, and you jump up to the next blue line.</a> <a data-t="731">So your ability started to fall off, but you practice, and now you can do it again pretty well.</a> <a data-t="738">And then it starts to fall off again.</a> <a data-t="741">But it doesn't fall off as fast as it did the first time if you had never practiced it.</a> <a data-t="747">And if you repeat this process and you practice again, you'll be on the next blue line, and then the next blue line.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="753">Whenever you don't use something, your brain does start to lose its ability to do it.</a> <a data-t="757">But the more you practice the thing, the more your brain decides to conserve those neural networks and retain that ability.</a> <a data-t="763">Now if you've practiced something enough times, you've learned to ride the bike and it stays with you forever.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="773">You could sort of see why this doesn't play out in a workshop that you take for half a day or two days or what have you, and then you never really see it again.</a> <a data-t="788">These practice sessions work because your brain is building new neural circuitry in between.</a> <a data-t="793">That means you have to go to sleep, nighttime sleep, in between these sessions to really build up a robust neural network.</a></p>

<h3>How the brain builds skills</h3>

<p><a data-t="818">All right, so we could see in the data that's how that works, but we also know from research and study why it happens.</a> <a data-t="827">This is your brain.</a> <a data-t="828">It's a collection of neurons that are joined together, and your abilities and the information you know and the memories you have are all encoded into your brain as networks between these neurons.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="850">The network is set up so when one neuron in the network fires, the other neurons fire as well, and they trigger whatever behavior needs to come together to make this experience that your brain is recreating for you.</a> <a data-t="871">We can imagine having two networks that do two separate things in our brain.</a> <a data-t="876">One of these we use, every time we use it, those neurons fire together, and then your brain wires them together a little more strongly, because this is how the brain works.</a> <a data-t="885">You probably heard neurons that fire together wire together.</a> <a data-t="889">Every time you use it, that network gets stronger and stronger, whereas the one you don't use gets weaker and weaker.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="895">Your brain has important things to do.</a> <a data-t="896">It's not going to maintain a network that doesn't do anything useful until eventually over time you'll have a very, very strong network at the thing you were doing, so strong that doing it no longer takes conscious thought.</a> <a data-t="919">It's completely automated.</a> <a data-t="920">When one of the neurons in the network fires due to some cue or trigger, all the rest just automatically cascade, and you juggle the ball, or fit the model, or interpret the data.</a> <a data-t="931">Whereas that other neural network that we never use, it doesn't even exist anymore.</a> <a data-t="935">It's gone.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="937">This is the biological phenomenon that explains the forgetting curves.</a> <a data-t="942">Like I said, this wiring together occurs while you're sleeping, so this is something that happens with lots of repetition over a long period of time, weeks, let's say.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="952">If we go back to our model and think about, do we want our colleague to be able to do this new skill, pick a skill where the answer is yes, and then consider, what is going to determine whether or not he or she could do that six months from now?</a> <a data-t="967">You'll discover it's very little to do with the initial instruction we give the colleague and everything to do with the amount of practice they've had shortly after that instruction.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="981">How do we train to take advantage of this?</a> <a data-t="984">How do we train to make people who can use R as well as SAS six months from now?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="993">Something else to consider as well, just watching a lecture or watching a video on your computer about doing something probably builds a neural network, but it's a neural network that's related to sitting still and watching a video unfold.</a> <a data-t="1009">It's actually doing the thing that builds a neural network so you'll later use to do the thing.</a> <a data-t="1020">We have to be conscious of that in training too, and unfortunately a lot of training doesn't really focus on the student actually doing the things.</a> <a data-t="1030">It's more, hey, watch this video.</a></p>

<h3>How Posit Academy works</h3>

<p><a data-t="1357">How do humans actually learn skills?</a> <a data-t="1360">Might seem like the training industry is botched, and if you want to help your people transition over to R, you have a hard time of it, but it's not actually the case.</a> <a data-t="1370">We learn skills every day in modalities that are very familiar and that lots of people have confidence in.</a></p>
