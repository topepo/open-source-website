<h3>USGS water data</h3>

<p><a data-t="42">There's a huge variety of data that the USGS collects concerning water.</a> <a data-t="46">Surface water data includes things like gauge height and discharge.</a> <a data-t="50">Scientists use this data to study how water levels are changing over time,</a> <a data-t="54">monitor current river conditions, and predict flooding.</a> <a data-t="58">The USGS also studies groundwater data.</a> <a data-t="61">This includes information about springs and wells that indicate aquifer conditions.</a> <a data-t="66">We can use this data to understand how human activities and climate change affects the supply of groundwater.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="72">There are estimates for how much water is used in the United States and generally what that water is used for.</a> <a data-t="77">Water use data might include things like the volume of water used for irrigation</a> <a data-t="81">or the amount of water in a public water supply system.</a> <a data-t="85">Water quality data includes physical data like temperature, chemical data, and biological data.</a> <a data-t="91">For example, with water quality data we can study the effects of road salt on streams or lakes.</a> <a data-t="96">Or maybe we're looking at chemicals of emerging concern such as pesticides, microplastics, or PFAS.</a></p>

<h3>Origins of the dataRetrieval package</h3>

<p><a data-t="104">I started at the USGS about 13 years ago and not too long after that I was asked to learn R.</a> <a data-t="110">It was around then that the USGS was deciding to move our support officially from SAS to R.</a> <a data-t="115">There were some scientists already using R but it wasn't very widespread.</a> <a data-t="119">For the most part Excel was king.</a> <a data-t="121">Keep in mind </a><a href="/software/rstudio/">RStudio</a><a data-t="121"> at that time hadn't been around much and alternative IDEs were not very user friendly.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="127">One thing that became obvious right away that it was very difficult to get our USGS water data into R.</a> <a data-t="132">This data bottleneck seemed like the first big hurdle that would need to be overcome</a> <a data-t="136">to convince people to invest in the time to learn R.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="140">I'd been hired into a smaller group within the USGS that had a great mix of scientists and software developers.</a> <a data-t="145">This meant that from the very beginning of my R development I was being taught about version control,</a> <a data-t="150">unit testing, project management, and other software management development best practices.</a> <a data-t="155">And so on one fateful day in November 2012 I pushed up a set of code to GitHub.</a> <a data-t="161">There were some hurdles we had to jump through to work in the open</a> <a data-t="164">but I've been lucky to have had extremely supportive supervisors who advocate for open source development.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="171">It did not take long to start seeing some immediate benefits of creating a package to help users get their data.</a> <a data-t="176">Standardizing the data retrieval phase of code made collaborating easier and jumping into analyzing the data quicker.</a> <a data-t="183">More emphasis was being placed on reproducible science and data retrieval made that a little easier.</a> <a data-t="188">Community engagement flourished.</a> <a data-t="190">We introduced still active GitHub and GitLab organizations, social media, blogging, and various training sessions.</a></p>
