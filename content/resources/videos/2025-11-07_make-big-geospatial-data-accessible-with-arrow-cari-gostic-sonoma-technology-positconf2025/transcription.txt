<h3>Introducing Firelytics</h3>

<p><a data-t="36">So our cool data product is called Firelytics.</a> <a data-t="40">And it's a historical database of fire progression in California.</a> <a data-t="44">So that map up there shows the fire footprint in California just for 2020.</a> <a data-t="49">Which, that's pretty scary. That's a lot of land burned in just one year.</a> <a data-t="53">And so I'll circle the largest fire in history, the August fire.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="58">And this is a true value of Firelytics.</a> <a data-t="61">It's a database that holds the spatial progression for every fire that's been satellite detected going back to 2012.</a> <a data-t="68">So this set of growth polygons exists for all fires on that map plus ten more years of data.</a> <a data-t="75">So Firelytics holds over 22,000 fires and millions of polygons.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="81">And this data could be really useful for a variety of audiences.</a> <a data-t="85">For example, scientists making fire predictive models.</a> <a data-t="89">We have people working on fuel reduction or evacuation planning.</a> <a data-t="93">And then we have the general public.</a> <a data-t="95">Anyone who has been in the path of a wildfire recently might find this interesting.</a> <a data-t="99">Or my sister actually pointed out this is really useful for hunting for morel mushrooms, which tend to pop up in burn scars.</a> <a data-t="106">So she was really interested in this.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="109">And my team full of our programmers, our obvious solution to deliver our data to meet the range of everyone here, is our </a><a href="/software/shiny-r/">Shiny</a><a data-t="109"> dashboard.</a> <a data-t="118">And our goal for this dashboard is for a user to pick any fire in that historical record and map its progression over time.</a> <a data-t="125">But generally spatial data is big and it's slow and it's not really well suited for a dashboard where users aren't going to sit around and wait for 10 seconds, 20 seconds for data to load.</a></p>

<h3>Using Arrow for geospatial data</h3>

<p><a data-t="137">And that's where Arrow comes in.</a> <a data-t="140">The Geospatial Arrow suite here, it consists of the Geoparquet file format.</a> <a data-t="145">And we use two R packages, GeoArrow and SFArrow, to help us out.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="151">So I'll show you some benchmarks for that 2020 fire data I showed earlier.</a> <a data-t="155">We saw some savings in memory.</a> <a data-t="159">The Geoparquet file is a little bit smaller than a shape file, but what was really impressive to me and my team is the read-in speed, which is less than half that for Arrow compared to SF.</a> <a data-t="171">And this is just the beginning of the time efficiency offered by Arrow.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="175">If you've used Arrow before, all of the functionalities that are available for tabular data are also available for geospatial data.</a> <a data-t="183">And that includes things like partitioning your data by a grouping variable.</a> <a data-t="187">Here we partition by Fire ID.</a> <a data-t="190">As well as pre-filtering and pre-aggregating your data before loading it into working memory.</a> <a data-t="195">So here we use </a><a href="/software/dplyr/">dplyr</a><a data-t="195"> filter to only load the Fire ID number one into our working memory out of the whole Firelytics database.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="203">And this is really well suited for our dashboard.</a> <a data-t="206">We can very quickly load very small subsets of Firelytics into our app.</a></p>
