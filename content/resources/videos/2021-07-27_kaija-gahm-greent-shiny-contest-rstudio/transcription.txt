<h3>What is grapheme-color synesthesia?</h3>

<p><a data-t="87">So for anyone who isn't sure what this is, synesthesia is a neurological phenomenon where, well there are many different types of synesthesia,</a> <a data-t="97">but there's a particular type called grapheme color synesthesia, and in this case</a> <a data-t="101">people associate letters or numbers or other sort of graphemes, like characters, with colors in their minds.</a> <a data-t="110">And that doesn't mean, and I have this type of synesthesia, that doesn't mean that I actually see colors when I see these letters,</a> <a data-t="117">but I sort of imagine associations between colors and letters.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="117">So I'm showing you a few letters of the alphabet here.</a> <a data-t="123">A is very strongly red for me, B is blue, C is yellow, D is also blue, but a very distinct shade of blue from B, so you get the idea.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="131">And I have had synesthesia as long as I can remember, and these associations have remained constant since I was a kid, and it's just kind of a fun little party trick.</a> <a data-t="140">It's not, it doesn't really affect my daily life very much, but when I tell my friends about my synesthesia, they always want to see my alphabet, they always want to know what color their name is, they want me to show them</a> <a data-t="151">words typed out the way I imagine them in my mind's eye.</a> <a data-t="158">And this is actually not trivially easy to do because you have to change the color of every single letter.</a> <a data-t="163">So for a while I had a Google doc going where I could copy and paste individual letters to show people what different words would look like,</a> <a data-t="168">but that was really clunky and annoying.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="174">So then I started Googling to see if there was a tool online that would allow me to show people my synesthesia, and I came across this project by Bernadette Sheridan,</a> <a data-t="180">where she is also a synesthete, and she created a tool where you can type in any word, in this case I've entered my name, and you see what it looks like in her synesthesia colors.</a> <a data-t="192">And that's really cool, but it's problematic for me because this looks really wrong to me,</a> <a data-t="198">these are the wrong colors.</a> <a data-t="203">Actually several of them happen to line up with mine, but for example K is not yellow-green in my head, it's more of a magenta, and so this bothers me and there was no way to put in my own colors.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="216">So I envisioned an app like this where I would have one color drop-down selector for every single letter of the alphabet and every number, and I'd be able</a> <a data-t="222">to set my own colors and have other people set theirs, and then type in my name and have it appear in my colors so it actually looks right to me, or type in any other word, and so other</a> <a data-t="233">synesthetes could enter their own colors as well.</a> <a data-t="239">So I decided to build it in Shiny.</a></p>

<h3>Demo of the greenT app</h3>

<p><a data-t="261">Okay, so I built the app, and I called it Green Tea because I wanted some sort of cute little pun, and the letter T is green for me.</a> <a data-t="268">It initializes with a bunch of random colors, but you can set them to my colors, so now everything looks very satisfying to me because all the letters are the correct colors, and you see this green T here,</a> <a data-t="278">or you can set them all to white and you can pick your own colors if that is what you want to do,</a> <a data-t="282">if you have synesthesia yourself, or if you just want to create a palette.</a> <a data-t="287">You can download the colors as a CSV that downloads their hex codes and their RGB codes as well, and then you can type anything you want here.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="301">I have it set to type something.</a> <a data-t="301">I could type my name.</a> <a data-t="305">You can see there's a slight delay, and I'll talk about that in a minute.</a> <a data-t="306">I could type, hi Boston, and it shows up in my colors, or it shows up in whatever colors you would like to use.</a> <a data-t="312">You can turn the letters on and off.</a> <a data-t="315">You can download the rectangles as a PNG, so those are the images that showed up on my first slide were just downloaded from my app,</a> <a data-t="319">and then I also added some other features.</a> <a data-t="325">I added an app where, I'm sorry, a page where you can read more about synesthesia and more about why I developed this app,</a> <a data-t="330">and I also have a Google survey mimic page here where you can enter your own information optionally,</a> <a data-t="337">because I was thinking maybe I would do some informal analyses of synesthetes and see if I could figure out any patterns,</a> <a data-t="342">and you can actually submit your own colors, so this listens to what has been selected on this page and actually contributes it to me as a CSV along with your survey responses.</a></p>

<h3>Technical challenges and features</h3>

<p><a data-t="361">So that's just a basic overview of the app itself, but I wanted to talk in particular about a couple things that came up while developing this Shiny app that were really fun for me.</a> <a data-t="370">So I had a little bit of prior experience developing a Shiny app.</a> <a data-t="370">I actually developed one this year for work, and it was much more complex than this app and took me a long time.</a> <a data-t="377">A lot of sort of focused effort all day every day.</a> <a data-t="384">So by comparison, this app was kind of a breeze.</a> <a data-t="384">I had a prototype mocked up in just a couple hours, but then I kept going and adding features and thinking to myself, you know, wouldn't it be cool if it could do this thing?</a> <a data-t="394">Wouldn't it be cool if it could do this thing?</a> <a data-t="394">And I was surprised at how much I could achieve, and so just to call attention to a couple features here that are a little bit more complicated than they seem.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="404">So first let's look at these rectangles.</a> <a data-t="404">This, although it might not look like it, is actually a ggplot.</a> <a data-t="409">It looks like just plain rectangles, but it's actually a ggplot with theme void and geom rect, if anyone's familiar with ggplot.</a> <a data-t="417">And behind the scenes, Shiny is listening to the inputs, the color inputs here, and creating a reactive expression, which is then fed into the ggplot,</a> <a data-t="424">and it remakes the plot every time that there's a change to one of these colors or a change to one of these letters.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="443">So one challenge that I faced with creating this plot is anyone who has used Shiny before might know that the default with Shiny is that reactive expressions all listen to each other all the time.</a> <a data-t="449">So whenever one of them updates, anything that is listening to that reactive expression will automatically update pretty much instantaneously.</a> <a data-t="457">And the challenge actually comes in preventing that from happening instantaneously or delaying things.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="463">So I was having a problem where every single time I tried to type text, for example, if I typed my name, it would refresh five times.</a> <a data-t="477">So you saw that just when I typed that, it basically flashed once.</a> <a data-t="477">It showed you the new graph with my name.</a> <a data-t="483">But before I fixed this, every time I typed a letter, the K, the A, the I, the J, the A, it would do what you just saw, except much faster,</a> <a data-t="489">and it looked really chaotic because it was refreshing every single time there was a change.</a> <a data-t="493">So I had to work in a delay where I used the debounce function to only have Shiny listen to that reactive expression every fraction of a second so that a reasonable person could</a> <a data-t="505">type something relatively quickly and not have the refresh happen on every single letter.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="511">And I want to emphasize that this app absolutely could have existed and could have been functional without that feature.</a> <a data-t="516">But tweaking little things like that to improve the user experience was part of what I really enjoyed about the opportunity to develop this app.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="511">And I want to emphasize that this app absolutely could have existed and could have been functional without that feature.</a> <a data-t="516">But tweaking little things like that to improve the user experience was part of what I really enjoyed about the opportunity to develop this app.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="528">Another thing that I would draw your attention to is this toggle for showing and hiding the letters.</a> <a data-t="532">And this turned out to be a pretty simple on off switch where this switch is set to either true or false depending on whether it's clicked.</a> <a data-t="539">And the reactive expression that generates the ggplot listens to that and replots the plot either with or without a geom text layer depending on the position of this switch.</a> <a data-t="552">And this was another feature where I had sort of dreamed about it.</a> <a data-t="556">Wouldn't it be cool? Maybe some people prefer it with letters, some people prefer it without letters.</a> <a data-t="561">And I never thought that it would end up being this simple to actually execute.</a> <a data-t="565">So I was really happy to be able to get this to work.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="571">One other portion of the app that I want to talk about is these color selectors.</a> <a data-t="571">There are, of course, 36 of them because there's 26 for each letter and then there's 10 for the digits.</a> <a data-t="577">And 36 is a really interesting number because it's few enough that I could type every single one manually to create the selectors.</a> <a data-t="584">I could have 36 lines of code where I create the A, create the B, create the C, create the D.</a> <a data-t="589">And that's how I started the app because I just wanted to get it mocked up really quickly.</a> <a data-t="594">I could copy and paste. No big deal.</a> <a data-t="600">But 36 is also a large enough number that doing something 36 times starts to be annoying after a while.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="607">And so this was a great opportunity for me to incrementally develop my app to move toward better practices in Shiny app development.</a> <a data-t="614">So I started with a lot of repeated lines of code.</a> <a data-t="620">And gradually, gradually, I streamlined everything, made it slightly more efficient until ultimately I ended up at a structure where I wrote a function that would generate each of these color selectors</a> <a data-t="630">and then I applied that function 36 times, feeding in the input IDs and the display text</a> <a data-t="636">and the starting colors each time.</a> <a data-t="642">And that was, again, really satisfying.</a> <a data-t="642">And this app, once again, could have existed, could have been functional without that sort of behind the scenes improvement,</a> <a data-t="646">but it was very satisfying to be able to sort of streamline all of that.</a></p>

<h3>Lessons learned</h3>

<p><a data-t="653">So those are just a few of the features in this app.</a> <a data-t="659">I could go on.</a> <a data-t="659">I wrote a blog post highlighting a couple of the challenges like that that were sort of fun to tackle that made the app what it is today.</a> <a data-t="664">And I'm going to provide a link to that blog post if anybody's interested in seeing my code or interested in reading about the other stuff.</a> <a data-t="668">But I wanted to just take a moment to reflect on some general lessons that I took away from this app for developing Shiny apps in general.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="679">And the first thing, which I've alluded to during this talk, is to start simple and then build up and really don't be afraid to dream about what you'd like to see in the app and then try to make it happen.</a> <a data-t="690">This definitely might not be true for everybody, but at least for me, I'm very much a person who learns best when I have a defined goal and a problem to solve and then I have to figure out how to do it.</a> <a data-t="700">So I did watch some Shiny tutorials when I was first starting out, but I didn't just sit down with a textbook and try to teach myself Shiny in a vacuum.</a> <a data-t="710">I taught myself the bare bones.</a> <a data-t="710">I made a bare bones app and then I thought to myself, you know, what would I like to see and how can I make it happen with really no regard to whether the feature I was trying to develop was appropriate for beginners</a> <a data-t="720">or whether it was, you know, something I should be working on?</a> <a data-t="726">Just is it something I want to work on and can I read enough help docs and get enough help from my peers and whatnot and just figure out how to make it happen?</a> <a data-t="736">And that was extremely rewarding and that's really how I got to have this app where it is today.</a> <a data-t="741">And it's a surprisingly successful process, at least for me, is to just go intramentally like that.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="747">And the second tip that I have for people is it was very useful for me to debug via video chat with willing friends or collaborators.</a> <a data-t="753">In my case, it was people I met on the R for Data Science Slack channel.</a> <a data-t="759">So I reached out to a couple people who were working on their own Shiny apps and had them sort of at a stage of development.</a> <a data-t="764">And we set up some video chat calls where I helped the person debug their app and then they helped me debug my app.</a> <a data-t="770">And it was really refreshing and really helpful to be able to show people in real time what was going wrong and to be able to help them in real time with their problems.</a> <a data-t="781">And that was that helped me a lot and I got some great ideas by talking to other people.</a> <a data-t="788">So if you have the opportunity to set up a collaboration like that, I think that can be a great way to learn Shiny.</a></p>
