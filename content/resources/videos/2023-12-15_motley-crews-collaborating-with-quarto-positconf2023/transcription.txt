<h3>The fragmented workflow problem</h3>

<p><a data-t="76">As we began to work together and increase our collaboration, we quickly realized that we had a significant problem.</a> <a data-t="84">And our problem was that we had fragmented workflows.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="89">So every time that we attempted a collaborative analysis, we were dealing with an assemblage of data that was obtained using query tools like Hyperion and Toad Data Point and SQL Developer</a> <a data-t="141">that we then analyzed using Excel, or R as the situation called for.</a> <a data-t="147">We plotted it.</a> <a data-t="149">We merged it with narrative analyses that were written and drawn from flat files that were stored in Obsidian or other PKMs,</a> <a data-t="160">and then that we tailored together in Word, published as a PDF because, my goodness, do people like their PDFs?</a> <a data-t="167">And then we distributed to our audiences via email.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="172">This workflow, we quickly realized, was unsustainable.</a> <a data-t="177">We needed to find a better way for us to work together.</a> <a data-t="180">Keep in mind also, this is happening after our university has gone remote during COVID, and so we're each working remotely.</a> <a data-t="185">We don't have an opportunity to meet together to do these things.</a> <a data-t="189">We have to unite our work in some way.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="192">So what we're going to talk to you is how we abandoned this and what we learned as we came to a new way of working together.</a> <a data-t="200">I'm going to share with you why the form of the work matters.</a> <a data-t="205">Susan's going to speak about how our new way of working together reduced barriers for the non-technical collaborators that we have.</a> <a data-t="214">And Mike's going to share with you how this new way has enhanced the accuracy of the work that we do.</a></p>

<h3>Why form matters</h3>

<p><a data-t="220">Design hurdles at the end of a long analytic project are mentally exhausting, and they can be very frustrating.</a> <a data-t="229">As I shared, we were assembling draft and final analytic products by merging analyses that were drawn from different areas into a Word document.</a> <a data-t="241">And we quickly realized that, like the James R. Thompson Center, which you see here pictured,</a> <a data-t="237">this became a maintenance nightmare for us.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="250">Every single time that we were asked to add a plot, when we realized that we needed to edit a caption, or when we thought, you know what, this section of this analysis really belongs somewhere else because it amplifies a message that we need our audiences to get.</a> <a data-t="282">It was a maintenance nightmare.</a> <a data-t="283">We revisited it every single time that we had one of those.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="287">You may be familiar with the adage, form follows function.</a> <a data-t="292">This comes from a principle of design that was advanced by Louis Sullivan, who's a prominent Chicago school architect.</a> <a data-t="300">Sullivan went so far as to say that form following function was a law of human design.</a> <a data-t="308">You can see his ethos in many buildings that he designed around Chicago.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="314">This is the auditorium building, which you can visit in a short walk down Michigan Avenue.</a> <a data-t="318">This photo was taken in 1890, about a year after the building was completed.</a> <a data-t="323">And with respect to Sullivan, who was certainly at the forefront of his field when the 19th century turned to the 20th century, we realized that for folks who do the kind of work that we and many of you do, this simply doesn't go far enough.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="277">So what we'd suggest instead is that the form of the work is its function.</a> <a data-t="304">Like the John Hancock Center, the structure of the analysis that we create in our new way of working together is visible in the final analytic product.</a> <a data-t="313">It's visible to us on the back end when we're looking at the source, when we can see how we structured the query, when we can see the R code that we use to analyze the data that we obtain from our query,</a> <a data-t="327">when we can see the comments that'll explain to us seven months down the line when we've forgotten why we queried things the way that we did, what we were trying to do, and the other things that we tried.</a> <a data-t="335">None of that's visible to our audiences, but it's there.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="304">Like the John Hancock Center, the structure of the analysis that we create in our new way of working together is visible in the final analytic product.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="343">And because we can more easily accommodate other requests that our audiences have, that structure is something that we can fall back on to do our work better.</a> <a data-t="351">So the form of our final analysis facilitates its function now.</a> <a data-t="355">And the function of producing our analysis fashions its final form.</a></p>

<h3>Embracing Conway's Law and a unified workflow</h3>

<p><a data-t="358">How did we get there?</a> <a data-t="361">We realized we had to embrace Conway's Law.</a> <a data-t="365">Melvin Conway is a computer scientist, and he suggested that any organization that attempts to design a product or a system is going to inevitably in some way replicate that organization's structure within that product.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="399">The example is, if you have a four-person group working on something and you need to build a bypass, you're going to have a four-component bypass.</a> <a data-t="408">That's the basic concept.</a> <a data-t="410">And what we realized is that we have disparate backgrounds that draw on different analytic styles, and we needed to find a way to bring those together.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="418">We needed to unify the production environment for our analyses, and we needed to unify our workflow.</a> <a data-t="427">And so that's what we did.</a> <a data-t="429">We adopted a unified workflow that allows us to leverage </a><a href="/software/rstudio/">RStudio</a><a data-t="429"> and Quarto as the single place where we now query, process, analyze, and visualize data for our audiences,</a> <a data-t="440">and where we write our narrative analyses and our executive summaries that we can share with those audiences to help them make sense of the data that we've given them.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="448">Because we're working oftentimes remotely and in different places from each other, and we're working on different components of the analysis, it's really important that we have version control.</a> <a data-t="457">You may use GitHub for version control, we use GitLab for our version control,</a> <a data-t="462">so that when I'm working on doing some copy editing in a nearly final draft, and Mike and Susan are tweaking plots or doing some other things, maybe accommodating a last-minute request from a stakeholder,</a> <a data-t="472">we're not getting in each other's way, and we're not having to recompile a PDF document to incorporate those changes.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="482">And once we've reached the point where our document is finished and we feel like the analysis is ready to share with our audiences, we can publish it online using the pages feature of GitLab,</a> <a data-t="492">and our audiences can view the document there, and if we have other things that need to be updated, it can be updated.</a></p>
