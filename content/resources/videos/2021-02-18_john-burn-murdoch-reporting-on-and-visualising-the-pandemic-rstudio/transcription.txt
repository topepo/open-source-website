<h3>Understanding how people consume charts</h3>

<p><a data-t="118">So to start with something that I suspect reads as incredibly obvious, but I think is actually relatively little discussed in the database world.</a> <a data-t="129">I'm going to talk about the fact that to create really effective, really powerful charts, you really need to understand how people consume charts.</a> <a data-t="140">Now to sort of talk a little bit about why that's not as obvious as you might think, if you look online at the numbers of tutorials there are for making graphics, making data bits, whether it's in R, Python, D3, you name it, generally they're focused on the technical element of constructing a chart.</a> <a data-t="159">What chart type to choose, how to get your geometries, your shape, space and colour exactly right, how to do things more efficiently, optimising interactivity, that kind of thing.</a> <a data-t="171">There's relatively little on thinking about what more qualitative elements you can optimise to ensure that people understand, enjoy and remember your charts.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="190">This is where I perhaps get almost a bit too corporate or business focused in my view of this, but when you're making charts for a mass audience, it serves you well I think to think about them almost as if you're creating an advert.</a> <a data-t="220">There is decades of research from people who create billboard ads for example on what you should do in terms of messaging, in terms of how you use text, in terms of how you use colour and all of that to elicit a specific response in your audience.</a> <a data-t="239">I think it's that communication way of thinking that is relatively underrepresented at the moment in best practice guidelines for charts.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="251">So as I say, when I've been creating charts that I'm hoping and latterly expecting are going to be seen by an audience of millions, I've really had to focus on first of all how do I ensure they're clear enough to reach and be easily understood by millions of people and then how do I make sure that they stick in their memory and they're not just post 79 out of 100 that ping on their Twitter feed every day.</a></p>

<h3>Smart vs. clear: the log scale debate</h3>

<p><a data-t="842">The next lesson, and this is for me one of the most fundamental lessons anyone can learn in the process of building dataviz, is that there's a big difference between making the smartest version of a chart you possibly can and the clearest version of a chart you possibly can.</a> <a data-t="853">As with everything, there are obviously exceptions where you can do both of these things, but during the pandemic, I think there have been several examples of how these really do, these really are in tension with one another a lot of the time.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="874">So sometimes, and this was more the case, I think, in the earlier part of my career, but it's still a recurring theme, and I think this is something that a lot of people here today will recognize as well.</a> <a data-t="880">Sometimes we can get into the habit of thinking that effective data visualization is about optimizing for precision and objectivity.</a> <a data-t="886">There's a sort of list of technical tasks, and if you nail every one of them, you'll make the perfect chart.</a> <a data-t="893">You're using maths and geometry to arrive at the best solution.</a> <a data-t="898">It's a very sort of technical and structured process.</a> <a data-t="906">But then I actually publish a chart, and it suddenly becomes very clear that that is simply not the case.</a> <a data-t="912">That is a convenient but very rarely true description of what we should be aiming for when we make a data visualization.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="937">So the first difference, and I'm just going to flick back between these two charts.</a> <a data-t="941">This is the one that I've shown you already.</a> <a data-t="941">This was the data plotted on a logarithmic y-axis.</a> <a data-t="946">The point of this was to say, well, we know that the pandemic, especially in the early stage, spreads exponentially.</a> <a data-t="961">So the shape of the curves on this chart, if we plotted it on a linear axis, on a linear scale, they would all be arcing upwards from gentle slopes to steep slopes.</a> <a data-t="971">And to me, you're using a lot of visual bandwidth there just to show something that we assume is the default, that all of these curves are going to be getting steeper and steeper in these early weeks.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="980">So by going for a log scale, you sort of free up more bandwidth, as it were, in the reader's head, and you allow them to focus on what matters, which is you can now do the key thing that readers at this stage wanted to do, which is compare the trajectories of different countries on a straight line.</a> <a data-t="1014">You can look at this and say, OK, the virus seems to be spreading more quickly in Spain than in France, and France, in turn, more quickly than the UK.</a> <a data-t="1020">You can also, because it's a straight line, you can forecast out ahead and work out where the rate of cases in your country is going to be a few days or a week further down the line.</a> <a data-t="1031">You can answer that critical question that we set out to answer here, if we refer back to my colleague's email, which is how are countries doing compared to Italy?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1051">So my thinking here was all framed around this idea that when someone's looking at this chart, there's a whole sort of framework they're operating in that determines what their takeaway message is, is about are these two countries or three countries on the same course, or how many days until the country I'm interested in is at a certain level of cases that I know sort of means something.</a> <a data-t="1058">And therefore, that they're not thinking, well, how many pixels represent 100 cases?</a> <a data-t="1058">They're not worried about what's going on on the y-axis.</a> <a data-t="1064">They're looking for more of a sort of overarching message.</a></p>

<h3>Dataviz as dialogue: soliciting feedback</h3>

<h3>The risk of being too clever: splines vs. rolling averages</h3>

<p><a data-t="2002">The next point, big learning curve for me, and again that audience feedback was absolutely critical to this, was the risk of trying to be too clever, trying to be too technical with solutions to database problems and essentially losing people along the way and losing trust along the way.</a> <a data-t="2018">So the term blinding with science, I'm sure most of you are familiar with, but for those who aren't, it's the idea that you're telling someone about something in an overly complex or technical way so that regardless of the result of what you've done, they end up going away scratching their head.</a> <a data-t="2033">They're not quite sure because of the terminology, because of the methodology, they're not quite sure what you've done and I think there's often a risk with that in database.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2051">So the question that we had, this was still back in the spring, was could we improve the way we drew the lines on our charts so that instead of using a simple seven-day rolling average as we had been doing, we got slightly smoother lines but we also got lines where the final data point on the chart was a more faithful reflection of the final data point in real life.</a> <a data-t="2070">Because of course the problem when you're doing a seven-day average, whether you align that seven-day average to the right, to the middle, to the left, is it's never going to be an especially close representation of the last data point you have because it's being influenced by the other six.</a> <a data-t="2086">And so the issue we were having is the numbers in our headlines, in our news stories at the FT, would always be referring to that last data point, the thing that has just happened, but our charts were referring to the seven-day average which was, while cases were going up, always lower than that and so we would have this tension where the numbers on our charts didn't quite reflect the numbers of news.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2112">So what we decided to do was look at whether we could optimize the way we actually draw the lines on the chart so that they're still smooth, as you get with an average, but they also actually more faithfully reflect the very latest data point you have.</a> <a data-t="2122">So for those of you who have only ever seen seven-day average charts of COVID data, this is what the underlying data actually looks like.</a> <a data-t="2126">It's incredibly noisy, you get these huge peeps and troughs, new daily cases will drop to zero and then shoot up to a thousand the next day because the way that this data is actually collected and reported leads to enormous differences in the numbers you get from day to day.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2155">So here's the rolling average method we have been using, much clearer, much smoother, you can see exactly what's going on, you get clear trends over time.</a> <a data-t="2160">But the last data point on each of these lines didn't necessarily match up with the last data point that had been recorded.</a> <a data-t="2180">So what we considered was a smoothing spline, so it keeps that smoothness of the lines, it's still relatively easy to discern what's going on in one country and compare it to another, but the last data point is now, because of how the splines are calculated, much closer to the last data point that had been recorded.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2220">So we decided, let's switch over to the spline.</a> <a data-t="2223">And we did, again, a long explainer of why we'd done this, setting out that need to better represent the most recent data while keeping smoothness, big explanation out in the public for anyone to see.</a> <a data-t="2237">And I then, again, following my own advice, asked people what they think.</a> <a data-t="2242">And here's the kind of responses we got.</a> <a data-t="2242">And what you'll notice is there were sort of two quite clearly different camps here.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2247">On the left, top left, we've got a couple of people saying the spline's much better.</a> <a data-t="2252">Yeah, I'm very devoted to splining myself, for example.</a> <a data-t="2257">And the thing I should point out immediately about that is that these two people, Peter and Micah, I don't know if either of them are actually watching along today, are both data scientists to some degree.</a> <a data-t="2268">So I think anyone who's heard of splines felt very positive about that as an idea.</a> <a data-t="2275">But then we get some different feedback.</a> <a data-t="2275">In the upper right, we've got someone saying the rolling average is much easier for the average person to understand.</a> <a data-t="2285">And in the lower left, we've got someone with a data science background, but still saying that the rolling average probably makes more sense.</a> <a data-t="2291">And then in the lower left, we actually have one of the epidemiologists in the UK working on the development of the vaccines talking about how this is for the general public.</a> <a data-t="2302">The audience for this work is a mass audience.</a> <a data-t="2302">It's an audience with a very mixed visual and data literacy.</a> <a data-t="2307">And it's much easier for people to get their head around a seven-day average than a spline.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2320">So the takeaway that we had, again, from this was that you have to prioritize the ease of understanding.</a> <a data-t="2324">You may have come up with an incredibly technical, nifty solution that, on the basis of any sort of quantitative tests, works better.</a> <a data-t="2329">But if that is going to confuse people along the way, that is a problem.</a> <a data-t="2335">And again, for me, the fundamental goal of my data visualizations is communication with a mass audience.</a> <a data-t="2348">And I would rather have clear communication and as many people as possible understanding, remembering, and wanting to share these charts than to have something which is 5% more correct, shall we say, but that loses a lot of people along the way.</a></p>

<h3>Summary and call to action</h3>

<p><a data-t="2624">So all of that is to say that the last 12 months, I think, have been an incredible crash course for me and for our team at the FT in terms of really getting to the fundamentals of how people do read charts and, therefore, what we should be thinking about when we make charts, what we should be focusing on and prioritizing.</a> <a data-t="2647">And coming into this year, I think my assumption, and a lot of people here, I'm sure, the assumption would have been that people read charts a bit like this.</a> <a data-t="2651">They get out their protractor, their set square, their ruler, and their compass, and it's all about precision and people are really focusing on individual pixels and where different elements are in your plot when they're trying to take away their message.</a> <a data-t="2664">They're almost trying to pass the data set back out of your chart and so you, as the practitioner, should really be focusing on the most high fidelity, most clear encoding of that data as possible.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2682">But the reality, I think, is actually a bit more like this.</a> <a data-t="2682">It's a bit more like people going to a movie theater and sitting back and watching.</a> <a data-t="2694">They are coming to a chart to see something, to see some kind of story, to get a message, to take away something that is playing out in front of them.</a> <a data-t="2699">In some cases, literally with the animation, but in other cases, it's just a line chart.</a> <a data-t="2702">But it's a line chart where they're not focusing on tiny differences between data points on the chart.</a> <a data-t="2708">They're focusing on what is this top line message in the title, what's the takeaway here, and what are the other sweeping trends that this chart is conveying.</a> <a data-t="2716">They're not poring over it, trying to extract the values from every pixel on the page.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2724">So to summarize, the key lessons I think I've learned here, and I think these are lessons that I'm going to be using for the rest of my career.</a> <a data-t="2730">First of all, text is critical.</a> <a data-t="2730">I know we're all in the data viz industry.</a> <a data-t="2730">We make charts.</a> <a data-t="2736">But if we don't get the text right, none of the rest really matters.</a> <a data-t="2736">People will be lost.</a> <a data-t="2736">They'll be confused.</a> <a data-t="2736">It will be a disaster.</a> <a data-t="2741">The second point, we have to take responsibility.</a> <a data-t="2745">If someone is confused about our chart, if someone is confused about our chart, that's our fault.</a> <a data-t="2750">And the best thing we can do to combat that is to include explanations in situ around where that chart appears to make sure that there's as little risk as possible of confusion.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2761">On a similar note, we need to be considering what are people's beliefs, feelings, emotions, sentiments around this subject matter, and how could they color their reaction to this chart?</a> <a data-t="2767">How can we design this chart in as neutral a way as possible in that sense so that at least if anyone does have any issues with their chart, it's because they don't like the data.</a> <a data-t="2776">It's not because they feel put off or hurt somehow by the way we've actually conveyed the information.</a> <a data-t="2788">The next step, and I think, again, one of the most important, don't just publish and vanish.</a> <a data-t="2792">Stick around.</a> <a data-t="2792">Get involved in the conversation.</a> <a data-t="2792">You will learn so much about how people are understanding your chart that you can incorporate into the next one you make.</a> <a data-t="2803">And make sure that your chart above all else is easy to understand.</a> <a data-t="2811">It doesn't matter if you can fine tune it to make it 2%, 3%, 4% more faithful to the data.</a> <a data-t="2817">The key thing is that you focus on getting across that core message and getting it across accessibly.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2835">And finally, we should all be aware that we have this incredibly powerful tool in animation, and there are plenty of charts where we would unthinkingly just produce a static chart, but an animation can have that much more, I would say, in fact, orders of magnitude more impact.</a> <a data-t="2851">And I'm going to end with a call to action in terms of things that I would like us as the data viz community to do differently or do more or even do for the first time as we go away from this.</a> <a data-t="2863">Which is that first of all, as I mentioned at the very start, there's a huge amount of resources out there on how to achieve particular technical or visual effects in a chart, but there's not very much on the general way of framing your work and the language you use around it in order to achieve the clearest possible communication of a message.</a> <a data-t="2879">So what I'd love to see and what I hope to participate in myself is putting out more resources that can help people think of and refine this idea of data viz as communication more than data viz just as a technical exercise of creating the most objectively good chart possible.</a></p>
