<h3>Evaluating existing tools</h3>

<p><a data-t="191">So apologies for the very big table here, but you guys are all data scientists, we love</a> <a data-t="197">tables.</a> <a data-t="198">The current patchwork of analytic tools and approaches really fails to meet our needs</a> <a data-t="204">for scalable, self-serve reporting in a consulting context.</a> <a data-t="209">So in-product analytics often don't address the unique customer needs, and they often</a> <a data-t="215">lack the ability to tell a compelling story in the language of the client.</a> <a data-t="221">While on the other side of the spectrum, ad hoc customer reporting is time-consuming and</a> <a data-t="226">difficult to scale effectively to meet customer demand sustainably.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="230">Therefore, as companies, we often spend tons of time and money building self-serve analytic</a> <a data-t="237">systems using business intelligence tools, but we're still left with this huge burden</a> <a data-t="242">of effective enablement of less technical data consumers that often complain of information</a> <a data-t="248">overload, and understandably so.</a> <a data-t="252">So at the end of all of this, we still have often simple visualizations that lack visual</a> <a data-t="257">polish, which is not something we're particularly proud to share with our clients.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="264">One alternative code-based approach that I particularly love is parametrized reporting.</a> <a data-t="270">This provides a much more customizable and scalable end product with professional-grade</a> <a data-t="276">data visualizations.</a> <a data-t="277">However, it often lacks the ability to then customize in those final stages and really</a> <a data-t="284">tailor insights to the specific client goals and needs.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="289">So ultimately, none of these approaches quite fit the mold of what we're looking for.</a> <a data-t="294">Instead, we want to bridge the gap between software-as-a-service and standard analytics</a> <a data-t="300">consulting with a tool that is simultaneously scalable and easy to use, while still enabling</a> <a data-t="307">lots of downstream tweaking and customization.</a> <a data-t="311">More specifically, we were looking for something that lives kind of squarely between business</a> <a data-t="316">intelligence tools like Wooker and something like code-based parametrized reporting.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="324">So through lots of user interviews, I learned that, surprise, surprise, no one really loves</a> <a data-t="330">manually loading and taking screenshots from 10 different dashboards to build a single</a> <a data-t="335">presentation.</a> <a data-t="337">Instead, users wanted the convenience of self-serve tools, but also the visual polish and automation</a> <a data-t="344">capabilities that are really only possible with more code-based approaches.</a> <a data-t="349">So I said, por que no los dos?</a> <a data-t="352">Let's do it.</a></p>

<h3>Introducing BetterUp Insights</h3>

<p><a data-t="355">So introducing BetterUp Insights.</a> <a data-t="359">BetterUp Insights is a </a><a href="/software/shiny-r/">Shiny</a><a data-t="359"> app that I built to enable scalable, flexible, self-serve reporting</a> <a data-t="365">that allows members of our field team to select a reporting group of interest, whether that's</a> <a data-t="371">a specific partner or perhaps an entire industry, which is often useful for pre-sales motions,</a> <a data-t="379">and then generate a Google slide deck populated with various data insights, which can then</a> <a data-t="384">be further tweaked and modified downstream to help tell a really custom, tailored story</a> <a data-t="390">of organizational transformation in the language of the client.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="396">Just like any standard business intelligence tool, data is pulled directly from our database</a> <a data-t="401">at the time the user clicks load data, but we go one step further by not only generating</a> <a data-t="407">professional-grade visualizations, but then directly sending those visualizations and</a> <a data-t="412">metrics to pre-formatted Google slide decks with several different templates, including</a> <a data-t="417">things like inclusive leadership, coaching culture, hybrid work, and manager effectiveness.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="426">This workflow is not only much more efficient and enjoyable, but it also greatly cuts down</a> <a data-t="432">on opportunities for human error.</a> <a data-t="435">So things like making sure that the date the data was pulled or end counts displayed underneath</a> <a data-t="441">of a visualization in the caption, these may seem small, but they can make or break</a> <a data-t="447">deals in an economic environment where every single business purchase is highly scrutinized.</a></p>

<h3>App build architecture</h3>

<p><a data-t="456">Of course, this wouldn't be PositConf if I didn't get to geek out a little bit about</a> <a data-t="460">the build architecture of this app.</a> <a data-t="463">So here comes the more technical stuff, but feel free to ping me after if you want to</a> <a data-t="467">talk more.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="469">First and foremost, I am an early and enthusiastic adopter of the Rhino framework for scalable</a> <a data-t="475">and robust Shiny apps.</a> <a data-t="477">So having developed several Frankenstein monolithic Shiny apps in the past myself, I knew I wanted</a> <a data-t="483">to take a module-first approach.</a> <a data-t="486">And while I've dabbled in other module-first frameworks like Gollum, I really prefer to</a> <a data-t="492">keep my package development workflow separate from my app development workflow.</a> <a data-t="496">So for me, I found the Rhino framework really intuitive and a joy to work with.</a> <a data-t="501">So if you have a similar development mindset, I highly encourage you to check it out.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="507">Next I knew that I needed to carefully manage reactivity within the app.</a> <a data-t="510">So beyond just making some kind of standard design choices, like making sure that we only</a> <a data-t="516">run queries when a user clicks an action button and not just reactively when anything changes,</a> <a data-t="523">I also adopted the </a><a href="/software/r6/">R6</a><a data-t="523"> plus gargoyle triggers approach, which has been advocated by Juwan</a> <a data-t="528">Hill, which I link below.</a> <a data-t="532">I found this especially helpful because I can then embed small helper functions within</a> <a data-t="537">the R6 object itself that allow me to do things like apply minimum data thresholds for data</a> <a data-t="543">privacy reasons in a very easy to maintain way.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="549">And then finally, I was able to integrate much of my better app colleague Andrew Reese's</a> <a data-t="554">previous Python workflows for working with the Google API into my Shiny app using the</a> <a href="/software/reticulate/">reticulate</a><a data-t="559"> package, which I am fully convinced is pure magic.</a></p>

<h3>Making the case for Posit at your organization</h3>

<p><a data-t="664">So ultimately, BetterUp Insights was somewhat of a Trojan horse for getting our organization</a> <a data-t="671">to adopt Posit products.</a> <a data-t="672">And as I was telling some colleagues last night, I came into this role with admittedly</a> <a data-t="677">a bit of an agenda.</a> <a data-t="684">This was not my first time trying to advocate for Posit products to be procured at my company.</a> <a data-t="690">But it was, in fact, my first time being successful in that endeavor.</a> <a data-t="694">So I'd like to spend the remainder of my talk sharing some insights I gleaned through this</a> <a data-t="699">process so that you might be able to take a similar approach for building the business</a> <a data-t="703">case at Posit at your organization.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="708">So perhaps the most important insight that I gained was that you need to identify a core</a> <a data-t="714">capability that these products will unlock for your organization.</a> <a data-t="717">So what capability can you uniquely provide that's not already present in existing tools?</a> <a data-t="727">So figure that out, do it, and do it well.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="731">So one pitfall that many of us, including myself, fall into is being overly enthusiastic</a> <a data-t="737">about the full range of possibilities that you would be able to unlock if only you had</a> <a data-t="742">Posit.</a> <a data-t="743">So dashboards, multiple IDEs, easy API deployment, scheduled reports, just the possibilities</a> <a data-t="750">are endless.</a> <a data-t="752">Bring that enthusiasm here to PositConf, but save it for the boardroom.</a> <a data-t="758">Initially, you know, instead focus on one unique capability.</a> <a data-t="763">And that has to have a clear impact on your organization's bottom line.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="770">Equally, if not more important, build your team.</a> <a data-t="775">As open source enthusiasts, we often know how to harness the power of code-based tools,</a> <a data-t="780">but then struggle to communicate and gain buy-in from the right people.</a> <a data-t="784">So this is why I believe that cultivating a diversity of relationships is the key to</a> <a data-t="789">procurement success.</a> <a data-t="791">So be Kermit the Frog, have a creative vision and no ego, recognize the unique talents of</a> <a data-t="797">those around you, and attract your organization's weirdos.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="802">But in all seriousness, it's important not to over-anchor on any one stakeholder perspective.</a> <a data-t="809">So for example, your business person is going to know how to best navigate your organizational</a> <a data-t="814">hierarchy, gain buy-in, obtain resources, whereas your more engineering-minded folks</a> <a data-t="822">are going to have a better understanding of where Posit and your specific data products</a> <a data-t="826">are going to fit into the overall software development lifecycle.</a> <a data-t="831">Not to mention, those people are probably the people you're going to need to tap when</a> <a data-t="834">you actually need to get Posit up and running on your company's data infrastructure.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="841">You'll also want to develop strong relationships with your customers themselves, so the people</a> <a data-t="846">that you are building this product with and for.</a> <a data-t="850">So I highly recommend doing extensive user interviews with these people, which is a great</a> <a data-t="855">way not only to just clarify the problem you're trying to solve, but also simultaneously build</a> <a data-t="862">relationships and trust with your most crucial stakeholders.</a> <a data-t="866">And if you're lucky, you'll find someone to fill every one of these roles listed here,</a> <a data-t="871">but let's be honest, it might just be you or one or two additional people that are willing</a> <a data-t="877">to go to bat with you.</a> <a data-t="879">In this case, it's still helpful to be aware of these roles so that you can know which</a> <a data-t="882">hat to wear at any given time.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="897">This next point is related to that core capability point at the start.</a> <a data-t="901">So when making the case for Posit at your organization, it helps to think not in terms</a> <a data-t="907">of what question can I answer, but what service can I provide?</a> <a data-t="910">So i.e. create a data product.</a> <a data-t="913">So while dashboards answer questions, data products perform valuable services, and that's</a> <a data-t="919">ultimately what's going to get you through all of your procurement effort hurdles, which</a> <a data-t="923">you will inevitably face.</a></p>
