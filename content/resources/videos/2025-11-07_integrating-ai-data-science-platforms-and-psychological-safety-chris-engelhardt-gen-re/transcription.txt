<h3>The Nokia cautionary tale</h3>

<p><a data-t="33">Before diving into this topic, though, I want to take you back to 2005,</a> <a data-t="38">when Nokia was at the peak of innovation, as evidenced here by the N95 model.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="46">And as reported in a case study, Nokia soon had some challenges</a> <a data-t="49">because there was a new competitor in the landscape.</a> <a data-t="52">Apple had just introduced in 2007 their first iPhone OS</a> <a data-t="58">with a sleek touchscreen, which Nokia did not have.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="63">And obviously, there was some amount of concern going on at this point,</a> <a data-t="66">especially among senior leadership.</a> <a data-t="69">They were putting a lot of pressure on lower-level managers to deliver in a timely fashion.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="75">And what senior leadership was not interested in</a> <a data-t="78">was hearing negative news about meeting deadlines,</a> <a data-t="81">or the current status of products.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="85">And ultimately, this stifled innovation and led to Nokia's downfall,</a> <a data-t="90">at least over the 2005 to 2010 period.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="95">What I want each of you in the audience to do at this point</a> <a data-t="98">is to put yourself in the shoes of a Nokia manager.</a> <a data-t="103">And imagine if you worked in a team where you were afraid to speak up.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="112">Imagine if you were on a team where no matter what you thought,</a> <a data-t="116">no matter what you did,</a> <a data-t="120">would be met with belittlement or beratement.</a> <a data-t="125">Imagine if your voice didn't matter.</a> <a data-t="128">Imagine if your lived experiences didn't matter, your mistakes and all.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="134">What would the outcome of that look like?</a> <a data-t="137">Well, I think it can lead to some pretty negative consequences</a> <a data-t="139">as a direct result of not being able to speak up.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="143">One, of course, and this was evidenced in the Nokia case study,</a> <a data-t="147">is that there's information asymmetry.</a> <a data-t="149">So managers who were on a lower level</a> <a data-t="152">had access to different information, i.e. negative news,</a> <a data-t="155">that senior leadership did not have access to.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="159">I think also it led to a certain amount of learned helplessness</a> <a data-t="162">in the Nokia organization,</a> <a data-t="164">where the idea here is that no matter what you do,</a> <a data-t="167">it doesn't matter, you feel powerless to affect some change</a> <a data-t="171">and change the current state of your situation.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="175">Taken together with information asymmetry</a> <a data-t="178">combined with learned helplessness,</a> <a data-t="180">I think it ultimately leads to poor team performance.</a> <a data-t="183">And we saw this in the Nokia example as well.</a> <a data-t="186">And it has some pretty dramatic consequences as a result as well,</a> <a data-t="189">both on individuals' mental health,</a> <a data-t="192">but also on organizational consequences as well.</a> <a data-t="194">And this is a challenge.</a> <a data-t="197">And we need a way to address this challenge as a leader.</a></p>

<h3>Psychological safety as a solution</h3>

<p><a data-t="201">One strategy that I would submit to you all today</a> <a data-t="204">is to think about addressing this challenge</a> <a data-t="207">through the lens of psychological safety.</a> <a data-t="211">Where the idea here is that the teammates believe,</a> <a data-t="214">so this is a team-level construct,</a> <a data-t="217">it's where the teammates believe that the team is safe</a> <a data-t="220">for interpersonal risk-taking.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="222">By interpersonal risk-taking,</a> <a data-t="225">I mean that the team feels free to speak up,</a> <a data-t="228">to offer their unique ideas and contributions,</a> <a data-t="231">to admit their mistakes and faults,</a> <a data-t="234">and ask for help.</a> <a data-t="237">And these are challenging things to do,</a> <a data-t="240">because each of their own in isolation can lead to</a> <a data-t="243">perhaps other people perceiving you as incompetent</a> <a data-t="246">or not an effective teammate.</a> <a data-t="249">So there's an inherent risk in each of these behaviors.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="253">What I want to do is talk about our journey</a> <a data-t="256">on our team and psychological safety.</a> <a data-t="259">And the start of our journey began about a year ago,</a> <a data-t="262">where I presented to our team on this concept,</a> <a data-t="265">as well as the empirical evidence supporting it.</a> <a data-t="268">So I drew on Amy Edmondson's work back in the 90s.</a> <a data-t="271">And in her work, the earlier study,</a> <a data-t="274">she focused on furniture manufacturing companies,</a> <a data-t="277">not among technical teams.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="279">And so I thought if I wanted a great chance</a> <a data-t="282">of getting the architects and engineers</a> <a data-t="285">and data scientists on board with my messaging,</a> <a data-t="288">I also had to present something on evidence</a> <a data-t="291">among technical teams.</a> <a data-t="294">And who better to turn to for that than Google?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="297">As some of you may know,</a> <a data-t="300">Google spends millions of dollars</a> <a data-t="303">on investigating teamwork,</a> <a data-t="306">what makes some teams underperform versus overperform.</a> <a data-t="309">And they actually ran a study back in 2012 to 2014</a> <a data-t="312">examining this exact phenomenon.</a> <a data-t="315">So they included nearly 200 teams,</a> <a data-t="318">many of which were technical in nature,</a> <a data-t="321">and they asked them a variety of questions.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="324">A lot focused on individual differences.</a> <a data-t="327">Does it matter how introverted versus extroverted you are?</a> <a data-t="330">Does it matter if you socialize on the weekend</a> <a data-t="333">with your teammates?</a> <a data-t="336">Does it matter your academic pedigree?</a> <a data-t="339">Does it not matter?</a> <a data-t="342">What matters is how people work together as a team.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="345">I want to draw your attention</a> <a data-t="348">to the bottom of this pyramid here,</a> <a data-t="351">where of course they identified a few factors that matter,</a> <a data-t="354">but chief among them in importance</a> <a data-t="357">is psychological safety,</a> <a data-t="360">where again the idea is that teammates feel safe</a> <a data-t="363">to take interpersonal risks</a> <a data-t="366">and be vulnerable in front of one another.</a></p>

<h3>Our team's journey</h3>

<p><a data-t="369">I wanted to model the types of behaviors</a> <a data-t="372">that I want our team to espouse as well.</a> <a data-t="375">And I immediately asked for help.</a> <a data-t="378">And the help that I asked for is</a> <a data-t="381">how do we sustain psychological safety on our team?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="384">One unique idea that was brought forward during this time,</a> <a data-t="387">and this is admittedly two words</a> <a data-t="390">you don't see together very often.</a> <a data-t="393">We hosted a team anxiety party.</a> <a data-t="396">Where we started off in this meeting</a> <a data-t="399">with the fears that we all have,</a> <a data-t="402">just to get the ball rolling.</a></p>
