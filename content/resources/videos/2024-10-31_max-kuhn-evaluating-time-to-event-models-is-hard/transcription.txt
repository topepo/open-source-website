<h3>Inverse probability of censoring weights</h3>

<p><a data-t="398">Now, one thing we know from like statistics is, you know, generally speaking, we just don't throw away data and think like everything's cool.</a> <a data-t="404">So, you know, we have to think about what the consequences are in terms of measuring performance.</a> <a data-t="409">When we have data that is just eliminated, you know, in this case, for good reason, because we don't know what to do with it.</a> <a data-t="415">And typically we know that just doing the analysis of those data as if nothing ever happened generally leads to bias.</a> <a data-t="422">And that's a, that's, we've known that for like a long time.</a> <a data-t="425">And so there are a variety of ways you can handle that.</a> <a data-t="427">But the one that people tend to do is they tend to take a causal inference approach and then build what they call a propensity model</a> <a data-t="434">to predict, you know, the probability of that data point being missing and then use that additional score for each data point to weigh their metrics and things like that.</a> <a data-t="443">So this is a pretty well-known thing.</a> <a data-t="444">People do it quite a bit.</a> <a data-t="445">You've probably seen it if you were in the causal inference workshop.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="449">Now in our context, we're going to do basically, we're going to do that.</a> <a data-t="453">We're going to estimate the probability of something being censored at a specific time tau.</a> <a data-t="458">And there's detail at the end if we have time or if we need it.</a> <a data-t="461">But the way that we're going to take a very simple method, it's not informative censoring assumptions.</a> <a data-t="466">We don't think that there are any predictors influencing that.</a> <a data-t="468">We can change that later on in tidymodels.</a> <a data-t="470">But right now we do something that honestly I had never heard of, but apparently it's fairly common called a reverse Kaplan-Meier curve.</a> <a data-t="477">So it's a Kaplan-Meier curve.</a> <a data-t="478">We know what that is, but they just switch the event indicators and it's giving us basically the probability that something is censored up to time tau.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="487">And so then what we do is we do these things called inverse probability of censoring weights.</a> <a data-t="492">We get that probability for every data point, whether it's an event or non-event.</a> <a data-t="496">We take its reciprocal and we use that as a case weight when we compute our metrics.</a> <a data-t="500">And we'll see that in just a minute.</a> <a data-t="502">The best paper on this is Graf et al here.</a> <a data-t="505">I've read this like five times to just get my mind around it.</a> <a data-t="509">And that's probably the best brief reference we have.</a> <a data-t="513">It's mostly concerned with Breyer scores and that's what we'll talk about later.</a></p>

<h3>Breyer score</h3>

<p><a data-t="630">In our situation, so we have events and non-events, we're looking at a two class problem.</a> <a data-t="636">And what we do here is we do the same calculation, like a Y equal one here means this event, the probability here would be one minus the probability of surviving.</a> <a data-t="645">So that's what happens when you have an event.</a> <a data-t="648">And if it's not an event, it's a zero here.</a> <a data-t="650">And then it's just a survival probability.</a> <a data-t="652">And then these are the case weights I mentioned.</a> <a data-t="654">It's the same calculation, but instead of dividing by n, we divide by the sum of the weights at time tau.</a> <a data-t="659">And that sum of weights is going to, as you just saw in the last figure, is going to vary over time quiz.</a></p>
