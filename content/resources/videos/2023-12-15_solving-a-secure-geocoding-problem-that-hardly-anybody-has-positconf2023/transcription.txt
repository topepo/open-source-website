<h3>Why build a secure, code-free geocoder</h3>

<p><a data-t="46">So first of all, why build a secure, code-free geocoder?</a> <a data-t="52">Well, as my puppy is proudly telling you here, I work at Fox Chase Cancer Center, and I'm a geospatial data analyst there.</a> <a data-t="57">So I do things like this.</a> <a data-t="63">I characterize cancer burden by neighborhood.</a> <a data-t="68">Then I do the same thing with cancer risk.</a> <a data-t="73">So in this case, we're looking at smoking behavior and the density of tobacco retailers.</a> <a data-t="78">And then I use that to inform interventions.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="84">Basically, I'm arguing that targeted cancer prevention efforts depend on geocoded patient data.</a> <a data-t="91">So geocoding is the process of turning a text address into a geographically referenced coordinate system like latitude and longitude.</a> <a data-t="104">And there are many ways to do this.</a> <a data-t="107">Most people can use one of these tools.</a> <a data-t="111">But the thing about doing this with patient data is that you have to be really careful because an address is an individual identifier.</a> <a data-t="121">So we can't send that over a server.</a> <a data-t="124">At least the most conservative health researchers are not going to send that over a server for data security concerns.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="130">There are a few options that are secure for geocoding, but they tend to be clunky, expensive, and or require coding.</a> <a data-t="144">So this is a data science conference. Why is coding an issue here?</a> <a data-t="150">Well, while I am the only geospatial analyst at Fox Chase Cancer Center, I'm not the only one interested in using geocoded data.</a> <a data-t="160">Normally, the next step after geocoding is that we link the geocodes with some area-level measures of social determinants of health, which can be defined as the environments in which we live, work, and play.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="158">So I'm going to give you a few examples of other folks at Fox Chase who are interested in using this type of data.</a> <a data-t="164">We have clinical researchers who want to know whether socioeconomic status has any association with treatment outcomes in their patients.</a> <a data-t="175">We have a multidisciplinary team right now who's working on increasing diversity in our clinical trials and want help with focusing their recruitment and education programs and which community partners we should engage in this effort.</a> <a data-t="201">We have leaders of cancer prevention and control interventions like our mobile mammography unit and our patient navigation programs who want to make sure we're prioritizing and serving residents in the highest risk areas.</a> <a data-t="223">And finally, our leaders and administrators of the Cancer Center are required to report back to the National Cancer Institute how well we're serving the residents in our designated catchment area.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="245">So currently what happens is that these types of inquiries go to my fantastic boss, and then they're passed on to me like data requests.</a> <a data-t="262">And on one hand, it's really awesome that I get to support this really impactful work at Fox Chase.</a> <a data-t="270">And I also got to thinking that if I could give researchers and administrators at Fox Chase a tool to be a bit more independent in geocoding and potentially linking that patient data with common measures from area-level measures, that I would be freed up to do some of the more advanced spatial analyses that I'm interested in.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="275">So I wanted to create a code-free, secure geocoding application for researchers and administrators working with patient data.</a> <a data-t="280">And I actually workshopped this idea quite a bit with my fantastic boss, Dr. Shannon Lynch, and her collaborator, Dr. Kevin Henry.</a> <a data-t="287">And actually, because of those two individuals, I'm going back to school now for my Ph.D. in Geography.</a></p>
