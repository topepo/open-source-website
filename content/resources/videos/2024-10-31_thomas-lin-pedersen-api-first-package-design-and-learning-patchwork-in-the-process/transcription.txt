<h3>What API-first package design means</h3>

<p><a data-t="182">So, what do I mean with API-first package design?</a> <a data-t="182">APIs are everywhere.</a> <a data-t="182">This is how you interact with software as a user and also as a package developer.</a> <a data-t="189">API-first, in my mind, means that there is a coherent idea behind the API in the package, and that coherent idea drives all the decisions during development and doing maintenance of the patch.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="201">The reason why I'm deciding to talk to you about this instead of just making a regular patchwork talk is that it's very prevalent in how I think about patchwork, and even more so, I think it's a subject that is extremely important when it comes to designing visualization software.</a> <a data-t="221">The reason why I think that is that visualization and plotting is a bit of a specific or a special subject within programming, because it deals with what I would call an unbounded feature complexity, because plotting and data visualization are inherently creative.</a> <a data-t="244">It's a way of taking all of your artistic ideas and putting them down on paper or on the screen, and this means that more or less everything that you can dream up is fair game, right?</a> <a data-t="260">You might not be able to do it through a plotting library, but there is no upper bound on what you might want to do when it comes to data visualization, and this kind of unbounded feature complexity is something that is best tamed through a good sense of API design.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="260">There is no upper bound on what you might want to do when it comes to data visualization, and this kind of unbounded feature complexity is something that is best tamed through a good sense of API design.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="279">And I think that we have seen with ggplot2, for instance, that this can be a fantastic way to gain success.</a> <a data-t="287">So the rest of the talk, rather than just talking about patchwork and how it works, I will focus on three small nuggets of wisdom, perhaps, that I have picked up during work on both ggplot2, but certainly also patchwork, and all of these have definitely applied to when I was thinking about patchwork and developing patchwork and maintaining patchwork.</a></p>

<h3>Having a mission</h3>

<p><a data-t="313">So the first thing I'm going to talk about is having a mission.</a> <a data-t="319">Now, having a mission, I think everyone that sits down and develop a package is having a mission, but when I say having a mission, it means having a mission for how things work, how the user interfaces with the package, rather than just saying, I need to solve this and to hell with the rest.</a> <a data-t="352">The reason why it's so important to have a mission is that coherence just doesn't come organically.</a> <a data-t="358">You can't expect yourself to sit down, begin to code, and out of it just grows this perfectly little nugget of code that is internally beautifully composed.</a> <a data-t="372">No, you need to think about this, and you better think about it up front, rather than think about it as you're coding and having to go back and kind of get everything to work together.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="385">And I think in our team, in the </a><a href="/software/tidyverse/">Tidyverse</a><a data-t="385"> team, I think we have been pretty good at that.</a> <a data-t="390">Like, we have certainly made decisions that we have backtracked on, we have certainly broken things, because we have learned from our mistakes, but in general, I think we are pretty good at having a mission and having an idea about how we want the user to work with the code and integrate with our code.</a> <a data-t="417">And I really think this is not just superficial.</a> <a data-t="417">I think, and I think history kind of agrees with me, that the best user experience comes from this.</a> <a data-t="423">It comes from having a theoretical backbone behind what you're doing.</a> <a data-t="430">You can see this in ggplot2, which is based on the grammar of graphics, which is a whole book that defines the theory for how to think about data visualization.</a> <a data-t="441">You can certainly also see it in </a><a href="/software/dplyr/">dplyr</a><a data-t="441">, even though there's no fancy book that supports it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="449">But everything that these packages does is based on a very well-defined theoretical backbone.</a> <a data-t="456">And what it does is that it allows the user to not have to learn everything there is to learn.</a> <a data-t="462">What they just need to do is understand this theoretical backbone, and they can usually do that to extrapolate what they know into things that they want to do, without sitting down and learning everything from scratch.</a> <a data-t="475">And the thing with having a mission, why it's so good, is also that missions are contagious.</a> <a data-t="483">I don't have count of how many people have tried to take what ggplot2 does and try to apply it to some other programming language.</a> <a data-t="487">And the same with dplyr.</a> <a data-t="487">And I'm not saying this as to point fingers and say, oh, you're stealing from us. This is our idea. Not at all.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="495">Actually, this just goes to show that once you have a great idea, suddenly it becomes impossible to think about solving this problem without thinking about this mission that someone else laid out.</a> <a data-t="507">So missions are contagious.</a> <a data-t="507">And we see this with ggplot2.</a> <a data-t="507">We also see this with patchwork, actually.</a> <a data-t="513">But because not, I think, a year or two after patchwork came out, suddenly matplotlib allowed you to use a plus operator.</a> <a data-t="518">And this is not, again, this is not to point fingers.</a> <a data-t="518">It's just that a good idea is contagious.</a> <a data-t="523">And you actually want this because suddenly you are in the center of defining how to work with this problem space that you're deeply interested in.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="527">In patchwork, in the readme of patchwork, this is the first paragraph.</a> <a data-t="530">And this is the paragraph I wrote almost as the first thing when I began developing it.</a> <a data-t="530">The goal of patchwork is to make it ridiculously simple to combine separate ggplots into the same graphics.</a> <a data-t="538">As such, it tries to solve the same problem as grid extra, the grid arrange function, and cowplot plot grid.</a> <a data-t="545">But using an API that incites exploration and iteration and scales to arbitrary complex layout.</a> <a data-t="553">So I wrote this, and I have basically kept to that promise ever since during the last six years of development and maintenance.</a> <a data-t="559">And there's a surprising amount of this mission statement that's about the how, the API.</a> <a data-t="566">Not what it does, but how it does it.</a></p>

<h3>Embracing the no</h3>

<p><a data-t="735">And the best way, one of the best ways, at least one of the easiest ways to spend this energy is just by saying no.</a> <a data-t="741">Now, this seems like a simple idea.</a> <a data-t="741">Saying no is not that hard, right?</a> <a data-t="741">It is surprisingly hard.</a> <a data-t="757">The reason why it's so hard is that once you've created something of, I would say, beauty, hopefully you feel like you're creating stuff of beauty, you put it out in the world, people will begin to use it.</a> <a data-t="767">I mean, that's the whole idea of it.</a> <a data-t="767">And you will have many users, you will have great users, clever users, smart people, and they will have ideas.</a> <a data-t="773">And the thing with it, most of these ideas are great.</a> <a data-t="773">And it's super hard to say no to great ideas.</a> <a data-t="781">But still, your users have their own problems to solve.</a> <a data-t="781">And their ideas might be great.</a> <a data-t="788">But their idea and their main point of focus is not the purity of your API.</a> <a data-t="794">So it's up to you to say, no, I can't do that.</a> <a data-t="794">This is a great idea.
