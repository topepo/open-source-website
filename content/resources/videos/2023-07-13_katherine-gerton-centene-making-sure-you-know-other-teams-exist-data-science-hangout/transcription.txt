<h3>Risk adjustment explained</h3>

<p><a data-t="369">Yeah, so when the Affordable Care Act was put into place in about 2015, one of the things that was mandated there is insurance companies can no longer deny people coverage for pre-existing conditions.</a> <a data-t="400">So one of the things that is in place in order to ensure that that happens is what's called risk adjustment.</a> <a data-t="416">Throughout the year, all health insurance companies submit their claims data and their diagnosis data for their entire populations to these government-maintained servers.</a> <a data-t="436">And then you get credit for the risk.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="449">At the end of the year is when the risk adjustment takes place.</a> <a data-t="456">So insurers who insure a riskier population actually get transfer dollars from the insurers who insure a less risky population.</a> <a data-t="467">So it's entirely budget neutral.</a> <a data-t="472">There's no government money involved in changing hands.</a> <a data-t="479">They facilitate that transfer between those insurers but not anything else.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="487">So if one insurer insures a bunch of like elderly people with multiple comorbidities and one insurer insures a bunch of healthy college students, the one who insures a bunch of healthy college students is going to owe money to that other insurer at the end of the year.</a> <a data-t="530">I have a sister team who's involved in the risk adjustment and I am in charge of the audit of the risk adjustment.</a> <a data-t="540">So once you've submitted all of this data, the government takes a representative sample of your population and comes back to you and say prove it.</a> <a data-t="551">Prove that these members have the conditions that you say that they have.</a></p>
