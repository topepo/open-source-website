<h3>Lesson 1: Don't reinvent the wheel</h3>

<p><a data-t="65">The first one I want to share is don't reinvent the wheel.</a> <a data-t="70">So for some background for us, we've done some marketing campaigns more in the email marketing space,</a> <a data-t="76">where we could separate groups into a holdout group and a testing group and send emails just to our test group.</a> <a data-t="86">Now we're thinking, oh, can we use the same methodology for viewership?</a> <a data-t="91">However, you can't really go into somebody's house and say, you watch some ads and you don't.</a> <a data-t="97">So we had to find some other way to do this.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="101">Luckily, some of our colleagues were working with Hulu on a marketing campaign,</a> <a data-t="105">and were making use of a counterfactual model to do this modeling.</a> <a data-t="109">And even though they're different platforms, Hulu being streaming and our work was in the live TV space,</a> <a data-t="115">there's some commonalities that we could use to then refactor our code enough from theirs to our own project, hence not reinventing the wheel there.</a></p>

<h3>Lesson 2: Use the best available tool for the job</h3>

<p><a data-t="126">The next lesson I want to share is to use the best available tool for the job.</a> <a data-t="132">So the ads that we started with were, say, watching MLB and running this, again, on our script on your own laptop.</a> <a data-t="142">ESPN was also interested in how these ads would work on the other sports leagues that they have access to.</a> <a data-t="148">And in my mind, I'm like, okay, now we have an R script running now five times on one laptop.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="154">And this is how I kind of felt where we had this new model that we had to implement and refactor,</a> <a data-t="159">but also scaling up our processes to handle all these other different sports leagues.</a> <a data-t="165">So after going through some brainstorming, we're thinking, oh, okay, maybe we just need some sort of server to offload some of our compute,</a> <a data-t="172">maybe an Amazon web service or even Posit Connect.</a> <a data-t="176">Unfortunately, we didn't have access to those.</a> <a data-t="178">But coincidentally, our group also recently got access to Databricks.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="184">And after talking to some colleagues, we realized it was the best available tool that we had,</a> <a data-t="190">where it could offload some compute, but also organize some of our data workflows in their workflows feature.</a></p>

<h3>Lesson 3: Budget time for new tools</h3>

<p><a data-t="198">And the last lesson I'll leave you with is to budget time to make use of these new tools.</a> <a data-t="204">And so before, we had just things on our laptop and adding in some Databricks, right?</a> <a data-t="210">So this will take some time, and we're committed to this.</a> <a data-t="213">And I was like, how long could this take?</a> <a data-t="216">Maybe a couple of weeks?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="219">It turns out it took nearly two to three months to even just go through all the quirks and refactor our code,</a> <a data-t="226">putting things, getting used to Databricks, and et cetera.</a> <a data-t="230">So I would say, yeah, budget some time to make use of new tools or even existing tools.</a></p>
