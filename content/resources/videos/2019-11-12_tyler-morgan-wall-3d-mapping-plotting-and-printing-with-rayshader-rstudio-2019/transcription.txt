<h3>What is rayshader?</h3>

<p><a data-t="78">Rayshader is a package designed to create maps in R. And by create maps, I mean generating</a> <a data-t="84">maps directly from elevation data. This is in contrast to building maps off of preexisting</a> <a data-t="91">tiles. There's nothing wrong with using tiles to build your map, but it does limit your</a> <a data-t="97">aesthetic options to whatever the creator of those tiles decided on. By generating our</a> <a data-t="103">maps directly from the source elevation data, we can have full control over their appearance.</a></p>

<h3>Are 3D plots always bad?</h3>

<p><a data-t="675">So, are 3D plots always bad? No. The idea of 3D plots being bad stems from</a> <a data-t="681">the fact that 3D has often been used gratuitously, where the data itself is inherently 2D or</a> <a data-t="688">1D and 3D is just added for visual fluff. When a data has legitimate 3D mapping, creating</a> <a data-t="695">a 3D plot is perfectly fine. There's also the idea that since the display medium is 2D,</a> <a data-t="701">3D plots are not appropriate. So, here's two examples from the source of all things</a> <a data-t="707">cartographic in the USGS. The Atlas of Oblique Maps, published in 1988, contains beautiful</a> <a data-t="713">3D representations of topographic and bathymetric data. Unlike a 2D contour or color map, you</a> <a data-t="720">don't need to have a degree in cartography or to read a color bar to know what's going</a> <a data-t="724">on. The 3D structure of the landscape is immediately apparent.</a> <a data-t="731">Some might say, well, that's just</a> <a data-t="731">illustration. That's not data viz. But that's not the case. The cartographers of the USGS</a> <a data-t="737">actually created these maps by using what's called an isometrograph, which maps contours</a> <a data-t="742">to their 3D projection. These maps were based off of actual data visualized in 3D. And even</a> <a data-t="751">in 1988, cartographers understood something very important about these types of visualizations.</a> <a data-t="757">They are incredibly engaging and easy to understand. And this type of engaging tool is important</a> <a data-t="763">when trying to communicate complex ideas and analyses about our changing planet.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="769">Each visualization is part of a story, and sometimes the idea that the visualization</a> <a data-t="774">needs to be so precisely constructed that the reader could take a ruler to the plot</a> <a data-t="778">and reconstruct the data set manually needs to take a backseat to the story you're trying</a> <a data-t="782">to tell. This visualization was not made with rayshader, but I thought it was a great</a> <a data-t="788">example of a 3D map that tells a story on its own. The change in groundwater in Southern</a> <a data-t="793">California over time shows how the water table is breathing. Would this visualization be</a> <a data-t="798">as effective or engaging without the 3D? I don't think so. The 3D brings the data to</a> <a data-t="804">life in a way a color map or a use of small multiples wouldn't. This animation is likely</a> <a data-t="810">what caused this article to go viral and be exposed to thousands of people. And science</a> <a data-t="817">that is communicated to the general public and stokes their interest is infinitely more</a> <a data-t="822">valuable than science that just stokes the interest of those in academia. And 3D visualizations</a> <a data-t="828">are a great way to do just that.</a></p>
