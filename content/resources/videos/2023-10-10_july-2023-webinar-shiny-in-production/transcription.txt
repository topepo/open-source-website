<h3>What does "production" mean?</h3>

<p><a data-t="306">All right, so to kick things off, let's just talk about what does it mean to have something in production?</a> <a data-t="311">I'm going to quote </a><a href="/people/joe-cheng/">Joe Cheng</a><a data-t="311">, who's one of the creators of Shiny and also our chief technology officer here at Posit.</a> <a data-t="316">And way back in 2019 at our studio at the time conference, he defined production as software environments that are used and relied on by real users with real consequences if things go wrong.</a> <a data-t="328">So that's just one definition of what it means to have something in production.</a> <a data-t="333">But there are various goals of a production environment.</a> <a data-t="333">We need to keep it up, keep it safe, keep it correct, and keep it snappy.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="338">So in terms of keeping it up, we have to make sure we prevent any unplanned outages, keeping it safe.</a> <a data-t="344">We want to make sure that our data, the functionality, the code are all kept safe, unauthorized access within your firewall, your network, keeping it correct.</a> <a data-t="354">Make sure it works as intended.</a> <a data-t="354">Make sure that when you spin open the Shiny application, it's the same app today, tomorrow, a week from now, a year from now, 10 years from now.</a> <a data-t="364">And then also making sure to keep it snappy, that it has fast response times, there's not a lot of waiting from the end user.</a> <a data-t="370">And that's really what we're going to focus on for today.</a> <a data-t="374">We could certainly talk about these other ones, but just for our hour we have today, we're really going to focus on how to keep your Shiny applications snappy.</a></p>

<h3>The Shiny restaurant analogy</h3>

<p><a data-t="384">Now to help with my kind of presentation today, I'm going to make a fairly silly analogy, but I think it hopefully helps kind of explain how to make production-ready Shiny applications.</a> <a data-t="395">And we're going to talk about the Shiny restaurant.</a> <a data-t="395">So I really want you to put yourself in the shoes of a manager or someone who owns a restaurant.</a> <a data-t="407">And when it comes to a restaurant, obviously, to create food, you need recipes.</a> <a data-t="412">And a lot of these things in a restaurant, they actually translate pretty well over to the R ecosystem.</a> <a data-t="417">And so when it comes to a recipe, when you think about R, this is essentially your Shiny application, the code itself.</a> <a data-t="422">These are the instructions used to create your Shiny app.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="428">Next in our restaurant, we have our chef.</a> <a data-t="428">The chef is going to be the one preparing the food.</a> <a data-t="432">For our Shiny apps, that is going to be the R session or process that's going to be reading this Shiny application to create that Shiny app.</a> <a data-t="443">Next, we have our kitchen.</a> <a data-t="443">This is where your chef lives, where they cook all the food.</a> <a data-t="447">So this is going to be your computer, your server, anywhere you're running R.</a> <a data-t="453">And then finally, we have our end product, our food.</a> <a data-t="453">And that is going to be our rendered, our user interface, our Shiny application right here.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="460">So let's say we have your grand opening of your Shiny restaurant.</a> <a data-t="460">You've hired one chef, you have one kitchen, and in walks your very first customer.</a> <a data-t="466">They order some food, let's say a cheeseburger, the chef cooks it, delivers it to the customer, and the customer is super happy.</a> <a data-t="475">They leave the restaurant, and they spread the good word, they scream on the streets that your Shiny restaurant is the best restaurant in town.</a> <a data-t="480">So a few hours later, you get some more customers.</a> <a data-t="484">So now you have about six or seven customers, and things are still going great.</a> <a data-t="484">Your single chef can take in all these orders, prepare the food on the single kitchen, and everybody's happy.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="501">All right, all these customers leave, they start writing Google reviews, Yelp reviews.</a> <a data-t="505">And now your restaurant's starting to get a little bit more popular.</a> <a data-t="508">Let's say on the second day, now we have like 10, 15 people in your restaurant at the exact same time.</a> <a data-t="511">And your chef's starting to feel a little pressure, getting a little stressed out, but they can still accommodate all the requests, all the orders from your customers.</a> <a data-t="519">So things are great.</a> <a data-t="525">More and more, the word spreads how great your Shiny restaurant is.</a> <a data-t="525">The third day you're open, you get a line out the door around the block, everyone's pouring into your restaurant.</a> <a data-t="530">And now you have like 20, 30 people in your restaurant at the exact same time.</a> <a data-t="535">And at this point, your chef is like, oh, I am way overworked.</a> <a data-t="540">I'm in over my head, kitchen's on fire.</a> <a data-t="540">And this is going to result in some of your customers having a bad experience.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="551">So as a manager or the owner of the Shiny restaurant, we need to take a step back and ask, what is the breaking point of our restaurant?</a> <a data-t="557">So how many customers can visit our restaurant before things start to break down?</a> <a data-t="563">And if we translate that over to our Shiny application, the question becomes how many users can visit our Shiny application before things start to break?</a> <a data-t="573">And this process of kind of figuring out that threshold is known as benchmarking.</a></p>

<h3>Benchmarking with <a href="/software/shinyloadtest/">shinyloadtest</a></h3>

<p><a data-t="580">Now for Shiny applications, some of you may have heard of this tool, but we've created a tool here at Posit called Shiny load test, which does exactly that.</a> <a data-t="585">It tries to figure out how many people can visit your application at the exact same time before things really start to break down.</a> <a data-t="594">So I'm going to very quickly just run through how you would run a Shiny load test.</a> <a data-t="599">And in step one, you simply just have to run your application.</a> <a data-t="603">This can be running on your local computer, or you can have it running in like Posit Connect or </a><a href="/software/shinyapps/">shinyapps</a><a data-t="603">.io.</a> <a data-t="609">And once it's running, we're going to use Shiny load test to record your session.</a> <a data-t="613">So you have it running, you record your session, and then you just interact with your application.</a> <a data-t="617">You click buttons, you slide the bars, you generate plots.</a> <a data-t="621">And once you're done, that creates a recording log file.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="624">We then use in this third step, a second tool called Shiny Cannon.</a> <a data-t="628">This is actually a command line interface tool that we've created.</a> <a data-t="628">We feed in that recording log from step two, and then we can essentially simulate synthetic users to our application.</a> <a data-t="640">So in this command, we're going to generate four synthetic users or simulated users, and they're going to interact with our application for about two minutes.</a> <a data-t="651">And then once we run that command, we can essentially analyze the results.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="657">Now there's a lot of different output from Shiny load test, but this is probably the most common output you'll see.</a> <a data-t="662">So here we have this plot, it's a ggplot.</a> <a data-t="662">On the y axis, we're looking at the number of simulated users.</a> <a data-t="667">So we have our four users.</a> <a data-t="667">And on the x axis, we're looking at time.</a> <a data-t="672">In this case, it's going to be 600 seconds.</a> <a data-t="672">And on this gray background, you're going to see all these various colored blocks in each row.</a> <a data-t="677">These colored blocks represent time that the Shiny applications is essentially thinking.</a> <a data-t="682">It's frozen in time, it's doing some type of compute step.</a> <a data-t="688">And essentially, the user has to wait for the Shiny application to become responsive.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="693">So really, the goal of Shiny load test and with these plots is to see kind of, you want more of that gray background to show through and you want these colored blocks to be as thin as possible.</a> <a data-t="703">Because that's going to indicate that application is relatively snappy.</a> <a data-t="708">Someone clicks on a button, and the application does a step and is then ready for more input.</a> <a data-t="715">And just to give you a sense of what a very bad application would look like.</a> <a data-t="721">Here we have a plot where we're actually simulating 16 concurrent users against 600 or so seconds.</a> <a data-t="727">And here you can see that the colored blocks encompass the entire plot, we're not seeing any of the gray background showing through.</a> <a data-t="738">So this is essentially an application that can't really handle 16 concurrent users.</a> <a data-t="742">And so at this point, you know, you can start to get an idea of what that threshold is, we can see 16 is too much, we might want to backtrack it to figure out what is our kind of maximum number of concurrent users.</a> <a data-t="752">So that's Shiny load tests.</a></p>

<h3>Profiling your Shiny app</h3>

<p><a data-t="757">So let's go back to our Shiny restaurant.</a> <a data-t="757">And let's say we've done the benchmarking.</a> <a data-t="761">So we've determined that, yeah, we can have like five, six, seven or so customers in our restaurant.</a> <a data-t="765">And our chef's going to be happy, our kitchen is going to be happy, this is a good spot.</a> <a data-t="771">But by benchmarking, we've determined that 20 or so is too much.</a> <a data-t="775">All right, this is at this point is when the chef gets overworked, the kitchen bursts into flames.</a> <a data-t="781">And so that's too much.</a> <a data-t="781">So we find that sweet spot.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="793">But then we start asking some questions like, you know, well, how can I then support 20 customers into my restaurant?</a> <a data-t="806">You know, 20 is a no, I want more customers, I want to be able to support more and more people and make more money.</a> <a data-t="811">So how do we accommodate 20 customers?</a> <a data-t="811">Well, we have a few options.</a> <a data-t="816">We could again, as a manager, we can think about maybe adding more kitchens to our restaurant.</a> <a data-t="821">Alright, so we can have three kitchens here and just a single chef.</a> <a data-t="825">And essentially, they can multitask, right, so they can have multiple meals being prepared at the same time.</a> <a data-t="835">Maybe we hire more chefs.</a> <a data-t="835">Alright, so now we have three chefs in our kitchen, all sharing the same kitchen, which is probably fine, you can multitask a bit more here.</a> <a data-t="846">Maybe we do both, maybe we hire more chefs, we build more kitchens.</a> <a data-t="846">And yeah, this is absolutely going to be able to accommodate more customers.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="851">But it's worth noting that for these two options, it's going to cost money, you know, to hire more chefs and to build more kitchens.</a> <a data-t="855">And so for our Shiny application, that's essentially having more R processes, or more servers, more nodes, more computers supporting this application.</a> <a data-t="866">And that costs, you know, not only money, but compute resources as well.</a> <a data-t="873">So for today's webinar, I really want to focus on, you know, looking at our chef and asking the question, like, can we actually just improve the performance of our single chef?</a> <a data-t="883">And how would we do that?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="889">So again, you're the manager.</a> <a data-t="889">So what you do is you take a chair and you plop it in the kitchen, and you just sit there and you watch your chef.</a> <a data-t="894">And you have a stopwatch in your hand.</a> <a data-t="894">And as they go through the various steps of preparing a meal, you just clock how much time each step took a little bit of micromanaging, but bear with me here in this analogy.</a> <a data-t="909">So an order comes in, your chef collects the ingredients, then prepares those ingredients, cooks up the meal, assembles it, and then serves that finished product.</a> <a data-t="915">And again, as you go through each step, you determine how much time each step took.</a> <a data-t="920">Some steps go very quickly, some take a little bit longer.</a> <a data-t="925">So kind of cooking the ingredients, that's our rate limiting step here.</a> <a data-t="930">So this process of essentially figuring out every single step of your cooking process and how long each step takes, this is known as profiling.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="940">And we can do this with our Shiny application as well.</a> <a data-t="940">And before we actually talk about profiling, there's two rules here for profiling your Shiny application.</a> <a data-t="946">And the number one rule is don't guess.</a> <a data-t="953">Again, referencing Joe Cheng, our chief technology officer, even though you built a Shiny application, and you may know exactly what is the slow step, you still don't have to guess because there are tools out there that will tell you exactly what is slow in your application.</a> <a data-t="972">So some people might just say, Oh, I can know just using my intuition, I can think that this is probably the slow part of my Shiny app that I need to fix.</a> <a data-t="980">But Joe Cheng says, you know, your intuition stinks, I don't even know you, but it stinks.</a> <a data-t="985">And essentially, what Joe is jokingly saying is that you don't have to guess, you don't have to use your intuition, we have tools that will tell you what parts of your Shiny application are slow.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="995">So the number one, number two rule is to use a profiler.</a> <a data-t="1000">And probably the most common profiler for Shiny applications is a tool called ProfViz.</a> <a data-t="1006">So here in this GIF, I have a Shiny application that I'm opening up, and I'm just interacting with it as normally.</a> <a data-t="1011">But behind the scenes, I'm running this ProfViz tool.</a> <a data-t="1016">And so once I'm done interacting with it, it generates this report where we can see every single step in that Shiny application, how long each step takes and how much memory is being allocated or deallocated.</a></p>

<h3>Optimizing your Shiny app</h3>

<p><a data-t="1084">Alright, so now that we've profiled our Shiny application, right, and then going back to our restaurant, you start thinking of some ways of how we can improve our chefs kind of operations.</a> <a data-t="1090">So maybe we have the chef prepare the ingredients in advance, or maybe someone else in the kitchen can prepare those ingredients in advance.</a> <a data-t="1100">That way, not to spend the time preparing them as orders come in.</a> <a data-t="1106">Maybe we just keep the kitchen organized and updated, making sure the chef knows exactly where all the tools are, and making sure they have the best tools for the job.</a> <a data-t="1117">Prepare the most popular meals in advance.</a> <a data-t="1117">And I'll talk about this one here in a little bit.</a> <a data-t="1123">And then also multitasking, like training your chef to prepare two, maybe three meals at the exact same time.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1129">So we're going to go through each one of those steps, we're going to talk about it in the context of our Shiny restaurant, and then how that translates over to our Shiny application.</a> <a data-t="1137">And we're going to start with preparing ingredients in advance.</a> <a data-t="1143">So again, let's say your chef, they get an order for a ham and cheese sandwich.</a> <a data-t="1149">And when that order comes in, if this is their starting material, this raw material, they're gonna have to spend some time cutting tomatoes, slicing the bread, slicing the ham, and that's going to take time.</a> <a data-t="1159">And what would be much faster is if this was their starting material, all the ingredients were prepared in advance, they can simply grab each component, assemble the sandwich super quick, and then deliver it to the end user or the customer.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1175">So for Shiny apps, this essentially means preparing your data in advance.</a> <a data-t="1175">And 99% of all times when you have slow Shiny applications is likely because the data is too large.</a> <a data-t="1182">And you know, sometimes that's going to be kind of unavoidable.</a> <a data-t="1186">But other times we can prepare that data in advance, so that only the data that's needed for that Shiny application is processed.</a> <a data-t="1197">So a few different things to consider.</a> <a data-t="1197">Make sure when you're reading a data to your Shiny application that you read and process that data only once.</a> <a data-t="1203">You want to try to not get into a situation where every single time someone slides a bar in your Shiny application, it re-reads in the data, and then re-reads in the data over and over and over again.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1214">You should also think about, especially large data sets, having that stored somewhere else and not within your Shiny application itself.</a> <a data-t="1219">So maybe have it stored in a database.</a> <a data-t="1219">Or maybe move any data processing steps.</a> <a data-t="1224">So if you have to like transform your data or even model your data, you can actually try to move this outside of your Shiny app.</a> <a data-t="1232">You can have it within a pin.</a> <a data-t="1232">And so if you've never heard of pins before, it's a way to take data and store it either at like a S3 bucket, Bitbucket, Posit Connect can also store pins.</a> <a data-t="1249">Or you can take some of that data processing and you can put it into an R Markdown document and schedule that to run on Posit Connect.</a> <a data-t="1254">Or you can serve it as a </a><a href="/software/plumber/">Plumber</a><a data-t="1254"> API.</a> <a data-t="1254">These are all great options.</a> <a data-t="1261">Really the take home here, Shiny is a visualization tool.</a> <a data-t="1261">It was never quite designed to be a huge compute workhorse.</a> <a data-t="1268">And so anytime you can take these data transformation steps and move it outside of the Shiny application, the better.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1279">All right, so going back to our Shiny restaurant, another way that we can improve operations is to keep our kitchen organized and updated.</a> <a data-t="1284">So I've had to ask everyone here on the line, which kitchen would you prefer to work in?</a> <a data-t="1284">Would you rather one on the left where everything's nice and organized?</a> <a data-t="1289">Or do you prefer the one on the right where it's just a little bit more cluttered and disheveled, and you may not know where everything is?</a> <a data-t="1297">Now I'm going to guess you'll probably say one on the left.</a> <a data-t="1297">And let's say our chef, they said, hey, I need a new knife, a new kitchen knife to prepare the meal.</a> <a data-t="1303">As a manager, it would be a very poor decision to provide them with a very rusty, old, dull knife, or even worse, provide them with a medieval sword, which is not the right tool for the job.</a> <a data-t="1314">And essentially, we want to provide them with a very sharp, clean kitchen knife so they can do their job correctly.</a></p>

<h3>Caching outputs</h3>

<p><a data-t="1670">All right, let's go back to our restaurant and talk about another way that we can hopefully improve performance.</a> <a data-t="1675">And that is to prepare the most popular meals in advance.</a> <a data-t="1676">So I think the best way to think about this, let's say your Shiny restaurant is actually a pizzeria.</a> <a data-t="1681">And let's say if 10 people walk into your restaurant, nine of them are going to order your world-famous margarita pizza.</a> <a data-t="1690">So because of that, you start to think, well, okay, maybe I can start preparing a bunch of margarita pizzas in advance when I first open up my doors.</a> <a data-t="1701">And that way, when people enter the front door, I can just quickly deliver that already prepared margarita pizza.</a> <a data-t="1706">So essentially, you can have all these margarita pizzas in this hotbox, ready to be delivered instantaneously.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1715">So for Shiny applications, this essentially means caching your outputs.</a> <a data-t="1721">And some of you may be familiar with this, but what is caching your outputs?</a> <a data-t="1721">What Shiny will do is it'll store that combination of your input and outputs, so kind of the output of these render and reactive functions.</a> <a data-t="1731">And if the application, if it sees that input, it'll generate the resulting output only once, and then it will store that output in a cache.</a> <a data-t="1737">And if those inputs are seen again, it can simply retrieve that value or that plot from the cache instead of having to recompute it over again.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1757">Let me show you kind of conceptually how this works.</a> <a data-t="1757">So here we have a render plot function.</a> <a data-t="1757">So I'm generating a plot here of the state of Maryland, which is where I live here in the United States.</a> <a data-t="1767">And to generate this plot, it just takes a single input right up here, where I input the state Maryland.</a> <a data-t="1774">So let's say this takes about four seconds to compute.</a> <a data-t="1774">Not super slow, but you know, still four seconds, the user is going to have to wait.</a> <a data-t="1779">So what I can do instead is I can take this render plot function and I can pipe it into the bind cache function.</a> <a data-t="1784">It's going to take that single state input.</a> <a data-t="1791">That way, when I go to indirect to this application, I select Maryland, it generates this plot once, which takes four seconds, and then it caches that plot.</a> <a data-t="1796">And then if another user or my future self comes back to this application and selects Maryland, rather than having to regenerate the plot, it can just grab the plot from the cache and deliver it in a fraction of the time.</a></p>

<h3>Async programming</h3>

<p><a data-t="1815">All right, and now we have multitasking, which is going to be our last step we'll talk about.</a> <a data-t="1820">So it'd be really nice if your chef at the exact same time could have a turkey going in the oven, maybe some pasta sauce on top of the and on the range here, right next to some stir fry, all going at the same time preparing three different meals simultaneously.</a> <a data-t="1832">So what does that mean for Shiny apps?</a> <a data-t="1832">Well, it means using something called asynchronous programming, which sounds pretty complex.</a> <a data-t="1838">But the reason why we have to do this async programming is because R is inherently a single threaded programming language, meaning that it's kind of hard to do two operations at the exact same time.</a> <a data-t="1850">So to implement this async programming, we have to use the help of some packages, specifically promises and the futures package.</a> <a data-t="1856">There's also another way you can do this by essentially offloading any of these steps to something like an API, which can be running on a separate R session outside of your Shiny application.</a> <a data-t="1868">This is optional, but it's always good practice again to move any of those data processing or compute steps outside of your Shiny app.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1886">All right, so let's talk about how to actually leverage async programming specifically as promises package.</a> <a data-t="1892">And we're just going to use this example Shiny application I have here, which I'm going to call analyze data app.</a> <a data-t="1898">This application does two things, it takes in some inputs, and you can either plot the data, which goes very fast, it only takes about a second to plot that data.</a> <a data-t="1902">Or you can run a model on that data, which takes a little bit more time to run about 60 seconds.</a> <a data-t="1908">And again, let's say this app is using a single R session or an R process.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1915">Some of these users, they log in, we have four users here, and the only thing they want to do is plot some data.</a> <a data-t="1920">And they're happy because this step is very fast, they click plot data, and within a second, that plot is returned to them.</a> <a data-t="1939">Similarly, maybe you have a Shiny application that, you know, a user comes in, and they want to run the model.</a> <a data-t="1939">And so this takes about a minute.</a> <a data-t="1943">And the user knows that they know it's going to take some time.</a> <a data-t="1943">So they click run model, they go grab some coffee.</a> <a data-t="1947">And when they come back, the models trained and ready to be interacted with.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1953">But here we can get into an issue where let's say you have a user come in, and they want to run a model.</a> <a data-t="1953">So they click model.</a> <a data-t="1958">And then right afterwards, you want to have these users come in and plot some data.</a> <a data-t="1963">But now they essentially have to wait for this model to complete before they can plot the data.</a> <a data-t="1969">So something that used to take less than a second can now take, you know, over 60 seconds.</a> <a data-t="1973">And this is going to result in these users being very confused of why is it going so slow, and they're not going to be happy.</a></p>
