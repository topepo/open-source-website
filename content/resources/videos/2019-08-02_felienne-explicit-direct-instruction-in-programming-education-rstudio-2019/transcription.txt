<h3>The philosophy behind programming education</h3>

<p><a data-t="2099">And probably that's because of this philosophy that we're not really aware of.</a> <a data-t="2105">So Alan Blackwell, a really interesting scientist from the UK, he says that technology is covert philosophy.</a> <a data-t="2112">It's a really interesting statement.</a> <a data-t="2116">It means something like, if someone has built a piece of technology, from that you can see what the people believe about the world.</a> <a data-t="2123">And in a similar way, I think education is also covert philosophy.</a> <a data-t="2129">You can learn a lot about the things people believe from the way they teach.</a> <a data-t="2134">There's a lot to learn about our culture from the way we teach.</a></p>

<h3>The case for direct instruction</h3>

<p><a data-t="2298">So warning, some controversy ahead because some people really still very much buy into this philosophy.</a> <a data-t="2308">But across fields, it's been very, very clear that this left-hand side instruction phonics explaining is just better.</a> <a data-t="2318">It's just better than exploration.</a> <a data-t="2323">There's this paper by a Dutch psychologist called Paul Kirschner and some other people, why minimal guidance doesn't work.</a> <a data-t="2327">And it summarizes a long list of studies across fields saying you should just explain stuff to kids.</a> <a data-t="2336">There's this other paper actually by Sweller and Cooper that compares two groups of children.</a> <a data-t="2342">Half of the group got a worked example.</a> <a data-t="2344">So the teacher said, this is what you do and you just do the steps.</a> <a data-t="2351">And the other group of kids got a more traditional, maybe in some places, equation.</a> <a data-t="2357">They said, this is the equation and you have to solve it for A.</a> <a data-t="2359">And they didn't know how to do it.</a> <a data-t="2364">And they compared the children how well they did.</a> <a data-t="2368">And this left group, first group, did way better.</a> <a data-t="2371">They did the solutions in 1 5th of the time of the other group.</a> <a data-t="2376">So not 20% less in the 20% of the time.</a> <a data-t="2381">And they also did better on new programs, new problems.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2390">And this makes lots and lots of sense if you know a little bit about short-term memory.</a> <a data-t="2394">So here's another mandatory audience participation session.</a> <a data-t="2398">I'm going to put, let's say, $1,000 on the table.</a> <a data-t="2402">If you can remember this tiny snippet of things, I'll put in front of you for a few seconds.</a> <a data-t="2413">Ah, that was hard, right?</a> <a data-t="2413">No one got it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2457">That's super easy, right?</a> <a data-t="2459">But it was the same length, the same number of things, the same variety in things.</a> <a data-t="2461">And still, this was super easy to do.</a> <a data-t="2465">That's because our short-term memory can only hold seven plus or minus two items.</a> <a data-t="2471">And actually, you might be aware of this study.</a> <a data-t="2474">Neural research said it's now four items.</a> <a data-t="2477">So there's very, very limited room in our brain to store things.</a> <a data-t="2483">And this, why is this so easy?</a> <a data-t="2485">Because you just store three things.</a> <a data-t="2487">You don't store the individual letters.</a> <a data-t="2489">You don't store the individual words.</a> <a data-t="2491">You store the concept.</a> <a data-t="2491">You think of a cat, and then of love, and of cake, and it makes sense.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2520">So that has to do with something called cognitive load.</a> <a data-t="2523">If I would really bet you to remember this, then if you were to try memorizing it, you get this fullness in your brain, right?</a> <a data-t="2528">You're like, oh, it's an O with a dot, and a long stripe, and like a sigma, but reverse.</a> <a data-t="2532">You feel your brain getting fuller.</a> <a data-t="2535">And that fullness is called cognitive load.</a> <a data-t="2539">And that's the reason that this one group, the left group, does better, and in all sort of similar experiments, because the other group had a too high of a cognitive load.</a> <a data-t="2551">They're solving the problem, but they have to think of many things, like what approach am I going to take, and oh, what is that technique again?</a> <a data-t="2557">And their brain gets so full that there's no more room to actually store the memory of what they were doing.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2566">So I think the biggest, if you just remember one thing from my talk, is you don't become an expert by doing expert things.</a> <a data-t="2575">That's what these studies have shown.</a> <a data-t="2578">And that's the misconception that we have in programming, and to a certain extent, also in math.</a> <a data-t="2582">We think that if we want kids to be problem solvers, we give them problems to solve.</a> <a data-t="2586">If we want kids to be programmers, we give them programs to program.</a> <a data-t="2592">But it doesn't work like this.</a> <a data-t="2594">If you want kids to be problem solvers, you have to teach them to memorize a lot of strategies for problem solving, or a lot of strategies for programming.</a> <a data-t="2603">That's how they memorize.</a> <a data-t="2605">And then once they see a problem, their long-term memory is filled with all of these approaches they can then use.</a></p>

<h3>Research into direct instruction for programming</h3>
