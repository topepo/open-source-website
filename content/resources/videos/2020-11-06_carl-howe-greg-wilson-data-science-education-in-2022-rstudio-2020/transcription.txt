<h3>Three challenges for data science education</h3>

<p><a data-t="280">And one of the challenges I see is that data science faces serious challenges in the next two years.</a> <a data-t="291">You know, you would think, two years from now, it's within our vision.</a> <a data-t="296">It's right on the horizon.</a> <a data-t="298">But I think there are some big changes coming.</a> <a data-t="306">And in the immortal words of Yoda, if you're not afraid, you will be.</a> <a data-t="316">So I'm going to go through three challenges to guide you through this.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="321">The first is the data explosion.</a> <a data-t="329">Frankly, we're drowning in data nowadays.</a> <a data-t="336">I'm going to use just a really simple metric from IBM that said 90% of the data in the world was created in the last two years.</a> <a data-t="360">90%.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="369">So if you think of data science as looking for a needle in a haystack, we have a problem.</a> <a data-t="375">Because if we're looking for a needle in a haystack today, we're going to be looking at 10 haystacks by 2022.</a> <a data-t="384">And by the way, by the time we get to 2024, we'll have 100 haystacks.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="397">Now I know most of you sort of say, oh, but computers get faster.</a> <a data-t="403">It's no big deal.</a> <a data-t="413">Moore's law isn't going to bail us out.</a> <a data-t="415">This is Moore's law.</a> <a data-t="418">Factor of two increase in computing power every 18 months.</a> <a data-t="424">Actually originally a marketing campaign done by Intel, but it's worked out pretty well.</a> <a data-t="432">This is the data explosion.</a> <a data-t="437">If we look out five years, Moore's law gives us a factor of about four and a half in terms of computing power.</a> <a data-t="455">The data explosion, we're going to have 46 times as much data in five years.</a> <a data-t="460">So we're drowning in data, and we're not really prepared to process it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="466">Secondly, I want to talk about something that I really don't have to explain to you, but it's such a great topic, and it's something we really have to pay attention to.</a> <a data-t="485">It's irreproducibility results.</a> <a data-t="492">Sorry, I can't even say the word.</a> <a data-t="494">Unreproducible results.</a> <a data-t="496">We no longer trust science.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="502">This is a real issue, and it's not because science is inherently untrustworthy.</a> <a data-t="512">It's that we're kind of screwing up how we do it.</a> <a data-t="515">There's a great article in Physics Today.</a> <a data-t="518">By the way, I'm borrowing from Gerrit Grohlmann's slides here, because they're just so compelling.</a> <a data-t="523">He heard about this study that was being done about supercooled water published in Physics Today.</a> <a data-t="531">They published the paper.</a> <a data-t="532">They were basically just cooling down water, it had to be very clean water, and passed the point of freezing down to very, very low temperatures.</a> <a data-t="541">Two labs ran the same experiment, and they got different results.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="547">After seven years of suing companies like Nature, I think it was published ... No, it's in Physics Today, of course.</a> <a data-t="555">After suing the publisher, they finally got access to the other team's data.</a> <a data-t="560">What they discovered was there was a bug in the code that caused their studies to diverge.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="566">You can blame this as just one isolated institute, but there's a famous Amgen study that was done in 2012 that discovered that when they looked at landmark results, the foundations of their very science, they could reproduce only six out of 53.</a> <a data-t="446">Six out of 53.</a></p>
