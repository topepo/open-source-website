<h3>Teaching to a room full of empty chairs</h3>

<p><a data-t="419">The corollary to this is it's really, really hard to teach to a room full of empty chairs.</a> <a data-t="426">All of us all the time are picking up social cues.</a> <a data-t="431">We're constantly peripherally aware of whether somebody is nodding or whether they're checking their phone.</a> <a data-t="436">Did they just yawn or are they thinking because they're about to ask a question?</a> <a data-t="441">You might not notice that you're noticing, but you notice.</a> <a data-t="445">And as soon as you start teaching online, a lot of that is taken away.</a> <a data-t="450">If you take all of it away, you're going to find it very, very difficult to teach.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="458">What I suggest you do is get your students into some sort of webinar like this, but then have a few of them turn on their video so that you can see a few people.</a> <a data-t="471">If, for example, you're using Zoom, which is my preferred video conferencing tool, take a half a dozen names at random and ask them to turn on video, and then focus your attention on those six.</a> <a data-t="485">Ask them your questions.</a> <a data-t="487">Pay attention to their faces just as you would in a real class.</a> <a data-t="491">And in your next class, pick a different half dozen.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="495">This teaching practice predates video.</a> <a data-t="498">It was quite common.</a> <a data-t="499">It still is in some places.</a> <a data-t="500">If you've got a large class, to pick a few people, bring them down front and focus your teaching on them for this hour, but then focus it on a different half dozen for the next hour and so on.</a> <a data-t="512">They get individual attention.</a> <a data-t="513">You get the kind of responsiveness you need to make improvisation and teaching lively.</a> <a data-t="520">And everybody else now has permission to turn off their video and reduce the load on the system.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="525">If you're going to do this, please give people a way to opt out.</a> <a data-t="530">For a whole bunch of reasons, a lot of people are uncomfortable being on video online.</a> <a data-t="536">If you've ever had to deal with harassment or a stalker, you don't want images of yourself appearing that might go out of your control.</a> <a data-t="543">People have many other issues.</a> <a data-t="546">If they're working at home for the first time, for example, maybe they don't want others to be able to see the mess in the background, the kids playing, and so on.</a> <a data-t="555">So invite people, don't mandate it, but try to get, as I said, four, five, or six people that you're directing your teaching to.</a> <a data-t="563">That will keep you engaged and you'll do a better job with the teaching.</a></p>

<h3>Directing traffic and managing audio</h3>

<p><a data-t="568">The next one was recommended by one of my colleagues, and I'm embarrassed that I didn't think of it because it reveals my expert blind spot.</a> <a data-t="577">The first time you move your class online, give them a tour of the tool that you're using.</a> <a data-t="583">They probably haven't seen Zoom or whatever you've got.</a> <a data-t="588">They might not know where to click the button to mute.</a> <a data-t="591">Seems obvious once you know, it's anything but if you've just been thrown into it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="597">So taking 10 minutes at the start of your first online lecture to show people how you're expecting them to use the tool, or taking an hour beforehand to take a few screenshots and then share those with the class, can save a lot of grief.</a> <a data-t="614">If you're going to do this, make sure that you're logged in as a normal user, not as the teacher or the conference organizer, when you're giving the tool.</a> <a data-t="625">Because if you're logged in as a super user, you will see a different set of tools that they don't have, and that will confuse them.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="636">The next one up is to direct traffic.</a> <a data-t="640">You don't have to go full North Korean, that's where this picture's from.</a> <a data-t="644">But again, for a lot of people, this is their first time being in some sort of structured formal setting online.</a> <a data-t="651">So always give them verbal cues.</a> <a data-t="655">Ask them to put their questions in the chat.</a> <a data-t="658">Ask them to add their answers to a homework question to the shared Google Doc.</a> <a data-t="664">Call on them by name when you want them to speak, or ask them to type something like slash hand or raise hand in the chat when they want to speak.</a> <a data-t="676">Make sure that they're all getting direction about what they're supposed to do next.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="683">After a while, you can dial that down.</a> <a data-t="686">But I have found in my own teaching that I don't.</a> <a data-t="689">I constantly do it because I've constantly got people who are new or who are coming in from somebody else's class where they might have evolved a different set of conventions.</a> <a data-t="701">Whatever it is, you as the tour guide telling people now we're moving on to the Renaissance Arts section, everybody step this way, again, it takes away a lot of the uncertainty.</a> <a data-t="711">And right now, people have got enough uncertainty to deal with.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="718">One other point on this, mute early and mute often.</a> <a data-t="723">By default now when I'm in Zoom, after somebody finishes speaking, I don't wait for them to mute.</a> <a data-t="730">I don't even mute them.</a> <a data-t="732">I instinctively now hit the mute all button.</a> <a data-t="735">I put everybody back in a muted state, even if they don't need to be, because that way I never forget to.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="744">You can do a very good class audio only or audio with slides.</a> <a data-t="749">Video is helpful, but it's not essential.</a> <a data-t="753">And I can prove this by asking, what would happen if we left you with video of me talking right now but turned off the sound?</a> <a data-t="761">You wouldn't learn anything.</a> <a data-t="763">You've got to make sure that everybody can be heard.</a> <a data-t="767">You've got to make sure that you're not drowned out by feedback, by background noise, or crucially by somebody who constantly wants to be the next one to ask the question and constantly wants to jump in first.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="782">One thing that I do to ensure that is I actually keep a written list of names beside me of the people that I'm focusing my teaching on, that half dozen I referred to earlier.</a> <a data-t="793">Every time I interact with them, I just put a tick beside their name.</a> <a data-t="797">So that I can very quickly see who I have been talking to and who I haven't been talking to.</a> <a data-t="804">That way I can distribute my attention more or less fairly among those half dozen.</a> <a data-t="809">And if somebody is constantly trying to jump in, I can say, no, it's not your turn.</a> <a data-t="813">We're trying to take turns here.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="817">If you are relying on people to unmute themselves and just jump in with their questions, your class is going to be dominated by a couple of extroverts.</a> <a data-t="826">And everybody else will very quickly learn to not even bother to try to ask questions or to contribute ideas because they can never get in fast enough.</a> <a data-t="835">Mute early, mute often, and then have people request the microphone in order to speak so that you do the unmuting.</a> <a data-t="844">And as I said, call on people by name.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="846">If I ask the room, are there any questions, it will be followed with silence.</a> <a data-t="854">Sometimes that silence means nobody has any questions.</a> <a data-t="858">Sometimes that silence means people have questions, but they don't want to look foolish.</a> <a data-t="862">And sometimes that silence means they're very busy typing and the question is going to pop up in 15 seconds, by which point I will probably have moved on.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="873">Instead, when I want somebody to contribute, I call on them by name.</a> <a data-t="879">And if they want to pass, if they say, I don't have an answer, I don't feel like this is the right time, that's fine, I'll move on to the next person.</a> <a data-t="887">Calling on people by name also helps you level out your classroom.</a> <a data-t="893">You are about to discover that some of your learners think much more quickly than others.</a> <a data-t="900">That doesn't mean they're smarter.</a> <a data-t="902">Doesn't mean they're producing better work.</a> <a data-t="905">It just means that their first response comes faster.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="910">If you are calling on people by name, you can even that out by throwing softballs to people who might need a little bit more time, throwing harder questions at people who are a little quicker, and that way the whole class is progressing at about the same rate.</a></p>

<h3>Don't teach alone</h3>

<p><a data-t="927">This is all a lot to manage.</a> <a data-t="929">So this brings us to the other big takeaway.</a> <a data-t="933">Don't teach alone.</a> <a data-t="934">If you can possibly avoid it, don't be the only person trying to run things.</a> <a data-t="940">Every great surgeon has a great nurse beside her.</a> <a data-t="944">Every great teacher has a co-teacher.</a> <a data-t="947">It could be a fellow instructor, it could be a TA, it could be one of your learners that you've approached to say, you seem to be okay with this material, can I ask you a favor?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="959">Whoever it is, you want somebody else keeping track of whose turn it is to speak next, somebody else making sure that the slides are presenting properly, somebody else who is wrangling that shared document that we're going to talk about in a couple of minutes.</a> <a data-t="975">You want a co-pilot who's worrying about everything that isn't the actual delivery of the lesson in the moment to free you up to focus on your slides and what you're saying.</a> <a data-t="986">I've got that right now, and it makes a world of difference.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="991">It doesn't take a lot of practice to learn how to do that, and it does also mean that if next week you're unable to make a scheduled class for whatever reason, there's somebody else who's at least partly familiar with what you're teaching and with how you're delivering it.</a></p>

<h3>Practical setup tips</h3>

<p><a data-t="1019">So, do a dry run.</a> <a data-t="1025">I have a list someplace of things that have gone wrong while I've been teaching.</a> <a data-t="1030">I've fallen off stage twice and concussed myself once.</a> <a data-t="1033">I've had a fistfight break out, I've had a projector catch fire, I've had a woman go into labor, and I once actually fell asleep in one of my own lectures.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1041">Those are the easy ones.</a> <a data-t="1042">The hard one is nobody told me there was a firewall, so all of a sudden I can't get at any of the data sets.</a> <a data-t="1049">Nobody told me that they've updated Zoom and some of the controls have moved or behave differently, or I know all of that, but I've never done it with Robert, I've never done it with Karina, and so we're used to using the tool in different ways.</a> <a data-t="1067">Getting 10 or 15 minutes, half an hour before you're supposed to start talking, can make a world of difference to delivery and it can lower your blood pressure considerably.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1088">Another small note, keep your notes beside you.</a> <a data-t="1093">I've got a laptop on my desk and then I've got a large screen monitor above it.</a> <a data-t="1098">I think that's a fairly common setup these days.</a> <a data-t="1102">What that meant was that going back a while, whenever I was teaching, I was looking up and had my head tilted back, and my daughter eventually told me that as far as she was concerned, my webcam was a nose cam, and that's why I don't have a picture for this slide.</a> <a data-t="1113">If you've got your notes beside you, it's very natural to turn your head to the side, check what you're going to say next, and come back to your lecture.</a> <a data-t="1120">If you've got the slides up here, it's not something you're used to doing, and the angle can be rather unflattering.</a> <a data-t="1127">I know it's a small thing, but the small things add up.</a></p>

<h3>Accessibility and speaker's notes</h3>

<p><a data-t="1133">A much larger thing, please add speaker's notes to your slides.</a> <a data-t="1139">You are about to discover that a lot of your learners have trouble seeing or hearing.</a> <a data-t="1145">They've got good compensating strategies or accommodation strategies in a live classroom because they've got a lifetime of experience doing this.</a> <a data-t="1154">They may not have the same support now that you're bailing everything online.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1161">If you put speaker's notes in your slides, it makes the slides more findable because search tools know how to index them.</a> <a data-t="1169">It makes the slides more usable because people can understand after the fact what you meant by that picture, but crucially, it makes them more accessible, and however hard you think life is suddenly being thrown online, it's a lot harder if you're partially sighted or fully deaf.</a> <a data-t="1190">Please don't make life harder for people who already have more to struggle with.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1196">In fact, using accessibility tools helps everyone.</a> <a data-t="1200">I don't know if you can see my cursor right now, but I've used the accessibility tools on the Mac to make it as large as possible so that it's easier for people to see on video.</a> <a data-t="1213">I do that whenever I'm teaching.</a> <a data-t="1216">I use an enlarged cursor because a small cursor in a small window is really, really hard to see.</a> <a data-t="1222">I switch to high contrast mode so that people who might be sizing down the screen or might be on older devices or might just have crappy monitors have a better chance of being able to see what's going on.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1236">All of these tools help.</a> <a data-t="1238">Keycasting tools that will show people on screen all of the control keys, option keys, and other things that you're typing.</a> <a data-t="1247">You should be using those anyway when you're teaching so that somebody doesn't think you're typing 100 characters a second.</a> <a data-t="1254">They realize that you hit tab and got file name completion.</a> <a data-t="1258">Keycaster is one that you can use.</a> <a data-t="1260">Mouse Pose is another.</a> <a data-t="1261">There's a lot of accessibility tools out there.</a> <a data-t="1264">Most of them are either free or very cheap.</a> <a data-t="1268">Most of them are fairly easy to set up, and they will all help with your online teaching.</a> <a data-t="1272">And when you're done, keep using them when you go back in the classroom because they'll make life better for everyone.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1280">This recommendation comes again from Elizabeth Wicks.</a> <a data-t="1283">Teach from your smallest screen.</a> <a data-t="1287">If I try to take my half meter wide monitor and squeeze it down into a little video window, the video quality is going to be pretty grainy and the bandwidth demands are going to be pretty heavy.</a> <a data-t="1300">I'm not suggesting that you teach from a tiny handheld device like this, but please remember that most of your learners will be using smaller and older gear than you've got.</a> <a data-t="1312">You want to be at the bottom of the pack when you're the one sending out the video because that has the greatest chance of working everywhere.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1323">And I'll add a personal footnote to this.</a> <a data-t="1327">It's difficult to teach with an IDE when you're screen sharing because once you've sized things down, all of the controls in the IDE for different tabs, different menus, and so forth, all of those things that are really helpful, they're taking up a lot of space and you don't have a lot of leftover real estate for your own typing.</a> <a data-t="1347">I have seen people put RStudio IDE into a mode where only one pane is being shown at a time.</a> <a data-t="1355">That's great from a real estate point of view, but now it looks very different from what people are used to.</a> <a data-t="1360">I don't have a solution for this one.</a> <a data-t="1363">Ask your learners what they prefer.</a></p>

<h3>Voice, microphone, and notifications</h3>

<p><a data-t="1373">Going back to a topic from a few minutes ago, your voice matters more than your face.</a> <a data-t="1378">You can switch off video or you can go to still images like this one and give a decent class.</a> <a data-t="1385">You cannot lose voice or voice quality and still have a decent class.</a> <a data-t="1392">My setup is a Samsung microphone and a $25 stand from my local music store.</a> <a data-t="1397">I got the stand so that the microphone would be far enough away from the keyboard that it wouldn't be filled with all the clicking as I type.</a> <a data-t="1401">You can still hear the clicking, but it's a soft background noise rather than thunder.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1406">Again, try out your </a><a href="/software/rig/">rig</a><a data-t="1406"> with your co-instructor or with some randomly selected student, or if you don't have anybody else, mail me and I will listen to it.</a> <a data-t="1423">But getting the mic up level with your head, about 30 centimeters away from your mouth and away from your keyboard, and that's as much as you need to do now.</a> <a data-t="1433">If later on you want to go get spit guards and swaffles and do the full rig, absolutely, knock yourself out, but you don't need to do that first week.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1445">What you do need to do is turn off your notifications.</a> <a data-t="1451">You don't want things popping up on screen constantly telling you that you've got mail, that you've got something on Twitter you should be looking at.</a> <a data-t="1461">I turn Slack off completely whenever I'm teaching unless I'm using it to communicate with my students because it's just a distraction.</a> <a data-t="1470">You can ask them to turn theirs off as well.</a> <a data-t="1474">I know that most won't.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1479">And if you go into an in-person classroom, stand at the back and look over people's shoulders, at any given time about a third of them are doing something on their laptop.</a> <a data-t="1487">Some of it is course related, some of it isn't.</a> <a data-t="1490">Once they're in an online environment, there's going to be more of that, but it's worth asking.</a> <a data-t="1495">Again, if you've got a focus group of about half a dozen that you're directing most of your teaching to, they're more likely to switch off distractions and actually be engaged because they're being called on with higher frequency.</a> <a data-t="1515">So you can get a high quality experience for a small group and everybody else is going to do what they would do anyway.</a></p>

<h3>The shared document</h3>

<p><a data-t="1520">Now we come to that shared doc that I've been talking about.</a> <a data-t="1533">Every time I teach online, I create something like a Google Doc or a HackMD, for those of you who love Markdown, and I give edit permission to the people I'm teaching.</a> <a data-t="1540">Whenever I ask a question and want a response from everybody, I now have options.</a> <a data-t="1545">I can either tell them, put it in the chat, which I will do if I'm looking for a one word or two word response, or if I'm asking something a little larger and they need to show me a bit of code, write a couple of sentences, something like that, they paste it into the document.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1564">I say paste it in because a lot of people don't want you to see them typing.</a> <a data-t="1572">I'm comfortable with that, but I've been doing this for years.</a> <a data-t="1575">What I see a lot of newcomers do is have a separate text editor where they're composing their answer to question number three, and then they'll copy it and they'll just paste it into the Google Doc.</a> <a data-t="1586">That's cool.</a> <a data-t="1588">As they become more comfortable, maybe they'll do their editing directly in the doc so that others can see their mistakes and see them being corrected.</a> <a data-t="1596">That's cool too.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1598">The important thing is that it allows you to see their work in real time, and it allows them to see one another's work in real time.</a> <a data-t="1605">That dramatically improves engagement, and you get a lot of peer to peer learning.</a> <a data-t="1610">If you ask people to, for example, find the height of the tallest person in Louisiana in 1920 using a </a><a href="/software/dplyr/">dplyr</a><a data-t="1610"> pipeline, there are three or four plausible ways to arrange the commands to do that.</a> <a data-t="1627">Each person will have one, and then they'll be able to glance at a few others in the document and go, oh, that's cool.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1640">It's exactly like what we do in a music class where I say, okay, Janet, you play this piece.</a> <a data-t="1647">Okay, now, Janine, you play this piece.</a> <a data-t="1651">We're used to seeing and hearing others work in live contexts.</a> <a data-t="1655">We can do that online, and this is probably the biggest change in my teaching practice over the last 10 years is trusting learners to share work with each other in real time.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="1655">This is probably the biggest change in my teaching practice over the last 10 years is trusting learners to share work with each other in real time.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="1664">A couple of caveats for this.</a> <a data-t="1667">Number one, if you're going to do it, keep that list of half a dozen names or the entire class name, depending on how many are contributing to the doc, paste it into the doc before they start typing, and do that for each question so that everybody knows where they're supposed to start typing.</a> <a data-t="1683">If you say, just add this to the doc, you'll have two dozen people putting their cursors on top of each other and colliding with one another's work.</a> <a data-t="1692">That's not helpful.</a> <a data-t="1694">They'll very quickly opt out because it's frustrating.</a> <a data-t="1697">If on the other hand, I've got a list of names with a couple of blank lines between them in a text file, and I paste it into the doc, everybody immediately knows where to type and there's no collisions.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1709">The second thing has to do with scope.</a> <a data-t="1712">Google Doc will comfortably support 14 or 15, maybe 16 concurrent typists.</a> <a data-t="1718">It won't support 30.</a> <a data-t="1720">You'll see a sudden dramatic slowdown at some threshold, and I've never done the experiments to measure where it is, but it's somewhere between a dozen and 20.</a> <a data-t="1729">Again, you solve this problem by having a subset of people contributing to the shared document and swapping that up each time you take a coffee break, each time you restart your class.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1748">Talked about this before, keep track of who has and hasn't spoken to make sure that the people who aren't extroverts are getting airtime.</a> <a data-t="1758">It doesn't have to be electronic, just a piece of paper and a pencil beside you, putting tick marks in, trying to make sure that you distribute your attention fairly so that everybody feels like they're not just allowed in the room, but they're welcome in the room.</a></p>

<h3>Ask fill call and predictions</h3>

<p><a data-t="1791">Ask fill call is a pretty common tactic from the days of broadcast radio.</a> <a data-t="1797">If I ask a question, it's going to take people a while to formulate an answer.</a> <a data-t="1806">If I'm asking them for a long answer, three lines of code, for example, it's probably going to take them 15 seconds and even a gap of seven seconds, which you just heard can be really uncomfortable.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1832">If I want somebody to come in with a question, I ask the question, and then as they're composing their answer, I fill the </a><a href="/software/air/">air</a><a data-t="1832">.</a> <a data-t="1840">I say something else, I elaborate on a previous point, they'll probably tune me out while they focus on their answer, so it can't be anything too important, but it's like making small talk while somebody is setting the table.</a> <a data-t="1852">You want to ask your question, you want to fill in a little bit so that there's not dead air, and then you want to say, okay, Sarah, now what have you got for us?</a> <a data-t="1862">Again, you're doing this to make up for the lack of social cues.</a> <a data-t="1867">You don't have that peripheral awareness of people's attention, so you're going to have to be more directive.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1876">Another way that you can take advantage of the online teaching is to ask people for predictions.</a> <a data-t="1882">This is particularly important when you're teaching coding.</a> <a data-t="1886">If I'm teaching a programming class, I'm probably sharing my screen so that you can see the code as I'm building it.</a> <a data-t="1894">Every time I'm about to do something, I have an opportunity to say, all right, everybody, in the chat, what's going to happen if I run it now?</a> <a data-t="1903">I've just added this line, how's it going to behave?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1907">Pedagogically, this is forcing people to pull recent knowledge back into consciousness and use it and reinforce it.</a> <a data-t="1915">That will help them learn and remember.</a> <a data-t="1918">From a motivational point of view, it increases their engagement.</a> <a data-t="1921">They're constantly being asked to think and to contribute, and from your point of view, it gives you a very quick formative assessment.</a> <a data-t="1931">For those who don't know the term, formative assessment is what you do during learning to find out whether or not people are learning.</a> <a data-t="1939">It's your real-time feedback loop.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1942">You cannot trust people to go, uh-huh, I got it, I got it, because a lot of people will lie.</a> <a data-t="1948">They'll say they have it when they don't, or they'll believe that they do, but they'll be wrong.</a> <a data-t="1952">And they're even more likely to mislead this way when you're teaching online.</a> <a data-t="1958">If on the other hand, I say, all right, Paul and Ahmed, what's this going to do next?</a> <a data-t="1964">If they're right, you know that they're understanding.</a> <a data-t="1969">If they're wrong, then you know that you've got something to explain, to clarify, or to cover a second time, and you can do it right then and there before their misconception has a chance to set in concrete.</a></p>

<h3>Breakout rooms and peer instruction</h3>

<p><a data-t="1965">This is a point where I would normally poll my class to say how many of you know breakout rooms.</a> <a data-t="1971">For those of you who don't, if you're using something like Zoom, you can go down and put everybody into a small sub-conversation.</a> <a data-t="1981">You can assign them manually, or you can assign them at random.</a> <a data-t="1985">You can vary the size of the rooms, and you can determine how long they're in that side conversation.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1993">Here's a question.</a> <a data-t="1994">The answer isn't obvious.</a> <a data-t="1996">Everybody go and think about it, and then argue with your three partners, and see if you can come to a consensus about what this code does or doesn't do.</a> <a data-t="2005">Three, two, one, go.</a> <a data-t="2008">You've got a couple of minutes, and then I pull you back, and I poll a couple of people.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2015">This emulates a practice called peer instruction, which is the most effective scalable teaching practice we know.</a> <a data-t="2024">The slide deck has a link to a video from a math class in India in 2013 that illustrates the method.</a> <a data-t="2031">Short version is, I ask a motivating question, something whose answer requires more than just factual recall.</a> <a data-t="2041">You all then go and argue with each other over the answer, and then I pull you back and give you the explanation.</a> <a data-t="2048">Why the argument stage?</a> <a data-t="2050">Because that's when you have to think through the inconsistencies or gaps in your mental model, and you've got other people there who can fill them in.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2058">One of the great discoveries is, it doesn't really matter if anybody in one of these little discussion groups is right at the start.</a> <a data-t="2064">What matters is that you disagree, so that you can clear up each other's misconceptions.</a> <a data-t="2069">Pedagogically, it's a well-justified method.</a> <a data-t="2072">Practically, it also helps with motivation, because instead of only teaching to half a dozen for half an hour, you can teach to that half dozen, and then say, okay, now everybody's going into a small discussion group, and then I'm bringing you back, and now they have to interact with their peers.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2092">Again, you want people to be able to opt out.</a> <a data-t="2096">They may not be in a situation where they can have a conversation.</a> <a data-t="2100">They might be in the living room, muted, because the entire family is here at the moment.</a> <a data-t="2105">You've got to respect that.</a> <a data-t="2106">Some people may be uncomfortable arguing with strangers at the outset of a class.</a> <a data-t="2111">You respect that.</a> <a data-t="2112">But for everyone else, it's a very effective technique.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2117">One of the things that we can't do with any video tool I have today is put people into breakout rooms in which they can all screen share with each other.</a> <a data-t="2129">I would really like to be able to put people into pairs and have them screen share so that they can co-program.</a> <a data-t="2137">The technology will get there eventually, but something like that or a shared whiteboard in the breakout room would probably help a lot.</a> <a data-t="2146">We just have to wait.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2148">And since I mentioned whiteboards, Zoom provides polls so that you can ask people questions in real time and do a survey.</a> <a data-t="2156">It also provides a whiteboard.</a> <a data-t="2159">Don't try to use it yet.</a> <a data-t="2161">Become comfortable with the online teaching, sort out some of your other problems, and then start to try to take advantage of all the science fiction you've been handed.</a> <a data-t="2169">You don't need it today.</a> <a data-t="2171">Take advantage of it tomorrow.</a></p>
