<h3>GSK use cases: quantitative decision making</h3>

<p><a data-t="619">So the two GSK use cases I wanted to talk about, the first one is a </a><a href="/software/shiny-r/">Shiny</a><a data-t="619"> use case.</a> <a data-t="619">And this is something we call quantitative decision making at GSK.</a> <a data-t="630">In a nutshell, it is the probability of being able to make a decision at the end of the trial, given</a> <a data-t="634">the operating characteristics of the study, so the study design, and various other pieces of information that we know up front.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="658">Essentially, what we try and do with quantitative decision making at GSK is we take known values.</a> <a data-t="658">So up in the top right-hand corner, there's a diagram here where it talks about this is looking at treatment difference.</a> <a data-t="662">So on the x-axis, you've got a difference, for example, a difference between our drug and placebo, or our drug and some competitor on the market.</a> <a data-t="674">And in a standard clinical trial, you might look at the difference between those two compounds.</a> <a data-t="679">And you're going to generate some kind of p-value.</a> <a data-t="684">And if it's a late phase study, you might be looking for something like p less than 0.05 to show that there is some kind of treatment difference.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="694">And that's great at the study level.</a> <a data-t="694">But what QDM is looking at really is the lifecycle of the compound.</a> <a data-t="707">So in the early phases of development, so in phase 2, where QDM is particularly used at GSK, you can't power study</a> <a data-t="707">sufficiently to be able to make those p less than 0.05 kinds of statements.</a> <a data-t="714">What you want to be able to do is, given everything you know about the drug, you want to be able to estimate probabilities.</a> <a data-t="719">What's the probability that the difference between our two compounds is greater than some clinically meaningful, commercially viable difference?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="734">Now, all of this is quite complicated.</a> <a data-t="734">It uses a Bayesian framework.</a> <a data-t="740">We put priors on all the information we know.</a> <a data-t="740">There's a lot of study design elements that we consider.</a> <a data-t="744">And what we're trying to get to at the end is the probability of success of the study.</a> <a data-t="748">So the probability that we're going to be able to make a clear decision as to whether to continue into the next phase of development or stop development of our compound.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="764">And because it's quite complicated, when we trained a lot of our clinicians in the methodology, they weren't immediately receptive to everything that we were saying.</a> <a data-t="769">It's a lot of complex mathematics when mathematics is generally not their first job.</a> <a data-t="778">So we developed an app.</a> <a data-t="786">And in doing so, take all those factors, things like the sample size,</a> <a data-t="787">the randomization ratio, the standard deviation, things you can see on this screen here, hopefully.</a> <a data-t="791">And you combine that with information about the minimum value and the target value,</a> <a data-t="796">and potentially lots of other operating characteristics as well.</a> <a data-t="802">And you allow the clinicians and the clinical teams to play around with the sliders, change different options.</a> <a data-t="807">And then it starts to become real for them.</a> <a data-t="811">They can start to see what the impact of the study design is on the ultimate probability of success.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="817">And that's really led, because this is such an important initiative at GSK, it's rolled out across all of our studies at GSK that are in that phase of development.</a> <a data-t="833">And because of that, we're seeing widespread adoption of QDM methodology.</a> <a data-t="833">Everyone is seeing the Shiny apps, and there's a huge number of requests for additional Shiny apps.</a></p>
