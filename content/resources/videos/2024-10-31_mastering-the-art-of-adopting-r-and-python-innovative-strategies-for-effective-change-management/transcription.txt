<p><a data-t="195">How do we do plan, go and manage change effectively?</a> <a data-t="198">Well, I have three phases.</a> <a data-t="200">First of all, we plan.</a> <a data-t="201">I'm going to spend a little bit more time on the planning part because it's sometimes</a> <a data-t="205">that is more overlooked.</a> <a data-t="207">It's about defining the change, understanding the potential impact of the change and creating</a> <a data-t="212">a detailed strategy to go to that process.</a> <a data-t="215">Then implementing the change itself, I can give some tips and tricks there, and then</a> <a data-t="220">eventually sustaining the change.</a> <a data-t="222">So it's not enough to just have people implement it or that we implement the change, but that</a> <a data-t="228">we also reinforce the behaviors and the processes there as well.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="233">So first of all, the planning phase.</a> <a data-t="236">It's not just enough to start planning for training.</a> <a data-t="239">So why plan for change?</a> <a data-t="242">I don't know if you've ever tried to assemble EKF furniture without any instructions.</a> <a data-t="248">It's like embarking on a heroic quest armed only with an Allen wrench, a screwdriver,</a> <a data-t="255">and I think a vague sense of optimism.</a> <a data-t="257">So you start very confident, convinced that you don't need a manual to only find yourself</a> <a data-t="263">an hour later with a mysterious piles of extra screws, a lopsided bookcase, and I think a</a> <a data-t="269">psychosynching feel that something is terribly wrong here.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="273">So planning for change is just like writing those EKF instructions, but then for others</a> <a data-t="279">and also better knowing EKF instructions to help avoid that you end up with a wonky outcome</a> <a data-t="285">and that you do basically everything fits together from the start.</a> <a data-t="290">There's a bonus that also most working relationships will hopefully stay intact.</a> <a data-t="296">So yeah, before launching a new software or going with a new programming language,</a> <a data-t="300">please prepare for that change.</a> <a data-t="303">Just providing training is not going to cut it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="307">And in planning for change, I like to go over five steps.</a> <a data-t="310">Basically explain the what, the who, the why, any concerns, and also the when.</a> <a data-t="315">So let's start off with the what.</a> <a data-t="318">So in defining the what of the change, you go from a current state to a future state</a> <a data-t="323">and you have a transition.</a> <a data-t="325">But there are four key questions that help explain to other folks what actually the future</a> <a data-t="331">state will be and how you're going to transition.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="333">And that is, what are the things that we are used to be able to do and still can do?</a> <a data-t="339">Another question is, what are the things that we couldn't do before and still cannot do?</a> <a data-t="343">These two questions are very important because it means that this is business as usual.</a> <a data-t="348">You don't have to change anything there and that calms people down.</a> <a data-t="352">So it's good to explain that as well.</a> <a data-t="354">And then, of course, there are the things that we could do before and can do now.</a> <a data-t="358">So these you have to let go.</a> <a data-t="359">And the things that you couldn't do before and can now.</a> <a data-t="363">So that is where you have to go.</a> <a data-t="365">That's a change that's hopefully giving you a lot of gains.</a> <a data-t="369">So without a clear definition, there is room for ambiguity.</a> <a data-t="372">It can lead to resistance, also confusion, or inefficient use of resources.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="380">Who is involved in the change?</a> <a data-t="382">So how do we define that?</a> <a data-t="384">These are entities, groups, individuals who are affected by the change.</a> <a data-t="388">Also there, I would divide them into four different groups.</a> <a data-t="390">The ones that are above the change, these can be your sponsors, can be influencers,</a> <a data-t="395">change partners on the change.</a> <a data-t="397">These are mostly the people in your project starting to work out the change.</a> <a data-t="402">The change recipients, those ones will be in the change or going through that journey.</a> <a data-t="406">And then also interesting onlookers around the change.</a> <a data-t="409">Sometimes other departments who are also looking at something you like to implement.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="414">And identifying these key stakeholders is also an ongoing process.</a> <a data-t="418">For instance, you can have groups who are onlookers and get interested.</a> <a data-t="422">Okay, they're adopting R, they want to use R.</a> <a data-t="424">They might also be, hey, can we not be involved in that journey maybe as well?</a> <a data-t="428">So they change from onlookers to be recipients.</a> <a data-t="431">And also a good thing to look out is for if you have recipients,</a> <a data-t="435">to maybe divide them into subgroups.</a> <a data-t="437">Going from softs to R, you have maybe a subgroup recipients is statisticians.</a> <a data-t="442">You might make a subgroup there of, okay, these are statisticians who already know R</a> <a data-t="447">or these are statisticians who don't know R yet.</a> <a data-t="450">So that helps in defining, okay, you might have to tackle those groups a little bit differently.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="456">Step three is assessing the impact of change.</a> <a data-t="459">It's not about producing widgets or features.</a> <a data-t="462">So it's not about, it's about changing behavior.</a> <a data-t="465">I give an example, if you put in a sign to say people to turn right,</a> <a data-t="470">that doesn't mean that people are going to turn right.</a> <a data-t="472">So the widget is there, but it doesn't mean that the behavior is changing</a> <a data-t="475">and that people are turning right.</a> <a data-t="477">So it's all about the outcomes itself.</a> <a data-t="480">And you can explain it very well, the impact for people with pains to gain.</a> <a data-t="484">So what is for them to gain?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="488">And that explains it a little bit more from the emotional side than from the technical side.</a> <a data-t="494">At heart is, yeah, the question that we need to answer is for what's in it for me.</a> <a data-t="498">And you need to do that uniquely for each stakeholder.</a> <a data-t="501">So for instance, from going from SAS to R, you can explain to statisticians</a> <a data-t="505">that they have a wider range of advanced statistical packages and libraries</a> <a data-t="512">to their availability.</a> <a data-t="514">And that gives them cutting edge, gives them access to cutting edge methods.</a> <a data-t="519">And that from a statistician's perspective can really help.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="525">The fourth step is developing strategies for managing resistance.</a> <a data-t="530">Well, people might say we've always done it in this way and it works fine with SAS.</a> <a data-t="535">Well, they're often expressing a variety of underlying sentiments and perceptions.</a> <a data-t="540">They have a sense of loss of control, they're uncertain, it's something new.</a> <a data-t="544">They might fear of failure, doubts that it might work</a> <a data-t="547">because they have already gone through past failures.</a> <a data-t="551">They don't see the problem with the current process.</a> <a data-t="554">So people resist for many, many different reasons and can also be very personal as well.</a></p>
