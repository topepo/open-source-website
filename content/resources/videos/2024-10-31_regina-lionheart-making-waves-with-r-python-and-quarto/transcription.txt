<h3>Introducing hydrodynamic modeling</h3>

<p><a data-t="120">We also have an incredibly developed shoreline.</a> <a data-t="120">Humans have been in this area for about 13,000 years.</a> <a data-t="125">And when Western settlers came to the area in the 1800s, the shoreline modification changed at a very high rate.</a> <a data-t="132">So a lot of shorelines look very different from how they used to.</a> <a data-t="135">And these modifications interfere with natural processes that include erosion and beach building and habitat formation.</a></p>

<h3>Clayton Beach: the project site</h3>

<h3>Running the wave models</h3>

<p><a data-t="304">So how we're going to do this is run hydrodynamic modeling over engineering surfaces that our engineers came up with.</a> <a data-t="311">And once we have the surfaces, we can identify areas of high risk.</a> <a data-t="317">So Clayton Beach is exposed to a very high fetch, which is the distance over which waves can develop.</a> <a data-t="322">It is openly accessed through the Strait of Juan Fuca.</a> <a data-t="326">And there are mountain ranges that surround us, creating variable wind patterns and a lot of things that we needed to account for in our models.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="335">So this is what a model domain looks like.</a> <a data-t="337">This is called a SWAN model, which computes random short crested wind generated waves in a hypothetical environment.</a> <a data-t="344">You're looking at depth there and energy dissipation over a certain space.</a> <a data-t="349">And these models can do everything that real waves would do.</a> <a data-t="352">So shoaling as they hit the bottom, refraction, changing the waves around items that they might hit, white capping, reflection, transmission,</a> <a data-t="362">all of these wave behaviors can be modeled in the software.</a> <a data-t="365">In other words, it can basically tell you what your sandcastle is going to look like by the afternoon.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="370">These are the shallow water equations.</a> <a data-t="370">This is how this modeling software works.</a> <a data-t="375">This describes the movement of fluid under a free surface with low viscosity.</a> <a data-t="379">And when you put these models into the real world, they start to describe the action of real waves.</a> <a data-t="385">So this is called a wave breaking, which is kind of like a person walking in the trip and their head goes over their heels.</a> <a data-t="390">So the top of the wave is moving faster than the bottom.</a> <a data-t="393">And once we have these equations, we put them over a geographic grid.</a> <a data-t="397">And then those equations are run in space and time over the grid.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="403">So what this ends up looking like, on the right hand side, you have a flat surface that's being modeled within a bathtub scenario.</a> <a data-t="410">The water equations are run across that surface.</a> <a data-t="413">And then all of that refracting and changing of the water is handled by the modeling software.</a> <a data-t="418">On the left hand side, you can see the waves are moving along that coastline.</a> <a data-t="422">When they hit the man-made armoring structure at the top, they refract around the sides.</a> <a data-t="427">So this is just visualizing what those mathematics are doing.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="432">So back to our site.</a> <a data-t="432">There's Clayton Beach up on the right hand side.</a> <a data-t="435">And here's the entire modeling domain that is containing the site where the mathematics will be run.</a> <a data-t="440">And if you remember this fun gif from earlier, it really does look something like this.</a> <a data-t="444">These waves are running over a computational grid.</a> <a data-t="448">Fortunately for us, the modeling software handles all of that mathematics internally,</a> <a data-t="453">because otherwise that would be a lot of math to do for that space.</a></p>
