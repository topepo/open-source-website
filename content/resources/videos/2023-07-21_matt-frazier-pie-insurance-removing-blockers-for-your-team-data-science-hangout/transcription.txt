<h3>The model building process</h3>

<p><a data-t="3081">Generally speaking, we will, on a regular basis, go out to each one of the department heads and we will ask them, what are your problems today?</a> <a data-t="3095">What is creating too much friction?</a> <a data-t="3098">What is something that you think could be a better decision that's being poorly decision today?</a> <a data-t="3104">Then we'll get all of that information back.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3106">Once we construct all of the potential opportunities for data science, then we start to look around at the available data sources that we have and we start to do some discovery around whether or not we can actually solve that particular problem with data.</a> <a data-t="3122">Do we have the data available to be able to solve that problem?</a> <a data-t="3125">Is that problem a big enough problem to solve?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3129">Usually, once we go to the department heads, we'll then go to the business or department analysts within that particular department and say, hey, how big of a problem is this?</a> <a data-t="3138">Can we quantify the friction?</a> <a data-t="3141">Can we quantify how much this is costing us?</a> <a data-t="3143">How many minutes?</a> <a data-t="3143">How many hours?</a> <a data-t="3144">How many days?</a> <a data-t="3145">How many employee days does this take?</a> <a data-t="3152">Theoretically, if we were able to remove that and automate that, what would the savings be?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3159">Then, of course, there's other things in the industry.</a> <a data-t="3159">How much better can we make around these decisions?</a> <a data-t="3164">Can I separate good risk from bad risk?</a> <a data-t="3166">Can I identify the probability that someone's actually going to pay their bills?</a> <a data-t="3169">Can I identify the cost if they don't pay their bills?</a> <a data-t="3173">Can I identify how many times they're not going to pay their bills and we've got to do something with that policy, whether it be to cancel it and reinstate it and all that stuff because that costs insurance company money.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3198">Once you sit on a problem and you know that there's enough value there and you know that you have the data to be able to solve that problem, then it's a pretty standard process.</a> <a data-t="3208">You have to wrangle the data.</a> <a data-t="3209">You have to normalize the data.</a> <a data-t="3211">You have to work together with the department experts in that department to identify what they think might be interesting or valuable covariates.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3220">So usually we will do some unsupervised modeling just to figure out what the data looks like.</a> <a data-t="3226">Where might there be blooms of data that could be interesting?</a> <a data-t="3230">And then we'll do a correlation study, which is with as many covariates that we can actually put together and then bring that back to the department and then ask the subject matter experts what's not in here that you think should be in here.</a> <a data-t="3243">And then they'll start to think about and construct some additional features.</a> <a data-t="3247">Then you start the feature engineering phase.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3249">You do that with the subject matter experts in that department and you try and get as many features that you think might be useful as possible.</a> <a data-t="3257">And then, of course, there's some target transformation sometimes.</a> <a data-t="3260">Are you going to build a logistic model?</a> <a data-t="3261">Are you going to build a linear model?</a> <a data-t="3263">Are you going to build something that you're going to use for triage?</a> <a data-t="3266">Is it going to be sort of a categorical model where you've got five or six different decisions that need to be made and you're going to split those up?</a> <a data-t="3274">You kind of figure out what the best sort of opportunity is there.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3278">And then we usually go through a sample and partitioning.</a> <a data-t="3281">How are you going to sample this?</a> <a data-t="3282">What's your strategy going to be?</a> <a data-t="3284">What is your validation strategy?</a> <a data-t="3286">So are you going to do cross-fold validation?</a> <a data-t="3288">What's your holdout going to look like?</a> <a data-t="3289">What's your train set going to look like?</a> <a data-t="3291">What's your testing set going to look like?</a> <a data-t="3293">Are those stratified correctly?</a> <a data-t="3295">Is anything time series based?</a> <a data-t="3297">Because that has a significant impact on how you're thinking about your sampling strategy.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3303">Usually in insurance, depending on data size and the composition of the target, we use anywhere between three partitions and 10 partitions.</a> <a data-t="3315">If it's a more complicated problem and we've got a lot of data, we'll use up to 10 partitions and we'll hold three or four of them out.</a> <a data-t="3321">And then we'll use incrementally four or five or six or seven of those in order to increment through the overall model building process.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3329">We'll use the first partition, maybe even split that up in order to do feature engineering and basic variable selection and reduction.</a> <a data-t="3339">Then we will build our initial model and then we'll test it on the next partition.</a> <a data-t="3344">And then normally what we do is we'll build on, let's say you have five different partitions.</a> <a data-t="3349">We build on one and two, test on three.</a> <a data-t="3350">Build on one and three, test on two.</a> <a data-t="3351">Build on two and three, test on one, right?</a> <a data-t="3354">And then once you're really confident that you've got the right model using that data, you add the next partition.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="3359">Now you've got one, two, and three built on four, four, three, and two built on, you know, tested on one and so on and so forth, right?</a> <a data-t="3366">And we're constantly looking at coefficients, whether they're shifting, whether they're going up and down.</a> <a data-t="3371">If it's a tree model, we're looking at what the splits look like.</a> <a data-t="3374">We're sort of doing hyperparameter selection usually on those early partitions until you get to the final model, usually with two or three validations that's already held out.</a> <a data-t="3384">And in insurance, we're usually using between five and eight cross-fold validation at each one of those stages, just to make sure that we're getting, there's a lot of volatility because of the zero point math in insurance.</a> <a data-t="3397">So you really need to make sure that you've got enough cross-fold validation there because you can have a very large loss that makes things look really weird in any one partition of the data that you're looking at.</a></p>
