<h3>A case study in fixing a slow process</h3>

<p><a data-t="268">But how can we take lessons from this and keep our next project from becoming the next Edsel?</a> <a data-t="273">So I'm going to go through a little case study of a project that we worked on where we identified a slow process that we wanted to fix.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="283">So inside of our organization, we have a programming team that does all our data analysis and data prep.</a> <a data-t="291">They process this data, they do all this formatting, and they've output to several different files and intending for downstream users to use this to generate reports and presentations and reports and whatnot.</a> <a data-t="304">These downstream users take this, the statisticians will generate reports or presentations, and our medical writers will generate reports of differing levels of programming backgrounds and whatnot.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="316">The issue with this is our programmers are generating data in formats that our downstream users couldn't use directly.</a> <a data-t="324">That meant that there was a lot of manual reproducing of data, a lot of copy and paste from the format that we were producing into their PowerPoint and Word documents.</a> <a data-t="334">When people are copying and pasting, that leads to formatting inconsistencies.</a> <a data-t="337">They're like, oh, I'll just delete that, I don't like the way that percent looks, I'll just put it at the top there, or I want more spacing here, I want to merge these columns.</a> <a data-t="346">Not fundamentally changing the data, but the way it's displayed.</a> <a data-t="349">And because we're doing all this copying and pasting and reformatting, we have to do a lot of really intense QC on data we've already generated and already QC'd to make sure that we're not showing anything that's incorrect.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="361">So there's all this effort going into this process that is just we're not producing the correct data, we're not working, we're not talking together to make sure that everything works together nicely.</a> <a data-t="372">But we saw this as, you know what, that's not a bad thing.</a> <a data-t="375">That's an opportunity for us to step in and help and try to improve the process.</a> <a data-t="379">Because the data is being used by the same downstream user, or the same data are being used by the downstream users, we can use this as a starting point and try to build processes and build a package to try to make their lives easier.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="397">Next, we found out and learned about packages that have come out like the Officeverse, which is a series of packages by David Goel that interact with Office products and help you write your OOXML to allow you to inject and work with your Word and PowerPoint package or presentations and documents and whatnot.</a> <a data-t="411">So Officer.</a> <a data-t="411">And there's also FlexTable, which is a package that helps you make tables into PowerPoints and Word documents.</a> <a data-t="418">Kind of seems like it will be pretty useful for us.</a></p>

<h3>Designing with purpose</h3>

<p><a data-t="422">So before we really went and just went all full tilt to develop anything, we wanted to make sure we had a full set of expectations so we understood what we were trying to build before we went to go build it.</a> <a data-t="432">So if we're designing a new workflow, we want to create collaboration.</a> <a data-t="436">That was what was causing problems before between our programmers, statisticians, and medical writers.</a> <a data-t="440">We wanted to work with our existing systems.</a> <a data-t="443">We don't want to have to try to create and roll up a new system.</a> <a data-t="447">What do we have that what can we reuse?</a> <a data-t="449">Where can we extract the data from?</a> <a data-t="451">Can we pull from existing streams?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="454">We're already using PowerPoint and DocX, so let's output to that.</a> <a data-t="457">Let's not make our end users relearn everything.</a> <a data-t="461">And then as we generate these inputs, let's automatically tag those, because you can use comments in PowerPoint and DocX, tag those with comments that, hey, you're going to need to QC this.</a> <a data-t="471">This was generated, you know, custom.</a> <a data-t="473">And then ultimately this process originally was very slow.</a> <a data-t="476">We wanted to reduce the timeline into less than a week.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="482">Then as we went to go develop the team, now that we had our expectations, we had a small team of experienced individuals that were both understood the process, what they were trying to replace, as well as understood what they were generating or how to program.</a> <a data-t="497">This was important.</a> <a data-t="498">So we understood what were the constraints.</a> <a data-t="499">What could we feasibly make or not make?</a> <a data-t="503">We had a single point decision maker.</a> <a data-t="504">They were ultimately responsible for making a call if there was a disagreement.</a> <a data-t="508">Then we worked with the experts, the people that we're using, our downstream users as well as upstream users, to define, okay, what are the core things that we need to create?</a> <a data-t="516">What are the features that need to be on the roadmap eventually?</a> <a data-t="518">We maybe don't need them immediately.</a> <a data-t="521">But ultimately they did not have the final decision.</a> <a data-t="523">They weren't the executives at Ford that were telling us what to do.</a> <a data-t="526">They were saying, hey, it would be really nice if.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="529">We tried to simplify things.</a> <a data-t="531">We didn't want to try to recreate the wheel.</a> <a data-t="533">Like I said, we didn't want to recreate systems.</a> <a data-t="535">So let's use existing tools.</a> <a data-t="537">Let's use Office first rather than trying to make our home cooked version of Office first to interact with the XML and PowerPoint and DocX.</a> <a data-t="546">Let's use the tools that already exist and try to maybe improve it as we need to.</a> <a data-t="551">People were adding their own standards and their own changes to the outputs.</a> <a data-t="554">So let's revert back to what are the standards?</a> <a data-t="557">What exactly is it that we have to make and then we can maybe iterate from there?</a> <a data-t="561">And then identify areas of common rework by the staff.</a> <a data-t="564">What are the common reformatting that is being done?</a> <a data-t="566">And can we do this programmatically?</a> <a data-t="568">Is it reasonable to do it programmatically?</a> <a data-t="570">What are things that we need to capitalize or lowercase or change?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="576">While we developed this, too, we wanted to watch our scope.</a> <a data-t="579">So we wanted to make sure that the system could grow as we need it to change as we need it.</a> <a data-t="584">The world is a changing place.</a> <a data-t="586">Let's try to keep that in mind.</a> <a data-t="588">The feature requests that we got, we would compare them against the feasibility of doing that.</a> <a data-t="593">Once again, can we do this programmatically?</a> <a data-t="594">Should we be doing this programmatically or should we push back and go, maybe this isn't the time?</a> <a data-t="600">We also have limited resources.</a> <a data-t="602">We had a small team of people working on this.</a> <a data-t="604">So that made us keep in mind that everything that we did was in strict adherence to the goals.</a> <a data-t="610">What are we actually trying to achieve and try to push away things?</a> <a data-t="613">This might sound a little bit familiar.</a> <a data-t="614">This is kind of agile.</a> <a data-t="615">I don't know if you've ever heard of that.</a></p>

<h3>Making a <a href="/software/gt/">GT</a>, not an Edsel</h3>

<p><a data-t="699">I'm going to close with a final story from the same company, Ford, and we're going to make a GT, not an Edsel.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="721">So 1966, Ford wins the world-renowned hardest race in the racing world, the 24 hours of Le Mans, which is a race where people drive for 24 hours and the winner is determined not by how many laps they do, but how far they drive.</a> <a data-t="488">This is a true endurance test of the quality of your car, how good is it made, how well did you design this?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="735">The impressive thing about this, this is the second year Ford had ever competed in Le Mans.</a> <a data-t="741">They'd never competed in it before.</a> <a data-t="745">The prior winner had been Ferrari, who'd won for the last six years and has a long history in racing.</a> <a data-t="751">So how did Ford go from a company that made a thing like the Edsel to the GT and won the whole darn thing in their second year of competing?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="762">Well, they had a small dedicated team.</a> <a data-t="766">They set out to beat Ferrari.</a> <a data-t="768">That was their goal in mind.</a> <a data-t="770">Not just win the 24 hours of Le Mans, but let's beat Ferrari.</a> <a data-t="775">They got together a small team, Carroll Shelby and Ken Miles, who had a history of success doing this sort of thing.</a> <a data-t="783">Carroll Shelby was an old racer, so he used to race, he actually won Le Mans I think a decade or two before and started his own car company called Shelby American.</a> <a data-t="792">You might know him from the Shelby Cobra.</a> <a data-t="795">As well as Ken Miles, who had been a successful driver in Britain, racing various cars of various degrees.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="804">And then everything they did for the next two years was focused on results.</a> <a data-t="809">What could they do to make the GT faster?</a> <a data-t="811">How could they win Le Mans?</a> <a data-t="813">They've worked through aerodynamics.</a> <a data-t="817">They had an initial concept.</a> <a data-t="819">They did, they constantly drove around.</a> <a data-t="822">They had their drivers that were going to be in the race drive the car around, give feedback to the engineers.</a> <a data-t="826">Even this is a picture of the engineers and drivers with little pieces of string that they taped onto the GT to see how aerodynamics worked and if there were any areas of low pressure that they would need to address.</a> <a data-t="837">And they trusted the driver's experience.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="844">In addition to that, they pushed technologies forward.</a> <a data-t="847">They took the Ford's proven racing engine from NASCAR and improved it.</a> <a data-t="851">They made it faster, they made it stronger.</a> <a data-t="853">They took existing technologies and made it better.</a> <a data-t="855">And they also innovated.</a> <a data-t="857">They took the quick change or they invented a quick change brake pads and rotors.</a> <a data-t="861">The GT was a very heavy car.</a> <a data-t="863">They brake a lot in Le Mans or they have to press the brake pad.</a> <a data-t="865">So they needed a quick way to redo this.</a> <a data-t="867">So they created new technologies in order to solve the problems at hand.</a> <a data-t="870">And that was all they were ever focused on.</a> <a data-t="872">How can we do this faster?</a> <a data-t="873">How can we do this better?</a></p>
