<h3>Hard skills and soft skills of tools</h3>

<h3>Knowing your users</h3>

<p><a data-t="1202">So when we think about the soft skills, I really</a> <a data-t="1204">want people to think about the human side of a tool.</a> <a data-t="1208">And the first thing is to know your users.</a> <a data-t="1214">At Posit, a lot of us were data scientists.</a> <a data-t="1216">So we have a lot of data science knowledge.</a> <a data-t="1218">So we really intimately know the struggles of what we were running into.</a> <a data-t="1224">And I think something interesting here is</a> <a data-t="1227">the difference between a data scientist and a software engineer.</a> <a data-t="1230">Data work inherently looks a little bit different</a> <a data-t="1233">than what we would consider software engineer work.</a> <a data-t="1236">Data practitioners are more on exploring uncharted territories.</a> <a data-t="1241">They do a lot of iteration and a lot of experimentation.</a> <a data-t="1246">And then software engineers, we can think of them as building castles</a> <a data-t="1249">in known lands, things that involve a lot of structure,</a> <a data-t="1254">and things that have a very specific outcome.</a> <a data-t="1258">So you're not going to give your cartographer a mason's tools.</a> <a data-t="1262">So knowing your users is really important.</a> <a data-t="1264">And if you don't know, it's OK to ask.</a> <a data-t="1266">I think that's a very thoughtful way to approach this problem.</a></p>

<h3>Discoverability</h3>
