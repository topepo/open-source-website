<h3>Understanding polygenic risk scores</h3>

<p><a data-t="126">So the polygenic risk score follows a normal distribution in which</a> <a data-t="131">the majority of us will have an intermediate score, which is</a> <a data-t="136">the same risk as the general population,</a> <a data-t="138">but some of us will have a high score due to having a lot of this small effect variants and</a> <a data-t="145">we'll have an increased risk for breast cancer due to the polygenic risk score.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="153">This variants with small effects are discovered through very large studies with hundreds of thousands to millions of people.</a> <a data-t="162">These studies are called genome-wide association studies or GWAS.</a> <a data-t="167">They are generally conducted with individuals of a single genetic ancestry</a> <a data-t="172">because it facilitates the discovery of this variants.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="177">The majority of this study so far as you can see in this plot</a> <a data-t="180">90% of them have been conducted with individuals of European ancestry and the big problem about that is that</a> <a data-t="188">not all PRS is discovered in European populations can be generalized to other ancestries</a> <a data-t="195">because especially there are differences in</a> <a data-t="200">frequency and effect of variants among ancestries.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="188">not all PRS is discovered in European populations can be generalized to other ancestries</a> <a data-t="195">because especially there are differences in</a> <a data-t="200">frequency and effect of variants among ancestries.</a></blockquote>
