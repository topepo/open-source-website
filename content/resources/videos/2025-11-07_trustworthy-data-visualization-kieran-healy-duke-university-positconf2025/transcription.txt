<h3>Technical analysis and the chart men</h3>

<p><a data-t="263">One early stream of data that people really wanted to tell a story about was this one, the Dow Jones Industrial Average or averages.</a> <a data-t="272">In the early days there was one for industry and one for the railroads and you can just see kind of at the bottom there one for utilities too, which actually still exists.</a> <a data-t="285">The Dow Jones was a steady stream of data about the stock market as a whole and the desire to transmit that data far and wide in a more or less continuous stream was one of the driving forces behind the expansion of the telegraph, the development of modern communications technology generally.</a> <a data-t="308">Deep in the bones of your terminal, of your PowerShell, of your </a><a href="/software/positron/">Positron</a><a data-t="308"> or RStudio or VS Code session, you will find the marks of the teletype and ticker tape machines that were built to transmit price data.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="326">There was a reason that people really wanted that stream of data.</a> <a data-t="330">They wanted to make money and in some cases they wanted to make money by using graphs to see into the future.</a> <a data-t="338">Charles Dow, the founder of both the Wall Street Journal and Dow Jones, was retrospectively credited with the Dow Theory, which later became technical analysis.</a> <a data-t="349">And technical analysis started from price charts, which of course had to be drawn by hand, and combined it with a theory of what investors in the market were thinking and then he used that to predict prices.</a> <a data-t="363">And in practice, the main technique was telling stories with graphs.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="368">The people who did this were the chart men, and they were all men.</a> <a data-t="373">The classic source is a book by Robert Edwards and John McGee called Technical Analysis of Stock Trends.</a> <a data-t="380">It came out in 1938 and it is still in print.</a> <a data-t="384">It's now in its 11th edition, I think.</a> <a data-t="387">The idea was that if you looked at trends in stock prices, either for the market as a whole or for individual stocks, you could spot recurring patterns and patterns that told you what might happen next.</a> <a data-t="401">Not infallibly, of course, but enough to give you an edge, to let you make an informed decision to buy or sell.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="409">So you start with a high-low closed chart like this one, which is from Edwards and McGee, and then you start looking for the telltale patterns.</a> <a data-t="419">What patterns, exactly?</a> <a data-t="421">Well, it turns out there are a lot.</a> <a data-t="423">Some of them seem more or less unobjectionable.</a> <a data-t="426">A number will go up, a number will go down, a number will go sideways.</a> <a data-t="432">I think this style of drawing is very nice, by the way.</a> <a data-t="435">They're not like sparklines, right, that people put in summary tables even today.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="439">But the chart men also thought that you should take care to look at fluctuations within the trend.</a> <a data-t="444">They were very keen to argue that downward trends, true downward trends, had to be measured by the downward trends of the highs, whereas true upward trends had to be measured by the upward tendencies of the lows, for example.</a> <a data-t="460">Now anyone can see a basic upward or downward trend, but at this point the theory starts to get a little richer.</a> <a data-t="467">One idea was that after a period of steady growth, you would often see investors pull back from a stock as they lost confidence in it, and hence the pullback effect.</a> <a data-t="479">So the trick would be to sell at the top of that third peak there.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="483">They had a whole family of these patterns, each with a name, a distinctive visual signature, and a story associated with what to do when you spotted it.</a> <a data-t="493">Their favorite was one that was called head and shoulders, which was this triple peak thing where the third rally of a stock failed and it ended up falling below its original price.</a> <a data-t="505">And so what you really wanted to be was where the smart money was, which was selling to buyers at the top of the head there.</a> <a data-t="513">As I say, the underlying theory here was a kind of psychology of market segments, which is not an unreasonable idea.</a> <a data-t="521">John Maynard Keynes made a lot of money in the stock market in his day and once remarked that the business of the investor was to predict what average opinion thought average opinion would be.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="521">John Maynard Keynes made a lot of money in the stock market in his day and once remarked that the business of the investor was to predict what average opinion thought average opinion would be.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="533">By the way, I've often wondered if the anti-dandruff shampoo was named after this phenomenon, and I honestly think it was.</a> <a data-t="541">Procter &amp; Gamble introduced head and shoulders in 1961 and it was marketed as a medicinal cream to well-dressed men embarrassed about getting dandruff on the collars of their suits.</a> <a data-t="553">Men who, as you can see here, looked at stock prices in the paper.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="557">Anyway, the chart men didn't think these patterns were iron rules or laws of nature.</a> <a data-t="562">They regularly cautioned that you can't just look at the chart and read the future, obviously, because if you could then everybody would be doing that.</a> <a data-t="571">But they were still committed to the theory, to the story, and this led them understandably to develop what a philosopher of science might call some auxiliary hypotheses to save the main idea.</a> <a data-t="583">For instance, maybe some data doesn't quite fit the head and shoulders pattern.</a> <a data-t="587">That's probably because sometimes head and shoulders patterns are complex, like maybe you have two heads, or two shoulders, three or four shoulders.</a> <a data-t="597">You already have two shoulders.</a> <a data-t="599">Maybe sometimes there's a failure pattern either at the bottom or at the top, and of course when I say a failure pattern, what I mean is that the world has failed us in some way, not the theory, because our theory knows about failure patterns.</a> <a data-t="613">We have a name for them and everything.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="615">So the technical analysts, the chart men, poured over charts like this and they made their recommendations.</a> <a data-t="621">They told their stories with their data.</a> <a data-t="623">What killed this theory in the end was the rise of the efficient markets hypothesis in its various forms, and that's the idea that in something like the stock market, asset prices reflect all available information, or at least all publicly available information about an asset.</a> <a data-t="641">So in the short run, prices follow a random walk.</a> <a data-t="644">That means you can't consistently beat the market, at least not like this.</a> <a data-t="649">The technical analysis people were just reading patterns into noise.</a> <a data-t="653">Sure, sometimes it worked, but in the long run, you wouldn't outperform an index fund, except by luck.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="661">I wanted to write a little tiny bit of code to generate a random walk of stock prices, and I was kind of hoping it had produced one of the standard patterns, and I put in my seed for today's date, and sure enough, it did on the first try.</a></p>

<h3>What makes a visualization trustworthy?</h3>

<p><a data-t="596">The thing about the chart men is that there's nothing untrustworthy about their data.</a> <a data-t="601">The stream of information they used came straight from Dow Jones, and as for the score, well, the thing about technical analysis is that it wasn't that technical.</a> <a data-t="609">Really the only score or technique they used was putting the numbers into graphs, which was a painstaking business.</a> <a data-t="614">You had to do it by hand, and there's nothing wrong with the graphs either, considered as graphs.</a> <a data-t="618">Some of them are really good.</a> <a data-t="620">What's wrong, of course, is the story, which is nonsense.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="634">Now, we don't like to think of ourselves as the sort of people who'd be taken in by something like looking for head and shoulders patterns in price charts, and certainly we don't want to think of ourselves as the sort of people who are inflicting some version of this sort of thing on our audiences.</a> <a data-t="648">For one thing, you might say, look, I'm not trying to predict the future.</a> <a data-t="651">I just want to describe something that happened.</a> <a data-t="653">I just want to explore my data.</a> <a data-t="655">Or you might say, hey, I actually have a real theory, a proper theory that can explain the thing I'm showing you.</a> <a data-t="661">I have real evidence.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="663">But this tendency really is pervasive.</a> <a data-t="666">Here's an example from a few years ago.</a> <a data-t="668">Vox published a story discussing this startling rise in the availability of fats, especially vegetable fats, in people's diets.</a> <a data-t="676">It was pretty widely circulated.</a> <a data-t="678">That word availability should alert you already to that there might be something going on, because the availability of something in your diet isn't the same as you actually consuming it.</a> <a data-t="690">But beyond that, the fact that something like 20 pounds of this increase happened in about a year is odd.</a> <a data-t="698">You can probably guess how this ends, but the thing is people really, and I mean really, wanted to tell a story about this trend.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="705">Maybe something about long-term shifts in the American diet, the insidious rise of veganism, stress eating caused by the Y2K problem, the emergence of avocado toast as a central feature in millennials' lives.</a> <a data-t="722">That was actually one of the theories.</a> <a data-t="724">In fact, what happened was a change in reporting requirements.</a> <a data-t="728">A good rule of thumb is that if you see a sudden shift or discontinuity in a time series like this, your first question should be, did something about the data generating process change?</a> <a data-t="739">You should sort of tap the instruments.</a> <a data-t="741">Or better, try finding someone who knows where the data came from.</a> <a data-t="745">As Tom Smith of the National Opinion Research Center likes to say, if you want to measure change, you can't change the measure.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="752">In this case, unlike with the Chartman, there really is a big signal in the data.</a> <a data-t="757">It's just not signaling what people imagined.</a> <a data-t="760">But in both cases, what people want to do is eyeball a graph and spin you a yarn.</a> <a data-t="765">I don't think graphs are uniquely to blame here.</a> <a data-t="768">People like to tell stories, and they'll tell them with whatever is at hand.</a> <a data-t="773">But I think it's fair to say that people giving talks like the one I'm giving right now can be tempted to make quite strong claims about visualization's unique capacity to provide insights into the structure of your data.</a> <a data-t="787">Maybe it's just the idea that visual storytelling or a strong data narrative makes good graphics uniquely compelling in a way that conveys your insights to others.</a> <a data-t="797">And implicitly or explicitly, there's also this idea that the best data visualizations are authoritative, that they can decisively settle a question or definitively make a point.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="808">We kind of want these things to go together, right?</a> <a data-t="811">We want to make something that fuses an insightful, compelling, and authoritative use of data into a single, immediately accessible image.</a> <a data-t="820">Ideally something like this, for instance, the one John Snow made with the Broad Street water pump, pinpointing the existence of a cholera outbreak before going and removing the handle from the pump.</a> <a data-t="833">The little squares are measures of deaths in the buildings on the streets.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="838">There really is something to the idea that a good graph can do a fantastic job here.</a> <a data-t="843">A compelling and authoritative insight is an epiphany, a moment of truth.</a> <a data-t="848">And a great graph is a kind of portable epiphany.</a> <a data-t="851">The history of data visualization, or maybe I should say the history of data visualization talks, gives many examples.</a> <a data-t="858">These often come with little stories of their own, little tiny myths, like how a particular image kick-started the field of epidemiology, or explained why the Challenger space shuttle exploded, or summed up the reality of climate change for people.</a> <a data-t="873">But I think we need to be careful.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="879">Don't get me wrong, some of these graphs are really good, elegantly assembled, cleanly executed, really effective.</a> <a data-t="887">I wish I had drawn them.</a> <a data-t="889">But the thing is, we know this can't be the whole story, as it were, because even if we just consider graphs alone, we know it's not the whole story, because the other thing data visualization talks are full of is examples of really bad graphs.</a> <a data-t="906">Like this one, for instance.</a> <a data-t="908">If you had to bet your life, what would you say those values are?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="918">To be fair, the 3D column chart is not the default way that Excel draws bar charts.</a> <a data-t="923">You have to make a slight effort to define the 3D option.</a> <a data-t="927">But believe me when I say, people are willing to put the work in.</a> <a data-t="931">We love these terrible examples, and there are so many of them.</a> <a data-t="934">Every time you think people have finally stopped using axonometric or oblique projections to show three or four numbers, a new one comes along.</a> <a data-t="943">But examples like this do tend to undermine this noble story of visualization as a distinctively powerful source of insightful, compelling, and authoritative narratives.</a> <a data-t="954">They make it clear that there's a lot more going on when we put together a visualization, a lot more ways for things to go sideways, or if not sideways, maybe sort of backwards and up about 120 degrees.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="968">And it's not just a matter of badly chosen graph types either.</a> <a data-t="972">We have undeniable evidence that this whole business of just looking at anything is totally broken.</a> <a data-t="979">And I don't just mean your choice of graph.</a> <a data-t="982">I mean it's a mess like all the way down.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="988">Now, I acknowledge that navigating a slowly cooling universe filled with blobs of condensed matter in a hellscape of electromagnetic radiation while not being eaten is a really hard problem to solve, especially on the fly.</a> <a data-t="1001">But still, whoever designed the human visual system has a lot to answer for.</a> <a data-t="1007">Visual perception is weird.</a> <a data-t="1009">The human eye is not a light meter, for example.</a> <a data-t="1012">It runs on relative contrast, not absolute brightness.</a> <a data-t="1015">Those two squares are the same shade of gray.</a> <a data-t="1017">People don't believe you, but I'm just covering it up.</a> <a data-t="1021">And methods for visualization have to deal with these things, sometimes riding around them, sometimes leveraging them.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1027">But as I say, we know all this.</a> <a data-t="1029">And that's why I'm not dwelling on a parade of horrible graph crimes.</a> <a data-t="1033">We have decades of work, from William Cleveland in the 1980s, on down to the present, investigating what does and doesn't work when it comes to visually representing data.</a> <a data-t="1044">Working out how good or bad people are at reconstructing relationships in the data based on graphs that we show them.</a> <a data-t="1051">Knowing how evolved features, capacities, and limits in the human visual system fare when put to work at looking at data, a task that they did not evolve to conduct.</a> <a data-t="1063">We really know a lot, and not just the relatively simple stuff either.</a> <a data-t="1068">But super tricky things, like color perception.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1072">And not only that, we all benefit from the many years of hard work getting to put this into practice.</a> <a data-t="1079">The work to develop a flexible and powerful conceptual language for describing graphs, like Leland Wilkinson's grammar of graphics.</a> <a data-t="1088">The work done by people in this room to develop tools to implement those ideas in software, like ggplot.</a> <a data-t="1094">The work to integrate it with other freely available tools, developed in the same spirit of openness and cooperation, in a giant collaborative enterprise that lets me sit in my kitchen and pull down, process, and visualize, say, 40 years of global sea surface temperature data, where each day has about 4 million observations.</a> <a data-t="1117">As if that was a reasonable thing to expect to do in your kitchen.</a> <a data-t="1121">It's not even a very nice kitchen.</a> <a data-t="1123">It's not even remodeled.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1127">So this is where we are now.</a> <a data-t="1129">Version 4 of ggplot has just come out, and congratulations to everybody involved in getting it out the door.</a> <a data-t="1137">As June pointed out in the announcement, ggplot is old enough to vote now.</a> <a data-t="1143">Unlike most 18-year-olds, it is mature, well-organized, and stable.</a> <a data-t="1149">And so are other visualization packages and frameworks and toolkits in the R community and beyond this, in Python and the JavaScript people, and many more besides.</a> <a data-t="1160">We really are in excellent shape.</a> <a data-t="1162">Our software is reliable in the sense that it does what we expect it to do.</a> <a data-t="1167">It maps quantities and features to points, lines, shapes, and colors in a perceptually accurate way.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1171">The way we work is also increasingly reproducible.</a> <a data-t="1174">The tools that we have for scripting, testing, and rebuilding our work are better than ever.</a> <a data-t="1179">The people who make </a><a href="/software/quarto/">Quarto</a><a data-t="1179"> have been doing fantastic work to make it easier than ever to produce and publish reports, articles, and books, while still keeping things based on code that we can rewrite and rerun.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1193">And there's that last part.</a> <a data-t="1195">We want our visualizations to be trustworthy in some general sense.</a> <a data-t="1199">Are they?</a> <a data-t="1200">Can they be?</a> <a data-t="1201">And what exactly is the role of software when it comes to making our work more trustworthy?</a> <a data-t="1207">After all, when someone tells you a story, when someone shows you a graph and gives you an interpretation of it, you want to know whether you can believe them, whether you can trust it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1218">Here's why I think this is worth emphasizing.</a> <a data-t="1221">I think that whatever it is that makes a data visualization trustworthy, it isn't really something you can include in the graph.</a> <a data-t="1228">If you look at stock examples of bad graphs, especially older ones, it's kind of comforting because most of them look really janky.</a> <a data-t="1237">And the same goes for contemporary examples of bad marketing materials or from media outlets trying to spin some ridiculous visual summary of polling data.</a> <a data-t="1247">They don't look trustworthy.</a> <a data-t="1249">It's possible to make janky graphs with ggplot, but you kind of have to work at it.</a> <a data-t="1255">By default, ggplot makes a lot of good choices for you, and this gives it what you might call a kind of aura of plausibility.</a> <a data-t="1263">Whatever data you're showing and whatever story you're telling benefits from a kind of rhetoric of credibility that's built into the software, more or less out of the box.</a></p>

<h3>Trust, reliability, and commitments</h3>

<p><a data-t="1280">So what does that mean to trust a visualization?</a> <a data-t="1284">As you might expect, there are large social scientific and philosophical literatures about trustworthiness.</a> <a data-t="1290">And like in many social scientific literatures, if you read them, you find that empirical studies confirm that trust has many dimensions.</a> <a data-t="1299">It's also really quite hard to measure, and it's very complicated.</a> <a data-t="1303">And like in many philosophical literatures, if you read them, it turns out that trust has many senses, and it's really quite hard to give a unified account of, and it's very complicated.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1314">Ever since the work of Annette Byer, the philosophical literature has made this distinction between trust and what gets called mere reliability.</a> <a data-t="1322">Now, in ordinary speech, trust can mean reliability too, but the idea is that our everyday way of talking is fairly loose, and it runs together something that's worth making a distinction about.</a> <a data-t="1335">And the argument is roughly that there are a lot of things in people that we rely on without actually trusting them.</a> <a data-t="1342">In particular, if they failed, we wouldn't say that we expected an apology from them.</a> <a data-t="1347">We wouldn't feel betrayed.</a> <a data-t="1349">We wouldn't feel that we'd been lied to.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1351">So for example, I might rely on you having a USB cable I can borrow in an emergency if I want to give a presentation, because I know you're the kind of person, I know you're out there, who has a bag full of cables from all kinds of things, and you've always had one before when I've asked to borrow one.</a> <a data-t="1371">But if I needed one and you happened not to have your bag with you one day, I couldn't reasonably say that you had betrayed my trust.</a> <a data-t="1381">Or I might rely on my alarm clock to wake me up, but if it failed one morning, I'd be making a kind of mistake if I thought that it owed me an apology.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1391">Another philosopher, Catherine Hawley, argues that trusting someone to do something means, first of all, believing that they have a commitment to doing a thing, and then relying on them to meet that commitment.</a> <a data-t="1403">A trustworthy person keeps their commitments.</a> <a data-t="1406">And when you mistrust someone, it's because you think they have a commitment to something, but you doubt that they can be relied on to meet it.</a> <a data-t="1415">What's interesting about Hawley's view, amongst other things, is the negative implications of that.</a> <a data-t="1421">Because being trustworthy, for her, is mostly about making sure you don't end up getting stuck with commitments that you can't, won't, or don't fulfill.</a> <a data-t="1432">Being trustworthy doesn't mean taking on as many commitments as you possibly can.</a> <a data-t="1438">Being trustworthy doesn't mean that you have an obligation to be responsive to every single demand made of you.</a> <a data-t="1445">Being trustworthy doesn't mean that you're answerable to literally anyone.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1452">Now, in a talk like this, I can't give these ideas the room they deserve, but they're useful when we think about what we're looking at when we're looking at data and we're showing visualizations.</a> <a data-t="1463">Because even in quite technical discussions, I find that when we're talking about how we should proceed or what should we do, again, even in quite technical discussions, the topic moves very quickly to issues of social relations, of people's roles, and the expectations associated with these roles, what we can reasonably expect.</a> <a data-t="1484">Not so much the technical issues, but sort of the context in which they're embedded and whether we can, you know, what should we expect of others and what are the limits on those expectations?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1497">And this is a general feature of any kind of organized knowledge production.</a> <a data-t="1502">Data visualization is a good case because a graph is such a deceptively simple and portable object.</a> <a data-t="1508">When you make a graph and tell a story with it, all kinds of norms and expectations get compacted down into this single thing, into an image meant to authoritatively convey some structured bit of information to somebody.</a> <a data-t="1521">And when you tell a story with your data, you're implicitly warranting it.</a> <a data-t="1525">You're making some commitments.</a> <a data-t="1527">You're not offering an unconditional warranty.</a> <a data-t="1530">You're not beholden to everyone for all time.</a> <a data-t="1532">But questions of trust do arise.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1534">So when you look at this modern classic of data visualization, for example, what I made, you might ask yourself, can I trust this?</a> <a data-t="1541">Does it look like the person who made this knows what they're doing?</a> <a data-t="1545">And if you spent any time looking at graphs, then you know the answer is absolutely not, which is too bad.</a> <a data-t="1552">I mean, look at these beautiful drop shadows, that finely textured wood grain, the tasteful use of papyrus, the king of off-brand herbal tea fonts.</a> <a data-t="1563">What could be more warmly authentic?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="2638">One last example of the difficulties involved with getting a stream of data, accurately producing a score, and telling a visual story in a trustworthy way.</a> <a data-t="2648">NOAA is one of several international organizations that has collected, managed, and distributed climate data over the years.</a> <a data-t="2656">Thanks to those efforts, we have a number of carefully maintained time series of sea surface temperature measurements, some going back well into the 19th century.</a> <a data-t="2667">Some years ago, there was quite a bit of debate in the scientific literature about how this particular time series didn't quite line up with other data that we had from other sources of measurement.</a> <a data-t="2679">In particular, the sea surface data seemed to indicate a kind of pause or plateau in warming in the post-war period, beginning around 1940, when other indicators did not.</a></p>
