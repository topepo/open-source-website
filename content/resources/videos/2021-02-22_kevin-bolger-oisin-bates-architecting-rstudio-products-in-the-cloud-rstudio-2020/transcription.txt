<h3>Why use the cloud?</h3>

<p><a data-t="701">So you might ask yourself then, why would I want to use the cloud?</a> <a data-t="707">Why not just deploy this on a local environment in an on-premise server?</a> <a data-t="712">So for those who are uninitiated with the cloud, I'll take it slow.</a> <a data-t="715">There are some major benefits to using the cloud.</a> <a data-t="719">One of the most cited use cases is that you get to pay as you go, or rather you only pay for what you need.</a> <a data-t="729">You also lower your total cost of ownership, so you're not paying for massive overheads.</a> <a data-t="737">You take a lot of the guesstimating out of your work, so you can plan scalable infrastructure.</a> <a data-t="743">You can focus more on innovation as opposed to managing and maintaining expensive infrastructure.</a> <a data-t="749">And you can utilize the global networks and the ever-expanding global networks of different cloud providers to make sure your teams across the globe can interact with each other.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="765">Diving a small bit deeper into the pay-as-you-go model, what does it mean?</a> <a data-t="770">Well, you trade in that expensive and rigid on-premise hardware for more highly available cloud infrastructure, only paying for what you use, no more or less.</a> <a data-t="783">And so many providers provide this model, and this means there's a very low or sometimes even no upfront cost.</a> <a data-t="793">So with some service providers, you'll even get a free tier where you can experiment with the different services and see if they meet your needs.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="812">And you can follow this graph here, it kind of trays a very basic model of how cloud costs can compare to very strictly managed on-premise costs.</a> <a data-t="822">So as demand rises for on-premise servers, the engineers will have new servers spin up over time, and then once demand drops, they'll have to drop down that demand and so on and so forth over time.</a> <a data-t="835">So this obviously leaves a massive amount of delta.</a> <a data-t="839">If you overestimate, you're obviously paying for more compute than you need.</a> <a data-t="843">And if you underestimate, sometimes you won't have enough compute power to meet the demand.</a> <a data-t="847">With the cloud, you don't have to worry about that.</a> <a data-t="849">Everything is scalable, everything's automated, and it can always meet the demand as it's needed.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="875">You lower your total cost of ownership, so diving a small bit deeper into this one, you can think of cloud providers as, like I said, being able to benefit from the massive scale of having millions of customers.</a> <a data-t="896">And so you don't need to dedicate specialists to maintain on-premise infrastructure and replace broken parts and faulty parts.</a> <a data-t="906">Cloud providers take care of this completely.</a> <a data-t="909">So all you have to worry about is configuring your infrastructure.</a> <a data-t="913">So cloud runs high volume, low margin business model.</a> <a data-t="916">That means they're passing those savings on to you as well.</a> <a data-t="919">So again, because of that scale, they can just achieve a cost efficiency that is pretty much impossible at a local environment level.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="928">So this drastically reduces the cost of ownership.</a> <a data-t="931">And if you go to the link I have attached here, you'll see a diagram that just compares the costs between on-premise and in cloud.</a> <a data-t="941">And you can think of it like an iceberg where you have the tip of the iceberg that many people see and compare and contrast the two, but with on-premise, there's a lot of stuff hidden beneath that iceberg.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1018">And of course, not having to manage all that infrastructure and not having to pay massive overheads for large compute machines and clusters, it means we can focus more on innovation.</a> <a data-t="1030">And I've taken some inspiration here from a previous cloud presentation I saw.</a> <a data-t="1036">And what we see here is if we look at our on-premise, we have the ability to experiment a lot less frequently.</a> <a data-t="1042">And this is because the kind of clusters that we need to do really advanced experimentation are very expensive, not just to provision, but also to maintain.</a> <a data-t="1052">And we've got to have dedicated engineers monitoring the health status of these.</a> <a data-t="1057">So that means failure is quite expensive if we experiment and result in nothing.</a> <a data-t="1062">That's a lot of cost incurred for that experimentation.</a></p>
