<p><a data-t="0">So I appreciate the bit of history, because this talk is going to start with a story,</a> <a data-t="12">going all the way back to the year 2012.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="83">And I found out a couple years later, an engineer at Stack Overflow, the same company that I was answering these questions on, discovered this question when he was working on improving AAB testing.</a> <a data-t="94">So he ended up tweeting about that answer, and then he followed it shortly with, I don't know how much you're enjoying your PhD, but if you want an interview here, you can have one.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="106">So a couple of weeks later, I interviewed with him, and that was my first data science job at Stack Overflow.</a> <a data-t="113">I later learned the conversation that had gone on internally was the effect of, wow, what if we just hired that guy?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="121">This is what I'd call a freak accident.</a> <a data-t="126">It's not something I would necessarily want to learn too much from.</a> <a data-t="128">I tell the full story in my blog post, One Year as a Data Scientist at Stack Overflow, but it is something that has profoundly affected my philosophy around public work.</a> <a data-t="138">You see, when I was in graduate school, I thought of my goals like this.</a> <a data-t="141">I thought, well, I'm going to start with an idea, and that's just the beginning.</a> <a data-t="145">Then I'm going to start working on it, get some preliminary results.</a> <a data-t="148">Then I'm going to draft a manuscript, and it'll be most of the way there.</a> <a data-t="151">I'll complete the manuscript, and finally, one day, I'll have a valuable published paper.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="157">What I realized is I should have been thinking of my goals a little differently.</a> <a data-t="162">Anything still on your computer is, to a first approximation, useless.</a> <a data-t="166">It's not going to be shared.</a> <a data-t="167">It's not going to be used by anyone else.</a> <a data-t="169">When I look back at grad school, the things I did that ended up not being publicized, things that stayed on my computer, even I've forgotten them.</a> <a data-t="176">If there is anything out in the world, it could be a published paper.</a> <a data-t="180">It could be a product, like a web product.</a> <a data-t="184">It could be a blog source.</a> <a data-t="185">It could be an open source contribution, or it could be as small as a tweet.</a> <a data-t="189">All of those are way more valuable than anything that you didn't share.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="162">Anything still on your computer is, to a first approximation, useless.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="193">Fundamentally, I say it's a talk about public work, but it's really a talk about sharing.</a> <a data-t="199">It's a talk about taking what we have and what we've gotten good at, taking our skills, and then sharing them with other people.</a></p>

<h3>Types of public work and why they matter</h3>

<p><a data-t="211">In particular, I'm going to walk through a few types of sharing that I think are incredibly effective, everywhere from blogging to writing a book.</a> <a data-t="220">I'm going to share my observation that no matter where you are in your career, these are probably helpful, though each of these media probably becomes more helpful at later stages of your career.</a> <a data-t="231">So I'll start at areas where, even if you're an undergraduate student or an aspiring data scientist, you can still get into some of these media, like blogging and tweeting, and if you're someone with a lot of experience and expertise, some other technologies that you could use to share your work.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="250">So I'll give three reasons for spending time in public work, and they're going to keep reappearing throughout this talk.</a> <a data-t="255">The first is to advance your career, really to build a public portfolio of your work and let other people know about your skills.</a> <a data-t="262">It led to a job for me, but it's also led to other opportunities and many other things that wouldn't have happened if I hadn't been sharing my work publicly.</a> <a data-t="271">Certainly this keynote wouldn't have happened if I'd just been working without sharing anything that I did online.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="278">Another is practicing good habits.</a> <a data-t="280">I find that public work builds particular kinds of habits that you wouldn't build if you were, or at least more effective than you build them, working within one company, one team, or just on your own.</a> <a data-t="291">I'll go through a few examples.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="294">And finally, it helps you contribute to the community.</a> <a data-t="297">There's selfless reasons that we want to share public work, to make sure that everything that we build, other people can use.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="307">And a disclaimer.</a> <a data-t="311">One is that, as I've already described in my own job story, a lot of this talk is what worked for me.</a> <a data-t="317">And that means we need an obligatory XKCD about survivorship bias.</a> <a data-t="323">The fact is, we do need to observe that if I talk about just what had been working for me and my particular successes, maybe you don't see people that try that and it didn't work for them.</a> <a data-t="332">I think this is very much worth keeping in mind and maybe taking it with a grain of salt, but I'd also say that I've seen a lot of people who have had a lot of success by sharing their work online.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="343">And throughout this talk, I'm going to share particular examples of other people who, through blogging or tweeting or other types of public work, made a difference for themselves and their community.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="352">The second disclaimer is that this title is called The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Public Work, not The Unreasonable Necessity of Public Work.</a> <a data-t="359">There are other ways to succeed and there are other ways to have a really fulfilling career.</a> <a data-t="363">So, if you're someone who, some people just aren't going to have the time, maybe the ability or the interest in doing public work, but I'd like to convince as many people as possible that it might be interesting for you.</a></p>

<h3>Blogging</h3>

<p><a data-t="381">Why start a blog?</a> <a data-t="382">Well, you can see a couple of reasons.</a> <a data-t="385">One of the most important is building a public portfolio.</a> <a data-t="388">If you're someone early in your career who might start doing job interviews, a blog is not just a great way to get employers to hear about you, it's a way to put your best foot forward.</a> <a data-t="397">You can take whatever kinds of analyses you're most interested in doing, whatever skills you're interested in showcasing, and put them online so that other people can use that to understand what you'd bring to a company.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="410">Another is that blogging is an incredibly effective way to practice writing, visualization, and other kinds of communication.</a> <a data-t="415">In Hadley and Gary Grelin's book, R for Data Science, they talk about the importance of communication within the data science workflow.</a> <a data-t="425">And it can often be hard to practice communication within a typical data science pattern.</a> <a data-t="431">Sometimes it's hard to fit into a usual job.</a> <a data-t="436">And blogging is all about what visualizations can I create, how can I structure my argument, how can I structure these points, and I've found that blogging has been one of the ways I've most improved my own writing skill.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="451">Third is that it's a way to teach or give advice in a way that scales, a way to change the community for the better by thousands of people at a time.</a> <a data-t="460">So I've shared a thought that when you've written the same code three times, write a function.</a> <a data-t="465">When you've given the same in-person advice three times, write a blog post.</a> <a data-t="469">So the reasons for writing a blog post are similar for the reasons we'd write a functioning code, to avoid constant reuse.</a> <a data-t="475">How often do you find yourself in a conversation, maybe at this conference, where you say to someone, oh, I have very strong opinions on that, let me try and walk you through them.</a> <a data-t="483">And you meet someone else, and you walk them through those opinions as well.</a> <a data-t="487">If you have a blog post, that's the opportunity for thousands or tens of thousands of people to read that opinion.</a> <a data-t="492">And any time in the future you might want to change someone's mind, you can point them towards that.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="500">So how do you start a blog?</a> <a data-t="501">There's the extraordinary </a><a href="/software/blogdown/">blogdown</a><a data-t="501"> package by Yihui Hsieh, as well as Amber Thomas and Allison Hill, who wrote the blogdown book, about creating websites with R Markdown.</a> <a data-t="512">So if you've used R Markdown for creating reports, you can now use one R Markdown file for each post in a blog.</a> <a data-t="520">There's a really fantastic guide towards starting your own, and it's one that lets you integrate R, turn from R directly to code, results, and figures in your blog posts.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="531">I give a lot more advice in terms of how you can start a blog in Naturally, my blog post, advice to aspiring data scientists, start a blog, which I'm now pointing all of you towards and thereby proving my point.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="550">One of the points that I make in this blog post is that it can be very difficult to, it can feel like you're screaming into the void when you first publish online if you don't already have people that are following you.</a> <a data-t="557">So my recommendation is if you start a data-related blog, tweet me a link, and I'll tweet about your first post.</a> <a data-t="563">In the year plus since I made that offer, I've had the real privilege to get to retweet and tweet about a lot of people's work, a lot of people from all over the spectrum of a data science career.</a> <a data-t="574">I'll share a couple examples of those in a moment.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="578">So if you're wondering what could I blog about, the place I'd always start is analyzing a data set.</a> <a data-t="583">I think this is the bread and butter of data science blogging, is I've opened, I've created a new post, I'm going to start with a data set, I'm going to share some things that I learned.</a> <a data-t="592">And for that, I think one of the most remarkable projects that enables that is TidyTuesday, run by the RF for Data Science online learning community.</a> <a data-t="601">So this, each week, TidyTuesday releases a new data set every Monday, and then on Tuesday, the community generally discusses it, shares the results.</a> <a data-t="612">So a fantastic approach to starting a blog is go to TidyTuesday, find the latest data set, or even an older one that you're interested in, share what you can learn from it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="621">I think there's a lot of data sets you can use, though, and I want to highlight an example of someone who did it to remarkable effect.</a> <a data-t="627">One of the people who shared their blog post with me was Jeff Kao, who was at the time an aspiring data scientist at the Metis boot camp.</a> <a data-t="636">And he wrote an article discovering that more than a million net neutrality comments were likely faked.</a> <a data-t="642">He used natural language processing to discover enough repetition in these comments to show they were created by bots.</a> <a data-t="649">And that actually got national news coverage and a lot of really notable attention.</a> <a data-t="656">So one good place for looking for analyzing data might be current events that you're interested in and have a data-driven perspective to add.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="665">Another really good place to look for blog entries is if you're an academic, for every paper, turning it into a blog post.</a> <a data-t="672">This is great advice from Philip Guo, where he shares that everything after his Ph.D., he's taken each of the papers, turned it into a blog post, and encourages students to do the same, because 10 to 100 more people will read blog posts than read a published paper.</a> <a data-t="689">So peer-reviewed papers are a very important part of the public work ecosystem, but they're very well complemented by blog posts.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="700">Another great thing to do in a blog post is to teach a concept.</a> <a data-t="704">So Mohamed was an aspiring data scientist who shared this post with me, and it was a really excellent look through some topics that he'd been learning about performance metrics for machine learning.</a> <a data-t="715">So as he was learning these concepts, as he was taking classes and mastering these skills, he was also publishing this work online.</a> <a data-t="724">When I talk to people that are taking data camp courses and are learning to become data scientists and they're asking me what's next, that's almost always my advice, to try creating blog posts where they share what they've learned.</a></p>

<h3>Contributing to open source</h3>

<p><a data-t="982">So move on to the next area.</a> <a data-t="983">This is something that once you've gotten a little bit of comfort, at least you have some comfort programming in R, you certainly don't have to be an expert.</a> <a data-t="991">But once you're ready, an amazing point is to contribute to open source.</a> <a data-t="997">So this is a slide from Mara Averick's talk, Contributing to </a><a href="/software/tidyverse/">Tidyverse</a><a data-t="997"> Packages, that points out the motivation for open source is that it helps both yourself and the larger community.</a> <a data-t="1007">So when you find there's something you're working on, that's an amazing time, that you generally find useful, that's an amazing time to turn it open source.</a> <a data-t="1016">And the reason it's so helpful is that when you contribute to the community, you'll find the community contributes back.</a></p>

<h3>Recording screencasts</h3>

<p><a data-t="1403">This next media of public work is one that I only became familiar with and started really thinking about in the last few months, but it's one that I'm really excited about, particularly recording screencasts.</a> <a data-t="1411">This is an example of a screencast where I've been taking tidy Tuesday data sets each week and opening them without ever having looked at the data before, going into them, exploring it, making graphs, trying statistical models, thinking about my conclusions for about an hour without ever having seen the data before.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1433">So I found this a really exciting way to teach and to generally narrate my thought process and share it, particularly because it takes minimal preparation and a fixed time window.</a> <a data-t="1445">So if I'm at a point in my career where it can be difficult to schedule a lot of time to write, but I can always schedule an hour and a half to set up and record a screencast.</a></p>

<h3>Writing a book</h3>

<p><a data-t="1579">Finally, I shared this advice before, when you've written the same code three times, write a function.</a> <a data-t="1584">When you've written the same in-person advice three times, write a blog post.</a> <a data-t="1589">Or, as Hadley advises, try writing a book.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1595">So writing a book is generally something that you'd want to be more advanced in a career.</a> <a data-t="1600">Who here has written a book?</a> <a data-t="1601">E-books count as long as you feel like they're finished.</a> <a data-t="1603">Who's written a book?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1619">Who thinks they plan to write a book sometime in their career?</a> <a data-t="1619">I'd encourage everyone to think about it seriously, because writing a book can seem somewhat intimidating, but it really fits as part of a continuum with a lot of these other media of communication.</a> <a data-t="1632">I think to write a book, the two things you need are a good amount to say, so you need to be a little farther in your career, enough to have a lot of advice you'd want to give on a technical or professional topic, and you'd have to have some practice in saying it.</a> <a data-t="1645">And that second part, the practice in communicating, is what all of this public work has been about.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1652">So we talked about short-form communication, like a tweet.</a> <a data-t="1655">So in a tweet, I might be able to do, this is a small bit of R code that's designed to demonstrate a concept of updating a beta distribution, and it's enough to fit in a tweet.</a> <a data-t="1666">And then there's medium-form communication, and this can be everything from blog posts to package documentation, like vignettes, and building more practice doing this kind of writing helps one to get to long-form documentation.</a> <a data-t="1677">So in this case, I took a series of blog posts that I'd written, each teaching concepts around empirical Bayes estimation, and turned them into an ebook, Introduction to Empirical Bayes.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1756">How do you write a book?</a> <a data-t="1757">Well, luckily, Yihui is on the case, because there's the </a><a href="/software/bookdown/">Bookdown</a><a data-t="1757"> package.</a> <a data-t="1761">So just as Blogdown allowed each RMD file to be one blog post, Bookdown has one RMD for each chapter.</a> <a data-t="1768">And Bookdown handles the enormous amount of formatting headaches and the nitty-gritty of references and indexes and all these problems, and it handles it for you so you can focus on the content and write something awesome.</a> <a data-t="1782">I find when I'm working with Bookdown, it feels more like I'm writing a sequence of blog posts than as if I have an enormous book that I have to contend with.</a> <a data-t="1794">In fact, all the books that I've described during this talk, all were developed in Bookdown.</a></p>
