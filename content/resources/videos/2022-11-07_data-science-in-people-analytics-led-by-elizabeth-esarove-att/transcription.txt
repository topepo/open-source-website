<h3>Analyzing employee survey comments</h3>

<p><a data-t="1509">So our last area where we found data science to be very helpful is analyzing employee survey comments.</a> <a data-t="1517">And I mentioned in our surveys, we sometimes ask employees to give us feedback about AT&T where they're allowed to type anything that they would like to say.</a> <a data-t="1529">And when of course you're looking at thousands of comments, it's hard to summarize what are people talking about.</a> <a data-t="1537">So we found topic modeling to be very helpful in this area.</a> <a data-t="1541">The modeling approach that we use for those of you that are familiar with text analytics is latent Dirichlet allocation.</a> <a data-t="1549">And this allows us without going into the math and a lot of detail, this allows us to group together certain comments based on the keywords that employees use in those comments.</a> <a data-t="1561">We take out some of the common words like the and of, because every comment is going to have those words in them.</a> <a data-t="1569">And that then reduces down the comments to the unique words that exist in each of them.</a> <a data-t="1575">From there, we can then summarize those topics.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1579">So here's an example of how topic modeling may work.</a> <a data-t="1583">And this is just based on industry data because we figure most companies are going to see comments about these topics.</a> <a data-t="1591">So you can see here on the left, I've got two lists of words here.</a> <a data-t="1595">And these are examples of comments, words that are coming from various comments from employees.</a> <a data-t="1601">And you can see there's two entirely different lists.</a> <a data-t="1604">Advancement, opportunity, career, promotion is entirely different from pay, retirement, salary, raises, insurance, and so forth.</a> <a data-t="1613">So from there, we can then decide our topic for the items on the left are more focused on career growth.</a> <a data-t="1621">And these items on the right are focused more on pay and benefits related comments.</a> <a data-t="1627">From there, we can continue this process to label all of our different topics that show up in the comments.</a> <a data-t="1634">And that then allows us to prepare some summaries for executives to let them know which areas are people commenting on the most, what are the biggest concerns of our employees.</a> <a data-t="1645">And from there, they can decide what actions they want to take to try and change things at AT&T based on the feedback that we've received.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1668">Another area in text analytics that we found very helpful is we often get questions from management regarding the sentiment of those topics or the comments in general.</a> <a data-t="1680">Are they positive or negative?</a> <a data-t="1681">So in that case, we've been using lexicon-based algorithms.</a> <a data-t="1685">There are two general purpose lexicons available in both R and Python.</a> <a data-t="1691">One is AFIN and BING, and these are developed by researchers who have examined a lot of comments and determined that certain words are representing a positive or negative sentiment in those words or in those comments.</a> <a data-t="1709">However, for people analytics cases where employees are commenting about their jobs or awards or benefits, we found it helpful to customize these lexicons to give us a better understanding of the sentiment expressed in work-related comments or job-related comments.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1724">So we've removed some words from the standard lexicons, and those words are listed here on the left.</a> <a data-t="1730">And you can see things like pay and work.</a> <a data-t="1733">They're more general topics of comments about pay and work, but what we really want to know is what is their sentiment about pay and work, for example.</a> <a data-t="1743">So we found it's easier to remove those words, and then we can get a better, more accurate understanding of the sentiment of their comments.</a> <a data-t="1752">And then you may find, looking at the comments in general, you may find there's words that should be added, maybe even some that are unique to your company or your industry, that would help give you an even better understanding of sentiment.</a> <a data-t="1768">And some words that don't exist in the general purpose lexicons, like burnout.</a> <a data-t="1773">We're seeing a lot of articles about employees feeling burnout these days.</a> <a data-t="1777">Micromanage, which seems to exist in almost any company.</a> <a data-t="1779">These are things that you may consider adding to your custom sentiment lexicon to help give you a better understanding of your sentiment in these employee-based comments.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1787">What we do overall in sentiment analysis is then from that, once you've customized your lexicon, we apply that to the comments and try and measure the number of positive words and the number of negative words that appear in each comment.</a> <a data-t="1804">And we take the positive words minus the negative words and give us a general comment score so that we can see, overall, is that comment mostly positive or is it mostly negative?</a> <a data-t="1817">And from there, you can give management an understanding of, are the comments about a specific topic more positive or negative?</a> <a data-t="1825">Or even just overall, among all of the comments that employees make, are they mostly positive or negative?</a></p>
