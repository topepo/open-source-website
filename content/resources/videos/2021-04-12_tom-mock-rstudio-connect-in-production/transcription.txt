<h3>What is RStudio Connect?</h3>

<p><a data-t="105">So again, RStudio Connect, kind of what it is, is a content hub and execution engine for all your data products.</a> <a data-t="112">So this could be things like web apps, machine learning models as RESTful APIs, dashboards, reports, automatic emails or custom emails, tables and visualizations.</a> <a data-t="124">Basically, anything you're already creating in R, it makes it where you can take those insights and leverage them a little bit faster.</a> <a data-t="132">So you can share it widely, share it securely, just get it off your local environment and put it out there for your organization to actually use.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="144">And importantly, RStudio Connect is fully interoperable.</a> <a data-t="147">So it will play with any of the data products you can make in R.</a> <a data-t="152">And additionally, it can communicate with things like R and Python or R and Spark, JavaScript, </a><a href="/software/tensorflow/">TensorFlow</a><a data-t="152">, SQL.</a> <a data-t="158">All these things can be integrated into the data products you're creating.</a> <a data-t="162">So you have a nice platform to push out all of these things that you're making.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="168">The basic idea of what it would look like in your environment, and this is just a very simple example, is you create something locally, maybe on your desktop in RStudio IDE or RStudio Server or RStudio Server Pro.</a> <a data-t="178">You then have push button deployment to push whatever you create, say a </a><a href="/software/shiny-r/">Shiny</a><a data-t="178"> application or R Markdown document on to Connect.</a> <a data-t="185">Connect will then host it.</a> <a data-t="187">It will provide all the scaling and security you need.</a> <a data-t="189">It can connect to your file share system.</a> <a data-t="192">It can connect to your databases.</a> <a data-t="194">And ultimately, your end users will just access that content via their own web browser.</a> <a data-t="200">So they really just kind of see it as a website.</a> <a data-t="203">But in reality, it's a hosted on premise solution for your group.</a></p>

<h3>What does production mean?</h3>

<p><a data-t="258">So what does production mean to me?</a> <a data-t="263">I'll steal a quote from Joe Chang that he gave at the most recent RStudio Conf.</a> <a data-t="268">Production means a software environment that is used and then relied upon by real users with real consequences if things go wrong.</a> <a data-t="278">So specifically, you know, this doesn't talk about how big the environment is.</a> <a data-t="283">It doesn't talk about how widely it's used, just something the business relies upon, something the business is using to either make decisions or to gain insight about some data science product.</a> <a data-t="295">That's really the ultimate goal of data science is to inform a business decision to provide value of some type for the organization or for the individual.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="268">Production means a software environment that is used and then relied upon by real users with real consequences if things go wrong.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="299">So beyond kind of that basic idea, in my mind, there's some other production needs.</a> <a data-t="303">So things like scaling and execution, you know, you want to have it be able to work for your entire organization if you wanted to, as opposed to just locally on your own laptop.</a> <a data-t="311">You want to have security and access control.</a> <a data-t="315">So you don't want to just put it out there for everyone on the Internet to access.</a> <a data-t="319">And even within your own group, maybe you want to have only execs see these specific materials or only your data science groups see other material.</a> <a data-t="328">You also want to have logging and visibility into the applications you've already pushed out there or the things you're using in production, as well as logging about the performance or when they've been updated or changes that have been made.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="344">And as Garrett covered in the last webinar, reproducibility is also a huge component.</a> <a data-t="349">And with a code-based solution like R, you're really focusing on being able to reproduce both that environment as well as the actual code that you've used to create your asset.</a> <a data-t="360">And lastly, something I think is really important is the sharing and collaboration idea.</a> <a data-t="365">So sharing in terms of you're creating something you worked and spent a lot of time on creating a model or evaluating a model, making some type of prediction, you know, doing some core data science tasks.</a> <a data-t="376">And then ultimately, you have to inform somebody or share that with somebody and have a useful discussion with them.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="409">Ultimately, the kind of the idea here is production doesn't have to be as scary as you want it as it kind of always comes out to be.</a> <a data-t="419">So, yes, there are some environments that are extremely locked down, extremely complex.</a> <a data-t="423">They're serving thousands and thousands of users.</a> <a data-t="427">And, you know, just a lot goes into it from a DevOps standpoint.</a> <a data-t="431">There's other people who really just want to take some type of R based product and put it out there for five to ten people.</a> <a data-t="438">And the kind of the concept here is that I have customers on both ends of the spectrum, you know, very small groups serving other small groups and other people who are serving, you know, hundreds or thousands of individuals within their organization.</a></p>

<h3>What Connect provides</h3>

<p><a data-t="453">So in terms of what Connect provides in this concept is kind of hitting on the same points that I said before.</a> <a data-t="459">So scaling, execution, high availability.</a> <a data-t="462">Basically, you can take your Shiny application, your R Markdown and scale it out to your organization.</a> <a data-t="469">Additionally, because Connect can actually execute R code, it can actually rerun your R scripts or R Markdown documents or your Shiny applications for new people.</a> <a data-t="480">As far as the high availability concept, you can have multiple Connect servers kind of in a cluster.</a> <a data-t="486">So if part of your server were to go down, you have another one ready to go.</a> <a data-t="490">Or if you just want to share the load across multiple servers, that's also possible.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="496">Additionally, you have full security access control and visibility.</a> <a data-t="500">So you can bring your own LDAP, Active Directory, SAML security to authenticate users.</a> <a data-t="507">You have access control.</a> <a data-t="509">So at the application or server level, you can specify who can see what as well as have applications that are only for testing or only for production use.</a> <a data-t="521">And lastly, you have full visibility in terms of you can see what's been published there and you can kind of understand what's being used in your organization.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="527">Additionally, from the reproducibility and logging standpoint, I'll cover this a little bit later, but you have these kind of sandbox environments on Connect where you publish something, it tracks the R environment, it tracks all the different packages that are being used.</a> <a data-t="551">So you have this kind of metadata about the assets you're creating.</a> <a data-t="555">You also have logging about both use of it and updates to those assets.</a> <a data-t="561">And lastly, you have full sharing and collaboration.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="567">So yes, you can now take an R Markdown document and just publish the finished document out onto Connect and then people can access it via web browser, which is a huge win for a lot of people.</a> <a data-t="581">But additionally, it opens up as an execution engine where those people who may not know how to code can actually re-execute those R Markdown documents or Shiny applications to create something they want to see without ever having to go and bother the data scientist directly.</a> <a data-t="601">And lastly, you have the collaboration and discoverability.</a> <a data-t="606">You can actually search around on Connect and find things of interest.</a> <a data-t="610">Ultimately, Connect's getting your data products off your laptop and ready for consumption in your environment.</a></p>

<h3>Security and reproducibility</h3>

<p><a data-t="617">Again, when I talk about security, you have kind of three major levels of security that I like to focus on.</a> <a data-t="621">So your server level, just authenticated users accessing your content so you can bring your own kind of security.</a> <a data-t="626">A lot of people are familiar with Active Directory, LDAP, SAML.</a> <a data-t="631">You also have app level so you can restrict user access to your applications, whether they're Shiny or Markdown.</a> <a data-t="637">And lastly, you can even have data level access to specific things even within an application.</a> <a data-t="643">So all these different tiers of security and access that you like to see in your production environment.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="648">As far as the reproducibility, I hit on this a little bit earlier, but the big idea here is that you really don't want to create something, put it out there, and then all of a sudden not have governance or understanding of how you created it.</a> <a data-t="664">So you don't want to lose the code.</a> <a data-t="666">You don't want to lose the information about which package version you were on or which version of R you were on or what were even the system dependencies that you were using there.</a> <a data-t="680">So importantly, the way that Connect works is you create an asset locally.</a> <a data-t="686">So maybe it's on a server through RStudio Server or on the desktop IDE.</a> <a data-t="691">So you write an application, you create an R Markdown document, whatever you're doing, you actually bundle that together when you click publish.</a> <a data-t="701">So this is all done automatically.</a> <a data-t="703">You just click publish, Connect will bundle all that information together.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="707">So you have the source code, you have the metadata around the specific dependencies that's pushed out to Connect.</a> <a data-t="712">Connect then unpacks the bundle with this </a><a href="/software/packrat/">Packrat</a><a data-t="712"> bundle and it actually reinstalls the specific dependencies at the exact package version you're using.</a> <a data-t="722">So the same version of R, the same version of the exact packages you're using.</a> <a data-t="728">So you have this kind of sandboxed environment of exactly what you want it to be.</a> <a data-t="733">Importantly, each data product has its own R environment.</a> <a data-t="736">So this prevents breaking existing deployed content.</a> <a data-t="740">You can have multiple versions of R basically running across your different assets, multiple different package versions running across your different assets if you wanted to.</a></p>

<h3>Deployment options</h3>

<p><a data-t="756">So when people talk about pushing to prod, the joke is always don't push anything out on Friday afternoon.</a> <a data-t="762">You don't want to just put something out there into the world and then disappear for the weekend.</a> <a data-t="767">So people will think very differently about how they're publishing or how they're deploying assets into their production environment.</a> <a data-t="773">For a group just starting out, you know, they're ecstatic and super excited about just doing push button deployment, you know, just being able to create something locally and basically in a few seconds, publish it out and have it ready to go and shared across your entire group.</a> <a data-t="793">Other groups are very much more about the governance and the DevOps side where they want to have kind of man in the middle or woman in the middle, just someone there who is making a decision about is this ready to go to productions.</a> <a data-t="804">Maybe that means code review or putting it into Git or continuous integration.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="812">The first option would be just push button deployment, which is the default.</a> <a data-t="816">So within the IDE, you can actually click this little blue button and it will publish things on to Connect.</a> <a data-t="820">At that point, you could still restrict access.</a> <a data-t="823">So maybe you want to push it out, play around with it on Connect and only you can access it at this point.</a> <a data-t="829">And then after the fact, you could say, OK, well, this is running well.</a> <a data-t="833">I'll allow access to the rest of our audience and kind of open it up to the rest of the group to kind of use the data product.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="840">Alternatively, you could put things out through Git.</a> <a data-t="843">So you could have a Git back deployment where you're not going to push button deployment anymore, but rather you're actually pushing something out in a version control through Git and then Connect is kind of calling out to Git and installing it directly from there.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="871">On the alternative side, rather than having kind of this one step process, you could have a testing and then a production environment or what we like to call kind of a staging and then a production environment.</a> <a data-t="883">So in this case, you actually publish your asset initially into kind of a sandbox staging or testing environment.</a> <a data-t="889">So a different version of Connect and only your kind of DevOps or data science team is accessing at this point or just dogfooding it, testing it, making sure things are rock solid.</a> <a data-t="898">Once it's gone through that stage, you can then redeploy it onto your production environment and let the rest of your consumers use it now that you know that it's working properly.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="910">And lastly, another one would be continuous integration.</a> <a data-t="914">So still doing your sandboxing, your testing approval, and then the request is actually sent to merge into your version control.</a> <a data-t="921">It goes through code review.</a> <a data-t="923">It's pushed out to the master branch and then a continuous integration server will actually publish this production itself.</a> <a data-t="930">So something like Jenkins will go out there, pull the code that's been updated and then republish it onto Connect.</a></p>
