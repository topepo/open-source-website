<h3>The visualization process</h3>

<p><a data-t="161">All in all, I've been really lucky to be a part of these and other data</a> <a data-t="165">journalistic projects during my time at the ACLU.</a> <a data-t="165">So I wanted to talk to you a little</a> <a data-t="170">bit about the process that I often go through and the key steps I take in creating my data</a> <a data-t="176">visualizations.</a> <a data-t="176">This will include the feedback that I sometimes receive, what I've done or</a> <a data-t="181">not done with that feedback, and other considerations that I think are important in getting to a</a> <a data-t="188">final result.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="188">So some of you might be thinking that it's just this easy.</a> <a data-t="193">You go from data</a> <a data-t="193">analysis to visualization, you're good, you have a great viz, and you can go on and just</a> <a data-t="199">enjoy your weekend.</a> <a data-t="199">Maybe even I thought that at one point, based on this flack evidence,</a> <a data-t="204">when I was analyzing data a few months into the pandemic and came to the conclusion that</a> <a data-t="210">actually letting incarcerated people out of jails had no effect on crime levels nearby.</a> <a data-t="218">Spoiler, this is not the process, but it was the finding.</a> <a data-t="218">The actual process in my experience</a> <a data-t="223">can be really messy and definitely not linear.</a> <a data-t="223">But I'll share with you the key elements and</a> <a data-t="228">considerations to take that I believe have set me up for a success for projects like</a> <a data-t="233">this.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="235">First, you'll get some data.</a> <a data-t="235">A lot of the time for us here, that means that a lawyer</a> <a data-t="240">will have some kind of data that they've obtained through the discovery process or through a</a> <a data-t="245">FOIA request, and a lot of times we'll just use publicly available data.</a> <a data-t="251">Next, you'll</a> <a data-t="251">use R to read and explore that data.</a> <a data-t="252">During the exploration process, I use the </a><a href="/software/tidyverse/">tidyverse</a> <a data-t="257">quite a bit, but also the janitor package is one of my favorites to make easy tables,</a> <a data-t="262">and always </a><a href="/software/ggplot2/">ggplot2</a><a data-t="262"> to find quick trends and actually start visualizing my findings.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="265">So this is important.</a> <a data-t="265">By now, you'll want to start thinking about what are your main</a> <a data-t="269">points that you found in your exploration that you want to visualize and make clear.</a> <a data-t="274">Ask yourself, does my visualization support and highlight my main point?</a> <a data-t="280">You'll also want</a> <a data-t="280">to carefully consider your audience.</a> <a data-t="285">Is your audience made of purely academics?</a> <a data-t="285">Are they</a> <a data-t="289">advocates?</a> <a data-t="289">Are they people who know a lot about your topic already, or maybe they know</a> <a data-t="294">nothing at all?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="296">It's also good to ask for feedback on your visualizations from various</a> <a data-t="301">people.</a> <a data-t="301">You'll want to be specific about the main points that you're trying to visualize</a> <a data-t="306">before receiving feedback about whether those main points are coming through clearly or not to those people.</a> <a data-t="314">And then you might find that once you receive feedback, you'll start iterating on your</a> <a data-t="318">database, which means you'll likely need to go kind of back and forth and check a lot</a> <a data-t="322">of these boxes again.</a> <a data-t="326">The process could end up looking a lot like this.</a></p>

<h3>A real-life example: COVID-19 and jail populations</h3>

<p><a data-t="326">Now, to show you</a> <a data-t="330">how I've gone about using this process in practice, I'll go through these steps once</a> <a data-t="335">more with a real-life example.</a> <a data-t="338">For the purposes of the example that I'll use, I'll need to</a> <a data-t="342">start with a little bit of background on this recent project that our team worked on.</a> <a data-t="342">Sometime</a> <a data-t="347">into the very beginning of the COVID-19 pandemic, it became really clear that people in jails</a> <a data-t="353">and people that were incarcerated in America, which, by the way, has the highest incarceration</a> <a data-t="359">rate in the world in particular, would be at risk.</a> <a data-t="359">Incarcerated people are subject to</a> <a data-t="365">extreme overcrowding, and jails often lack the resources and facilities that allow for</a> <a data-t="371">handwashing and other hygienic practices that are necessary to slow the spread of COVID-19.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="371">So it was really clear that in order to save lives, action had to be taken, and it had</a> <a data-t="376">to be taken quickly.</a> <a data-t="381">Our team partnered with academic researchers to build a model that</a> <a data-t="381">accounted for the dynamics of the jail system and estimated the additional illnesses and</a> <a data-t="387">loss of life that we'd expect if none of these preventative measures were taken.</a> <a data-t="392">We</a> <a data-t="392">recommended reducing arrests such that people were only being arrested for the most serious</a> <a data-t="398">incidents and promptly releasing people who were pre-trial or charged for low-level crimes</a> <a data-t="404">or were particularly vulnerable to the disease and posed no risk of immediate threat to others.</a> <a data-t="411">All in all, the team estimated that taking these precautions could save 100,000 human</a> <a data-t="416">lives, both inside the jails and in their surrounding communities.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="421">Many communities</a> <a data-t="421">did begin prioritizing the lives of incarcerated people and the people who worked within jails.</a> <a data-t="426">Some localities reduced low-level arrests, or set bond at zero, or released a subset</a> <a data-t="431">of incarcerated people.</a> <a data-t="431">But there was some backlash.</a> <a data-t="436">Some people were worried that taking</a> <a data-t="436">these actions would result in a huge spike in crime in the surrounding communities, despite</a> <a data-t="441">research showing that in New York City, for instance, people released from jails due to</a> <a data-t="445">COVID were rearrested at lower rates than even expected.</a> <a data-t="452">To quell these concerns further,</a> <a data-t="452">our team analyzed trends in reported crimes during the initial three months of the pandemic</a> <a data-t="456">in 29 of the largest US cities where data was available and released this report with</a> <a data-t="462">our findings.</a> <a data-t="467">For the purposes of this project, our goal was to find whether there was any</a> <a data-t="467">relationship between the change in county jail populations and the reported crime rate</a> <a data-t="473">in the closest metropolitan area.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="481">So, back to the process, starting with the data step.</a> <a data-t="481">We needed two categories of data, one for county jail populations and one that showed</a> <a data-t="485">the number of crimes reported in the nearest city.</a> <a data-t="490">These were ultimately the 29 locations</a> <a data-t="490">for which I could find quality reported crime data for, for the time periods necessary that</a> <a data-t="496">were either aggregated daily, weekly, or monthly for all of 2019 through May 2020 that we could</a> <a data-t="502">also find jail population information for.</a> <a data-t="508">So, we needed both.</a> <a data-t="508">We collected this data</a> <a data-t="508">a bit in piecemeal, mainly through Googling for specific county sheriff's offices or finding</a> <a data-t="512">public open data sets.</a> <a data-t="518">We ended up downloading countless CSVs and Excel files and even PDFs</a> <a data-t="518">kind of continuously whenever we noticed a bit, a new bit of data had dropped.</a> <a data-t="524">Hence,</a> <a data-t="524">the circular arrow around the data step.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="530">We also used the Vera Institute's data that</a> <a data-t="530">tracked the number of people in each county jail over time and we ended up using the specific</a> <a data-t="535">dates of February 29th and April 30th to calculate the percent change in jail population.</a> <a data-t="542">Because</a> <a data-t="542">most jails took action early on in the pandemic, we looked at the crime rates during the time</a> <a data-t="546">period of March through May 2019 and compared those to the same time period in 2020.</a> <a data-t="553">And</a> <a data-t="553">then to sort of standardize the way that we analyzed crime, and also because many of</a> <a data-t="558">these cities' crime data sets only included these types of crimes, we focused on part</a> <a data-t="562">one crimes, which are defined by the Uniform Crime Reporting Program, and they encompass</a> <a data-t="567">the most frequently reported violent and property crimes.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="573">You'll see that data exploration here</a> <a data-t="573">was super messy for me.</a> <a data-t="579">This is an example of what I saw using this function for my very</a> <a data-t="584">favorite janitor package on the crime description column in just one city.</a> <a data-t="589">During my exploration,</a> <a data-t="589">I created this plot in R, which showed the relationship between the magnitude of the</a> <a data-t="594">change in jail population and the magnitude of the change in crime during that same time</a> <a data-t="600">frame.</a> <a data-t="605">Now, it could be really tempting to draw a regression line and to try and focus</a> <a data-t="605">on some type of significant correlation that just isn't there.</a> <a data-t="611">I received feedback from</a> <a data-t="611">my colleagues that this graph focused a little bit too much on sort of the outliers, which</a> <a data-t="615">were one location that increased their jail population, and one location where there was</a> <a data-t="620">more reported crime than in the previous year.</a> <a data-t="625">It was then I realized that one of the most</a> <a data-t="625">important main points that I could make was actually the lack of relationship between</a> <a data-t="629">X and Y.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="636">So after lots of data cleaning and trying out more charts and receiving more</a> <a data-t="641">feedback, these were the three main points that I wanted to make clear in my report and</a> <a data-t="647">visualize to my audience.</a> <a data-t="647">One, crime was not on the rise compared to the same time period</a> <a data-t="654">the year prior.</a> <a data-t="654">Crime rates were instead right within normal range or even lower.</a> <a data-t="660">Second,</a> <a data-t="660">most of the locales that we found data for did decrease their jail populations during</a> <a data-t="665">this time period.</a> <a data-t="665">And like I said before, just one location actually increased its population.</a> <a data-t="670">Lastly, and importantly, we found no relationship between the county jail population and its</a> <a data-t="675">increase or decrease and the number of crimes that were reported.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="676">So once I decided on these</a> <a data-t="680">main points, it was time to come up with a few mock-ups of graphs that I thought would</a> <a data-t="685">clearly express these.</a> <a data-t="685">Of course, though, this is not always easy, especially when you</a> <a data-t="691">realize that you need to take your audience into consideration.</a> <a data-t="691">So unfortunately, this</a> <a data-t="696">is an example of a time where I thought I nailed it, I'd come to the perfect solution,</a> <a data-t="701">but I hadn't yet considered our audience.</a> <a data-t="701">So this is a graph that shows the 29 locations</a> <a data-t="707">in order of the percent change in jail population.</a> <a data-t="707">The gray bar indicates the range of monthly</a> <a data-t="711">crimes between January 2018 and February 2020, and the three dots indicate the number</a> <a data-t="717">of monthly crimes reported in March, April, and May of 2020.</a> <a data-t="717">You'll see that a lot of</a> <a data-t="721">the time.</a> <a data-t="721">The three dots are to the left of the gray bar, but never are they to the right</a> <a data-t="727">of the gray bar, which indicates that reported crime during these first few months of the</a> <a data-t="732">pandemic were within normal or expected range or lower.</a></p>
