<h3>From learning to applying</h3>

<p><a data-t="209">So how do we jump over this barrier, restoring a belief that we can achieve those lofty goals?</a> <a data-t="217">When I accepted my current position to develop and implement a training program in reproducible</a> <a data-t="222">computational methods two and a half years ago, I thought the most important part of</a> <a data-t="228">my job would be time spent in a classroom.</a> <a data-t="232">I had spent the previous four years teaching computational skills to students at the university</a> <a data-t="237">level, and after an entire career in academia, I thought that the highest impact I could</a> <a data-t="243">have at a research center would involve teaching formally structured short courses.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="250">During my career, I've had the privilege of personally teaching hundreds of people to</a> <a data-t="255">code in R.</a> <a data-t="256">Especially in my last few years focusing on adult learners outside of formal</a> <a data-t="261">university courses, I've learned a lot about what it takes to help people succeed in learning</a> <a data-t="266">R, and that success is not solely reliant on formal short courses.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="273">Now I define success as whether people continue to work with R after leaving my classroom,</a> <a data-t="280">or at least if they gain some general literacy in data and computing skills so they can work</a> <a data-t="285">more effectively with computational staff.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="273">Now I define success as whether people continue to work with R after leaving my classroom,</a> <a data-t="280">or at least if they gain some general literacy in data and computing skills so they can work</a> <a data-t="285">more effectively with computational staff.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="289">Given the breadth of research topics members of my community pursue, I am faced with the</a> <a data-t="294">monumental task of helping people apply R skills to many different types of data and</a> <a data-t="300">research questions.</a> <a data-t="302">The question I then continually ask myself as I prepare to deliver training materials</a> <a data-t="308">is this, what prevents people from using these tools after they've learned them?</a></p>

<h3>Levels of expertise</h3>

<p><a data-t="315">Let's take a quick tour of the process of gaining expertise and think about what this</a> <a data-t="319">looks like with coding.</a> <a data-t="323">Regardless of the prior education someone possesses in a different field, I consider</a> <a data-t="328">a person who has no prior experience learning to code in R a novice.</a> <a data-t="333">In my community, that means they probably don't have any experience coding in a different</a> <a data-t="338">language and probably also don't know much about how to structure and store their data</a> <a data-t="343">effectively.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="345">I integrate general concepts in coding and data management into my intro R course and</a> <a data-t="350">focus on directly applicable skills like those I showed in code earlier.</a> <a data-t="355">By the time someone has completed my introductory R class, I like to think that they're no</a> <a data-t="360">longer a novice, since they have now transitioned to competent practitioner.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="366">I define a competent practitioner as someone who applies R code to their own research problems.</a> <a data-t="372">A practitioner probably doesn't know if the way that they code represents the most elegant</a> <a data-t="377">or efficient solution to a problem, but they get the job done.</a> <a data-t="383">I consider expert-level coders to be people who are writing code that make it easy for</a> <a data-t="388">other people to use it.</a> <a data-t="390">They encapsulate their projects in fully documented R packages and try to apply best practices</a> <a data-t="397">in coding to make it robust and reproducible.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="402">To be clear, I'm less concerned about formally categorizing people into these groups.</a> <a data-t="408">I think more about how to help people follow the arrows to the right.</a> <a data-t="412">In practice, most people I encounter are practitioners of some type, most often interested in getting</a> <a data-t="419">the immediate job done.</a> <a data-t="421">Regardless, this model is a really useful way to think about a few key questions.</a> <a data-t="426">What do people need to learn?</a> <a data-t="429">And how are they most likely to learn these things?</a></p>

<h3>Meet Ash, Avery, and Quinn</h3>

<p><a data-t="433">We're going to investigate these questions by visiting a few colleagues of mine who generally</a> <a data-t="437">represent each of these categories.</a> <a data-t="441">Please meet Ash.</a> <a data-t="443">Ash is trained in software engineering and currently works in data management and software</a> <a data-t="449">development in a research lab, writing software for scientists to use for research purposes.</a> <a data-t="455">Ash is writing a package named Borker.</a> <a data-t="459">Borker creates maps indicating density of dogs being walked in certain areas of a city</a> <a data-t="464">at certain times.</a> <a data-t="465">Ash hopes that Borker will be used for both public dog health tracking, as well as integration</a> <a data-t="471">with the dog social media network, Snoot Bork.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="475">However, Ash hasn't worked much with maps lately, and Ash needs to know the best approaches</a> <a data-t="480">in R to achieve their goals.</a> <a data-t="483">What Ash does is go and asks on Twitter and some local Slack communities for their recommendations.</a> <a data-t="489">They are able to narrow down the options available based on what they and their friends understand</a> <a data-t="495">about the project requirements.</a> <a data-t="498">Ash goes ahead and references the relevant documentation for each option, and given their</a> <a data-t="503">breadth of knowledge, Ash can quickly identify the relevant functions and how to connect</a> <a data-t="508">them together to implement mapping in the context of Borker.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="514">One of Ash's colleagues is named Avery.</a> <a data-t="517">Avery is a graduate student with some formal coursework in data science, but is still learning</a> <a data-t="521">the best methods for optimizing workflows in R.</a> <a data-t="526">Avery is developing documentation for a group of summer interns and wants to streamline</a> <a data-t="531">the process of creating projects with them.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="535">Avery goes and asks Ash, who recommends the package, use this.</a> <a data-t="540">Avery is really excited, goes and takes a look at the main documentation page, and is</a> <a data-t="545">really intimidated.</a> <a data-t="547">They haven't worked with a package like this before, and they also haven't worked much</a> <a data-t="551">with Git or GitHub, which is one of the really awesome features involved in use this.</a> <a data-t="556">They especially aren't comfortable in their ability to use this package and help other</a> <a data-t="562">people use the tool as well.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="565">So Avery comes to my office hours and asks for help.</a> <a data-t="569">I point them towards a project from our colleagues' lab that actually applies use this.</a> <a data-t="577">This particular project is a template that includes working with GitHub, starting an</a> <a data-t="584">analysis, and explaining why their particular approach works for data analysis projects.</a> <a data-t="591">The explanation Avery needed didn't just include the code to run, but an example of</a> <a data-t="597">how all the pieces would fit together in a final project.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="602">Avery is really excited about this breakthrough and uses the package to support their summer</a> <a data-t="606">intern, Quinn.</a> <a data-t="609">Quinn is an undergraduate in biology who has recently started learning computational methods.</a> <a data-t="614">Quinn thought my intro R class was quite intuitive and is eager to apply their skills to their</a> <a data-t="619">summer project.</a></p>

<h3>Prioritizing support and documentation</h3>

<p><a data-t="681">These of course are the problems with which we all grapple on a nearly daily basis.</a> <a data-t="687">How do we prioritize how to exert our energy, especially when we have different goals with</a> <a data-t="692">competing interests, and it's not clear whether or not the effort we're exerting is going</a> <a data-t="697">to make a huge difference in the long run?</a> <a data-t="700">These questions include things like, do I write a vignette or add a new feature to the</a> <a data-t="705">package I'm developing?</a> <a data-t="707">Do I keep trying to get this function to work for my analysis, or do I give up and try something</a> <a data-t="712">else?</a> <a data-t="713">How do I even find the help I need to address this key issue?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="718">As it turns out, even small differences in how support materials are developed can make</a> <a data-t="724">the difference between someone struggling and wrestling with implementing a new approach</a> <a data-t="729">or being able to take the ball and run with it.</a> <a data-t="733">This is because, in my experience, learning specific technical skills is easy.</a> <a data-t="741">However, once you've learned how a technical skill generally operates, being able to apply</a> <a data-t="747">that to your own problem is much more difficult.</a> <a data-t="752">The usefulness of training and documentation depends on both what information is provided</a> <a data-t="758">and how it's delivered.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="762">Now that we have an idea of the problem we face, how do we actually meet these needs?</a> <a data-t="766">So far, I've made some generally vague references to training materials and documentation.</a> <a data-t="772">I think it's worth highlighting what some of these options include.</a> <a data-t="776">And if you'd like more information on detailed descriptions of types of materials, head over</a> <a data-t="781">to the resources I've included in </a><a href="/software/rstudio/">RStudio</a><a data-t="781">'s collection of conference materials.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="786">First, manuals.</a> <a data-t="788">We have a baseline expectation in the R community that a standardized user manual documenting</a> <a data-t="794">features of a package is included.</a> <a data-t="797">This particular documentation provides basic information like how does a function work</a> <a data-t="802">and what options are available for working with the function.</a> <a data-t="805">In my experience, manuals are most useful for expert users or people who have some familiarity</a> <a data-t="812">with how the particular type of function can work.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="816">Another type of material is vignettes.</a> <a data-t="820">Vignettes answer questions like how do functions work together to answer general questions.</a> <a data-t="825">These tend to be more like demos or tutorials for implementing code, which ends up making</a> <a data-t="830">them much more accessible for a broad variety of users.</a> <a data-t="835">A third type of support material are example projects or code.</a> <a data-t="840">These demonstrate what an applicable, authentic project incorporating a tool looks like.</a> <a data-t="847">And these end up being very crucial, especially for practitioners.</a> <a data-t="851">Fourth, formally structured, in-depth short courses are generally in highest demand for</a> <a data-t="857">novices and often something that most people are familiar with.</a> <a data-t="861">They would like someone to tell them what the answers are.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="865">Now, each of these different approaches requires a different amount of effort to both find,</a> <a data-t="871">if you're wanting to learn from it, or develop, if you are creating it.</a> <a data-t="876">And sometimes these resources do blend together.</a> <a data-t="879">They're also generally supplemented by conversations with colleagues, which I've found is one of</a> <a data-t="884">the most valuable ways to fill in knowledge gaps.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="889">Now that we've considered how types of support vary based on levels of expertise, let's return</a> <a data-t="894">to Ash.</a> <a data-t="896">Ash is excited that their package is nearing completion, so they share the manual with</a> <a data-t="901">Avery and Quinn.</a> <a data-t="903">Avery has trouble understanding how some of the Borker functions work, but learns a lot</a> <a data-t="908">in the process of trying to figure it out.</a> <a data-t="911">Quinn doesn't understand any of it, including that Ash actually wrote the package, and then</a> <a data-t="920">Quinn tries to Google it to find more information.</a> <a data-t="923">They come across search results for a Chinese death metal band by the same name, and Ash</a> <a data-t="928">ultimately regrets not Googling the name before deciding on it for their package.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="932">At this point, Ash has a choice, continue the package development to integrate with</a> <a data-t="937">Snoot Bork, or make it clearer what the package is doing right now.</a> <a data-t="942">Ash decides to ask Avery to collaborate on explaining how functions fit together more</a> <a data-t="948">clearly in a vignette.</a> <a data-t="950">They are supported by another of Ash's colleagues, who have developed some code using Borker,</a> <a data-t="956">which Ash is happy to reference in the documentation for the package.</a> <a data-t="960">Quinn keeps attending meetings and learns a lot about the process of code collaboration,</a> <a data-t="964">and eventually even clarifies a few instructions in the manual.</a></p>

<h3>Guidance without a leash</h3>

<p><a data-t="968">This story from my colleagues is one example of the process of developing documentation.</a> <a data-t="974">What does it mean in broader practice, though?</a> <a data-t="977">There are countless online classes, tutorials, demos, and other resources that can help you</a> <a data-t="982">learn R.</a> <a data-t="983">These are approaches that walk us through the process of some coding tasks,</a> <a data-t="989">essentially leading you by a leash.</a> <a data-t="992">Given that availability, why do people keep coming into my classroom?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="997">What I've learned really makes or breaks progression to higher levels of expertise</a> <a data-t="1001">is access to information that helps fill in the biggest gaps in our knowledge.</a> <a data-t="1007">My job as a trainer is to provide guidance that fills in those gaps, which allows learners</a> <a data-t="1013">to keep moving in the same direction, but without a leash, so that they have guidance,</a> <a data-t="1020">but more importantly, autonomy.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="1007">My job as a trainer is to provide guidance that fills in those gaps, which allows learners</a> <a data-t="1013">to keep moving in the same direction, but without a leash, so that they have guidance,</a> <a data-t="1020">but more importantly, autonomy.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="1023">So while I'm developing support materials, I consider identifying what knowledge is assumed</a> <a data-t="1030">in the materials I'm developing, as well as collecting curated examples of code that</a> <a data-t="1036">apply methods commonly desired in my community.</a> <a data-t="1040">In fact, one of the most effective things we can do is share high-quality training materials</a> <a data-t="1046">with the people for whom they are most suited.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1051">The ecosystem of tools available in the R community really reflects the interests of</a> <a data-t="1056">the people comprising that community.</a> <a data-t="1059">With so many tools to maintain and develop, is it worth developing and maintaining support</a> <a data-t="1064">materials, too?</a> <a data-t="1066">Well, we share our code so other people can benefit from our hard work, and to increase</a> <a data-t="1072">the impact of our efforts.</a> <a data-t="1075">If sharing our code results in a multiplier effect for our effort, then making it easier</a> <a data-t="1080">for people to use the code multiplies it yet again.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1085">At a time when disparities in the world feel more stark than ever, access to information</a> <a data-t="1090">makes a huge difference in promoting equity for developing these skills.</a> <a data-t="1096">These values are why we support open science projects, and that means considering how even</a> <a data-t="1101">our small actions can continue to uphold these values.</a> <a data-t="1106">In many cases, we don't even have to create these materials ourselves.</a> <a data-t="1111">Knowing what resources exist and helping raise their visibility can have a huge impact, too.</a></p>

<h3>Advice for every level</h3>

<p><a data-t="1119">Regardless of what level of expertise you think best describes you, please consider</a> <a data-t="1123">the following.</a> <a data-t="1125">For those of us beginning to code in R, it's likely we'll encounter a piece of documentation</a> <a data-t="1131">we don't understand.</a> <a data-t="1133">That's okay, and it's not our fault.</a> <a data-t="1136">That information probably isn't written to be accessible for people with our type</a> <a data-t="1141">of expertise.</a> <a data-t="1142">There's almost certainly a better resource out there, or materials that can help us bridge</a> <a data-t="1147">that gap.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1149">For those of us who are practitioners, worrying that, even if our code works, it just isn't</a> <a data-t="1155">good enough, it's okay.</a> <a data-t="1158">Every expert coder has been where we are now.</a> <a data-t="1161">There's also no requirement that we keep learning more advanced skills, but the community</a> <a data-t="1165">is here to help us, and it'll probably be very satisfying to learn.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1171">Finally, those of us who are experts developing R packages, unsure how to make the information</a> <a data-t="1177">accessible to a broad audience, it's okay.</a> <a data-t="1181">This is a great opportunity for us to collaborate with someone with a different type of expertise.</a> <a data-t="1186">These relationships can help us share our tools more broadly while supporting other</a> <a data-t="1192">community members in advancing their own skills.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1197">For all of us struggling to communicate with people who possess different knowledge and</a> <a data-t="1201">skills, remember this is something everyone confronts.</a> <a data-t="1206">Given that we're all continually learning things, and yes, also forgetting things periodically,</a> <a data-t="1213">our own knowledge and that of those around us is constantly changing too, making for</a> <a data-t="1218">a sometimes rapidly moving target.</a> <a data-t="1220">One of the best things we can do for each other is understand that we can't expect</a> <a data-t="1225">a resource to be one size fits all, and that helping someone else learn is a way of supporting</a> <a data-t="1231">the entire community, as well as yourself in the future.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="1220">One of the best things we can do for each other is understand that we can't expect</a> <a data-t="1225">a resource to be one size fits all, and that helping someone else learn is a way of supporting</a> <a data-t="1231">the entire community, as well as yourself in the future.</a></blockquote>
