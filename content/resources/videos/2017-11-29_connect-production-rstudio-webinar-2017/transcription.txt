<h3>Resource management</h3>

<p><a data-t="575">So that's a bit about user management and we can transition into resource management now</a> <a data-t="578">which is a bit more of a complex topic.</a> <a data-t="578">So one of the most common questions that we get around Connect is the idea of resource budgeting or how large should</a> <a data-t="582">my server be, and the problem or the difficulty in answering this question is</a> <a data-t="585">that the requirements here almost depend entirely on what your users are deploying.</a> <a data-t="591">So if you just have a couple of simple documents that you know are a couple of simple dashboards that are updated once a day that people are going</a> <a data-t="598">to access on your Connect server, you could probably get away with some running this on you know something like very very small even you know a Raspberry</a> <a data-t="601">Pi could probably handle that kind of workload.</a> <a data-t="605">However if you're doing you know very intensive Shiny applications that are doing you know genomic analysis</a> <a data-t="609">on multiple gigabytes of data, then your server requirements are going to be much much larger.</a> <a data-t="616">And so ideally if you if you have the luxury of being in a virtualized environment where you can kind of scale up or down a server that</a> <a data-t="620">would probably be your best bet.</a> <a data-t="623">But otherwise if not you can kind of run a proof of concept and see what the hardware requirements are given the</a> <a data-t="628">applications and the types of work that your that your users are publishing to</a> <a data-t="631">the server, and that's really the best way to kind of get a feel for what your</a> <a data-t="635">requirements should be in terms of hardware for the server.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="641">So in terms of the philosophy of Connect, on-demand requests are largely what we're</a> <a data-t="644">servicing when when requests are coming in, and those are just going to be service-best efforts.</a> <a data-t="650">So you know as a request comes in for a Shiny application we're going to do our best to spin up that Shiny application and hope that</a> <a data-t="654">there's enough memory available on the server.</a> <a data-t="658">So but there are some knobs and some tuning that you can do to cap the resource requirements available to</a> <a data-t="662">particular applications or different different use cases.</a></p>
