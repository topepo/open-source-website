<h3>Principle one: start with a purpose</h3>

<p><a data-t="234">So we were transitioning from legacy systems to open source tools.</a> <a data-t="238">And when you as an organization go through such a big transformation, frustration will occur naturally.</a> <a data-t="244">So when we introduced R to my colleagues, the team found overwhelmed by the endless tutorials and the internal R courses they had to take.</a> <a data-t="253">And I could see their frustration.</a> <a data-t="255">But there's one thing.</a> <a data-t="256">If you learn an open source language, universal struggle is real.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="261">The sheer volume of resources you have to deal with can paralyze you and not empower.</a> <a data-t="268">But if you don't know what you're trying to solve, the sea of options actually become a swamp.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="276">So where did we even start?</a> <a data-t="278">Where did our team start this journey?</a> <a data-t="281">So here's one thing I realized.</a> <a data-t="284">Learning R isn't about mastering syntax.</a> <a data-t="286">It's about solving real problems.</a> <a data-t="288">But when you stare at your blank console, the possibilities overwhelm you.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="294">So what does your team need to do?</a> <a data-t="296">Well, your team needs to start with a purpose.</a> <a data-t="300">And in order to start with a purpose, we need to ask two really key questions before diving into any R code.</a> <a data-t="308">First, what does your team actually need?</a> <a data-t="311">And secondly, who can guide you through it?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="315">So let's break this starting with a purpose down.</a> <a data-t="318">There are three components to it.</a> <a data-t="319">First, the first one is focus on your team needs.</a> <a data-t="323">Ask the following questions.</a> <a data-t="325">Are you all new to R?</a> <a data-t="327">Do you have someone with experience in your team?</a> <a data-t="331">Or you are bringing in a new open source expert?</a> <a data-t="334">What tasks do you repeat every day?</a> <a data-t="337">Are you cleaning data, visualization trends, automating reports?</a> <a data-t="340">You need to understand what your day-to-day tasks are.</a> <a data-t="343">What data sets are you working with?</a> <a data-t="345">How big are they?</a> <a data-t="346">And what makes them unique?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="349">So the answer isn't learning everything.</a> <a data-t="351">It's about prioritizing.</a> <a data-t="353">For the healthcare data set, we didn't need a full R course.</a> <a data-t="356">We needed a script to handle the memory constraints we were dealing with.</a> <a data-t="360">A quick fix that saved hours of frustration.</a> <a data-t="363">So as I said, instead of forcing my colleague to learn R from scratch, I shared a solution tailored to the problem at hand with them.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="371">And that is where the second key comes in.</a> <a data-t="374">Find your R champion.</a> <a data-t="376">As I stated at the beginning of this talk, I was the pebble that started the first ripple.</a> <a data-t="380">And because I had prior R knowledge, I became the go-to person for my colleagues to ask questions, to help them troubleshoot error messages in R.</a> <a data-t="390">So I became what I like to refer to our R champion.</a> <a data-t="394">I became the person who helped my colleagues understand the language, explain its nuances, and I also tried to encourage them to shift the mindset and think like an open source coder.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="406">So here's the secret to your R champion.</a> <a data-t="407">An R champion is your anchor.</a> <a data-t="409">They don't just teach code, they teach how to think in R.</a> <a data-t="413">So my recommendation for every team that is trying to start this journey, find your R champion.</a> <a data-t="420">Someone who can explain the why behind R, not just the how.</a> <a data-t="424">Your champion doesn't just write code.</a> <a data-t="426">They help you scope solutions.</a> <a data-t="428">They will translate how to think in R to help you think in R.</a> <a data-t="431">And they will act as a translator between your team, your IT team, and any other departments within your organization.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="439">But here's also one thing I learned.</a> <a data-t="442">Even a champion cannot guide you through this alone.</a> <a data-t="445">You also need to shift your team's mindset from thinking of R as a tool to solve one problem to seeing it as a way to think differently.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="462">Shifting mindset means embracing open source as a collaborative mindset.</a> <a data-t="467">It's about seeing code not as a rigid script, but as a modular usable solution.</a> <a data-t="473">It's about learning from the community.</a> <a data-t="475">It's about learning how tools like GitLab, Stack Overflow, or the interactive features in </a><a href="/software/rstudio/">RStudio</a><a data-t="475"> work.</a> <a data-t="482">It's about iterating, failing, learning new methods, and actually unlearning old habits.</a> <a data-t="489">This shift isn't about efficiency.</a> <a data-t="492">It's about fostering a culture where your team isn't just using open source tools, but thinking like an open source coder.</a> <a data-t="501">That means asking questions, experimenting, failing, relearning, and trusting the community to help you grow.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="510">So here's my call.</a> <a data-t="512">In order to make a successful transition, don't start with the tool, start with your purpose.</a> <a data-t="519">Define your team's needs, find someone to guide you through it, and don't forget, shift mindset and let open source thinking become your team's new language.</a> <a data-t="529">Because when you prioritize real problem over abstract learning and trust the power of collaboration, you turn frustration into progress.</a></p>

<h3>Principle two: collaborate, do not compete</h3>

<p><a data-t="541">And that brings me to my second principle, collaborate, do not compete.</a> <a data-t="547">As I mentioned before, we work with healthcare data, and that data often contains information diagnosis codes, and those codes can change over a year.</a> <a data-t="556">Hence, if you do any trending analysis, you need to account for those coding differences.</a> <a data-t="561">And it happened that I had to solve a problem like this, and I was asking for the code from another team.</a> <a data-t="567">Yes, the code was still in the proprietary software language, but I could still use it as a base to figure it out in R.</a> <a data-t="575">However, I was told, no, we don't share codes.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="578">But I came from an environment where I was used to sharing my codes.</a> <a data-t="582">I was used to discussing error messages with my colleagues, and I was used to improve my codes to get it.</a> <a data-t="588">So I was taken back a little bit.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="593">So why do I share this story with you?</a> <a data-t="595">Because we need to talk about something that feels counterintuitive in a world where individual achievement often is celebrated.</a> <a data-t="603">We need to talk about collaboration.</a> <a data-t="605">Open source drives on sharing knowledge, but here's the truth.</a> <a data-t="609">Most of us have spent years working in silos.</a> <a data-t="612">We have been taught over and over to guard our codes, to guard our insights, and to not share our mistakes.</a> <a data-t="619">But what if I told you that the fastest way to solve a coding problem isn't by working alone or by working in silos?</a></p>

<p><a data-t="629">So here is how you shift from fear of sharing to embracing collaboration.</a> <a data-t="635">So first, we need to teach interdependent thinking.</a> <a data-t="639">We need to stop seeing knowledge as a resource to hoard and start viewing it as something to build together.</a> <a data-t="646">Normalize asking questions, build the trust among each other, help each other out when you try to solve a coding problem, be brave.</a> <a data-t="654">For me, asking for help isn't a weakness, it's a superpower.</a> <a data-t="659">It's how we turn frustration into growth and build trust.</a></p>

<h3>Principle three: learning by doing</h3>

<p><a data-t="789">And that leads me to my last principle.</a> <a data-t="792">Learn.</a> <a data-t="794">And because a lot of issues you and your colleagues will encounter in your learning journey,</a> <a data-t="798">it will be actually solving problems on the task at hand for your project.</a> <a data-t="804">So my last point to successful transition to R is learning by doing.</a> <a data-t="809">The best way to master R is through projects that matter, not just theory.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="815">But the struggle here is to having to learn R, having to learn a new coding language,</a> <a data-t="821">while also you have to do all the other work.</a> <a data-t="824">And often the resistance you and your colleagues will encounter here is putting off the learning.</a> <a data-t="829">Because there is no time.</a> <a data-t="831">We had the same challenge.</a> <a data-t="834">We were fully working on our projects, but we were also expected to finish our R conversion</a> <a data-t="839">or to finish our R courses.</a> <a data-t="843">And it would have been helpful if we all had even more time.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="849">So how do you embrace the newly established way of thinking in R to record your projects</a> <a data-t="855">when time is limited?</a> <a data-t="856">First, you need to realize you're learning professionally and not academically.</a> <a data-t="861">Academia teaches theory.</a> <a data-t="863">The course you take teach you theory.</a> <a data-t="866">But real world work demands application.</a> <a data-t="870">When you learn R for a project, you're not just solving code.</a> <a data-t="876">You're solving a business problem.</a> <a data-t="878">You're solving a data challenge.</a> <a data-t="880">And you're creating a workflow that matters.</a> <a data-t="883">You're creating a workflow that matters and hopefully a workflow that can be reused in the future.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="889">So we talked about shifting the mindset to think like an open source coder.</a> <a data-t="894">We also need to shift your mindset in terms of learning.</a> <a data-t="898">You're no longer in a classroom.</a> <a data-t="899">You're actually in a laboratory.</a> <a data-t="902">So focus on what your project needs.</a> <a data-t="905">Learn what you need for your day to day tasks first.</a> <a data-t="908">No more and no less.</a> <a data-t="910">Later on, once you're more confident in R coding, go and explore what else is possible.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="917">And that leads me to my second point.</a> <a data-t="919">Practice by working on projects.</a> <a data-t="923">Here's a secret.</a> <a data-t="924">Advanced coding skills come from experience.</a> <a data-t="928">And experience does not come from taking course after course after course.</a> <a data-t="933">Every unanticipated error, ever data wrangling hurting,</a> <a data-t="938">treat it as a lesson because that's what it is.</a> <a data-t="940">It's a lesson.</a> <a data-t="942">You won't master R by studying all the packages or every function within a package.</a> <a data-t="947">You will do it by coding through your projects.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="951">And that leads me to my third point.</a> <a data-t="954">Allocate fixed time for learning by working on your projects.</a> <a data-t="958">You need to create time to do this.</a> <a data-t="961">So how did our team do this?</a> <a data-t="962">My managers block three hours every Friday for R learning.</a> <a data-t="967">For about a year and a half.</a> <a data-t="969">During those times, there were no other meetings and also there shouldn't be any interruptions.</a> <a data-t="974">And to put this into perspective, that's about 10% of your time during a week.</a> <a data-t="978">But it was enough to build muscle memory.</a> <a data-t="982">So if your team, if you're on a team that goes through this journey, push for this.</a> <a data-t="986">Protect your time because it will help you learn faster, make fewer mistakes and turn projects into progress.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="994">So here's my challenge for you.</a> <a data-t="997">Next week, block three hours, start the project and stop waiting for the perfect time.</a> <a data-t="1002">Because the only way to master R is to actually use it.</a> <a data-t="1006">But let me be clear.</a> <a data-t="1008">This is not about perfection.</a> <a data-t="1011">It is about progress.</a> <a data-t="1013">Every line of code you write for your team's project is a step towards becoming an R expert.</a> <a data-t="1019">You don't need to master every package or every function.</a> <a data-t="1022">You need to solve the problem at hand.</a></p>
