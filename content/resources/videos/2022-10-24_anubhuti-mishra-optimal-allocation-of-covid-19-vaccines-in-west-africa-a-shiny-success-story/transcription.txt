<h3>The vaccine allocation problem</h3>

<p><a data-t="74">So in 2021, we were approached by a couple of West African countries with a very interesting</a> <a data-t="83">problem that there was an influx of millions of vaccines from COVAX, Gavi, World Bank,</a> <a data-t="91">USA, into African countries, but suddenly they found themselves with more than enough</a> <a data-t="99">vaccines to meet their needs, but they found themselves very lacking when it comes to supply</a> <a data-t="105">chain issues.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="108">Just the infrastructure around sending vaccines across the country, vaccinating millions of</a> <a data-t="113">adults in a period of less than a year or one and a half years was a huge problem.</a> <a data-t="121">And we found with a little bit of research and talking to our country partners, we found</a> <a data-t="126">that a lot of countries ended up wasting vaccines because they were just not able to</a> <a data-t="132">administer those vaccines before their expiration date.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="136">So this was a very interesting problem, very different from what we were seeing in countries</a> <a data-t="141">like USA or European countries.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="146">So we thought, okay, this is a great opportunity.</a> <a data-t="149">Let's build an allocation model.</a> <a data-t="151">Let's build an optimization model where we'll take into consideration a lot of factors like</a> <a data-t="157">time to expiration, travel time between distribution centers to vaccine sites, how much staff you</a> <a data-t="166">have available at vaccine sites to hesitancy and everything.</a> <a data-t="171">We had a whole model in place.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="174">But then we faced reality.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="178">So this is what the vaccination sites looked like in country.</a> <a data-t="184">This is a picture of a mobile vaccine site from Cote d'Ivoire or Ivory Coast.</a> <a data-t="191">And we found that data was being collected across different platforms.</a> <a data-t="196">Some sites had tablets.</a> <a data-t="199">Some sites had paper-based data entry.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="224">This is what vaccine storage at site looked like.</a> <a data-t="228">Some of the better-equipped sites had a data entry person with a tablet who was able to</a> <a data-t="233">enter patient-level data into an app.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="238">So essentially, we found ourselves in an extremely low-quality data environment.</a> <a data-t="243">So none of the pillars that our beautiful optimization models could stand on existed.</a> <a data-t="250">We found that, like I said, data was being collected across different platforms.</a> <a data-t="256">There was a lot of fragmentation across the data collection chain.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="262">Then we found that the stock data was being collected and managed in an Excel-based tool.</a> <a data-t="268">There were four different types of vaccines.</a> <a data-t="272">There was J&J and Sinopharm and Pfizer and AstraZeneca.</a> <a data-t="277">There was no data on healthcare staff available at site level.</a> <a data-t="282">So we suddenly found ourselves in a very challenging environment.</a></p>

<h3>Adapting the approach</h3>

<p><a data-t="291">So this is where we decided that we slightly decided to change the final goal of our work.</a> <a data-t="301">So we were no longer looking at building an exact optimization model that should give</a> <a data-t="306">you the exactly correct data on how to allocate vaccines.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="314">We figured that currently the Ministry of Health is just haphazardly using whatever</a> <a data-t="321">information they have at hand more often than not.</a> <a data-t="325">It is just based on what is the usage rate at a vaccination site or eligible population.</a> <a data-t="333">They are not looking at any other factors.</a> <a data-t="336">So we decided to use some open-source data, pull in whatever proprietary data we could</a> <a data-t="341">get from the government, and combine all of that to create a vaccine allocation tool.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="350">So this is a list of all sorts of open-source data that we pulled into our tool.</a> <a data-t="357">We pulled in population data from World Pop.</a> <a data-t="361">There was data on healthcare sites across sub-Saharan Africa.</a> <a data-t="366">Then we were able to use a bunch of R packages to calculate travel time, to calculate catchment</a> <a data-t="371">area for vaccine sites.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="376">That combined with a lot of country data.</a> <a data-t="380">So even the list of data sets listed here were not available at the start.</a> <a data-t="387">We had to, and we're still working with the country a lot to get these data sets.</a> <a data-t="395">They started collecting data on healthcare staff available at the site level after we</a> <a data-t="402">requested them to share that data with us.</a> <a data-t="404">We are working with them on how to collect these data sets in a routine and a systematic</a> <a data-t="413">format.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="415">So then this was our approach to creating this vaccine allocation tool, where we would</a> <a data-t="422">combine a bunch of open-source data with country-specific data and create this tool, which gives you</a> <a data-t="428">some sort of an estimate for how to allocate vaccines.</a> <a data-t="431">We are no longer asking the Ministry of Health to look at this tool as end-all, be-all for</a> <a data-t="438">allocating vaccines.</a> <a data-t="439">They should be looking at their ground realities and comparing with the suggestions from this</a> <a data-t="445">model and then making their decisions on how to allocate the tool.</a></p>

<h3>Rollout and optimization constraints</h3>

<p><a data-t="898">We still haven't launched it.</a> <a data-t="900">Next month we are going to have a training session with our Ministry of Health and their</a> <a data-t="907">COVID-19 team in-country.</a> <a data-t="911">And once this is done, we will be transitioning it off to the team so that they can update</a> <a data-t="917">and use this tool for making decisions, allocation decisions going forward.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="925">So like I said, this is essentially a linear optimization problem where our main focus</a> <a data-t="933">was our decision variable was to figure out how many vaccines should be allocated to each</a> <a data-t="939">site and the optimization objective was to minimize wastage and while maximizing the</a> <a data-t="948">number of people vaccinated in the eligible population.</a> <a data-t="952">And we ran into, we started with a smaller list of constraints, but over time we ran</a> <a data-t="957">into, as you work with the data, you figure out some additional constraints.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="965">So it included that we had to rank prioritized batches based on their time to expiration.</a> <a data-t="973">So not only did we have four different types of vaccines, for each type of vaccine you</a> <a data-t="978">could have multiple batches.</a> <a data-t="980">So we had to make sure that the model starts to allocate the batches that expire first</a> <a data-t="988">before it allocates batches that have more time to expiration.</a> <a data-t="993">Then we, yeah, we had to, there were a lot of constraints around expiration date, making</a> <a data-t="999">sure that it's delivered and administered before its expiration date, that there are</a> <a data-t="1006">enough people to administer those vaccines at the site.</a></p>
