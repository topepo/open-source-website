<h3>The historical problem: non-decimal currency</h3>

<p><a data-t="650">So let me start— before I get into what that is, let me discuss what the historical</a> <a data-t="656">issue that I was seeking to solve is.</a> <a data-t="659">So I'm interested in the history of accounting.</a> <a data-t="662">And that might not sound that interesting.</a> <a data-t="665">Or you may be an accountant.</a> <a data-t="666">I don't know.</a> <a data-t="667">But the reason I'm interested in the history of accounting is because accounting deals</a> <a data-t="673">with social relationships.</a> <a data-t="675">It's not the accounting part, it's the social relationships.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="679">And why accounting is particularly interesting for me, in my context, is that in the early</a> <a data-t="686">modern period, in most of the low countries where I do my work, inheritance was perfectly</a> <a data-t="692">equal, meaning that all male and female heirs received the exact same amount of inheritance.</a> <a data-t="701">And when you're dealing with merchant families, that means accounting has to go into figuring</a> <a data-t="705">out how much the estate is worth and then how much each individual person gets.</a> <a data-t="712">And it has to be exactly right.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="724">So merchant families inheritance involves accounting.</a> <a data-t="728">Now one problem with that, other than it's accounting, is that the currency that they</a> <a data-t="734">use, as you can see on the right, is essentially pounds, shillings, and pence.</a> <a data-t="745">One pound, if you can't remember before pre-decimalization, equals 20 shillings.</a> <a data-t="752">One shilling is equal to 12 pence.</a> <a data-t="757">So this creates a number of problems because it's not decimal.</a> <a data-t="764">Firstly, arithmetic calculations are cumbersome.</a> <a data-t="768">I never thought I would be doing so much arithmetic as a historian.</a> <a data-t="772">So I have pages and pages of just doing handwritten arithmetic.</a></p>
