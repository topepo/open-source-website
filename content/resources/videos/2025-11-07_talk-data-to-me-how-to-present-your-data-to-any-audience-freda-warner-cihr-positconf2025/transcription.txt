<h3>Identifying your main message</h3>

<p><a data-t="173">And a lot of speakers this week have done something similar.</a> <a data-t="175">They'll be talking and they'll say,</a> <a data-t="177">if you're only going to take away one thing from this presentation,</a> <a data-t="179">and they'll just go out and say it.</a> <a data-t="181">And that's really the idea here.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="183">And unlike this other story where it was about another group,</a> <a data-t="186">I've also, of course, been guilty of kind of poor communication</a> <a data-t="188">in my presentations.</a> <a data-t="190">One particularly vivid memory for me was very early in my career</a> <a data-t="193">it was my thesis proposal.</a> <a data-t="195">So it was a very important day.</a> <a data-t="197">And I was presenting to a very smart and important group.</a> <a data-t="199">And I just got mixed up as to what the goal of my presentation was.</a> <a data-t="203">Instead of identifying a message and kind of telling a story,</a> <a data-t="206">I just wanted to show them everything I'd done.</a> <a data-t="208">I wanted to show them all my data and all my analyses</a> <a data-t="211">and that I'd left no stone unturned.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="214">And ultimately I wanted to impress them.</a> <a data-t="217">And I wanted them to think I was smart.</a> <a data-t="219">And I thought maybe if I provided so much information,</a> <a data-t="221">they'd be less likely to ask a lot of questions at the end</a> <a data-t="224">because questions are scary.</a> <a data-t="226">And as you can imagine, my presentation was just like</a> <a data-t="228">this busy, incoherent mess.</a> <a data-t="230">And I was trying to include so much information</a> <a data-t="232">that I talked way too fast.</a> <a data-t="234">And this all came together and resulted in a ton of questions</a> <a data-t="236">because I couldn't identify my message.</a> <a data-t="238">They couldn't identify my message.</a> <a data-t="240">They didn't know what my proposal was about.</a> <a data-t="242">I wasn't leading anyone anywhere.</a> <a data-t="244">I was just kind of presenting defensively instead.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="247">Because once we identify what our main message is,</a> <a data-t="250">we can then identify what information we need</a> <a data-t="252">to lead our audiences towards it</a> <a data-t="254">and avoid getting lost kind of in this forest.</a> <a data-t="257">And unlike that thesis example that I was just saying,</a> <a data-t="260">you don't want to try and cram in all your data</a> <a data-t="262">and all your analyses into your presentation just in case.</a> <a data-t="265">Because this usually results in our message getting lost.</a> <a data-t="267">And it's not that you're hiding anything from your audience,</a> <a data-t="269">or I hope you're not,</a> <a data-t="271">but in focusing on what's relevant,</a> <a data-t="273">you're getting your message across,</a> <a data-t="274">and they're always welcome to ask about that extra stuff later.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="276">Just like your presentation is kind of one big message,</a> <a data-t="280">you should be focusing on kind of one piece of evidence</a> <a data-t="283">and one smaller message at a time</a> <a data-t="285">to keep your presentation clear and concise.</a> <a data-t="287">Because sometimes less is more.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="289">One of the ways I was able to kind of wean myself off this need</a> <a data-t="292">to overfill and overcomplicate my presentation</a> <a data-t="295">was to have supplementary slides,</a> <a data-t="297">sometimes a lot of supplementary slides.</a> <a data-t="299">Because often my need to overfill came from</a> <a data-t="301">either being so excited to show everything,</a> <a data-t="303">you know, what if they were interested in it,</a> <a data-t="304">or God forbid, what if it came up in the questions</a> <a data-t="306">and I wasn't prepared.</a> <a data-t="307">So having all the supplementary slides</a> <a data-t="309">really helped me to focus on my story,</a> <a data-t="311">knowing that they were there in case I needed them.</a> <a data-t="313">Kind of like, you know, your first aid kit in your backpack</a> <a data-t="315">when you're hiking.</a> <a data-t="316">Good to know it's there, hopefully you won't need it.</a> <a data-t="318">Usually you don't.</a></p>

<h3>Using visuals to support your message</h3>

<p><a data-t="321">And once you have your main message,</a> <a data-t="322">and you know the evidence you need to support it,</a> <a data-t="324">you can start incorporating visuals into your presentation.</a> <a data-t="327">And visuals are our tools.</a> <a data-t="328">Sometimes they're figures, sometimes they're images,</a> <a data-t="330">sometimes they're just text.</a> <a data-t="331">And I'm not going to sit here and be like, you know,</a> <a data-t="333">this data should be with this kind of figure,</a> <a data-t="335">you should use this plot for this kind of data,</a> <a data-t="337">because our work and our preferences can dictate that.</a> <a data-t="340">I mean, I was requested to make a pie chart this year.</a> <a data-t="342">It's fine.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="345">What I more want to focus on is that our visuals</a> <a data-t="346">are there to support us.</a> <a data-t="348">And one of the ways our visuals can help support us</a> <a data-t="350">is by making sure kind of each of these smaller pieces</a> <a data-t="352">of evidence and these smaller messages that we're using,</a> <a data-t="354">we're focusing and emphasizing on one at a time.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="358">And one recommendation I had, again,</a> <a data-t="360">early on in my career was this five by five rule.</a> <a data-t="363">Some of you might have heard of it,</a> <a data-t="364">some of you might have not.</a> <a data-t="365">But basically it just means no more than five bullet points</a> <a data-t="367">per slide, no more than five words per bullet point.</a> <a data-t="369">And I don't like hard rules.</a> <a data-t="370">There are exceptions to everything, including this.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="373">But I do think that this kind of rule helps to remind us</a> <a data-t="375">about two important things with regards to our visuals.</a> <a data-t="378">And that is, like I mentioned,</a> <a data-t="380">our visuals are there to support us.</a> <a data-t="382">Our visuals are not there to do the heavy lifting.</a> <a data-t="384">And you should never be able to send your slide deck</a> <a data-t="386">to someone and have them understand what it's about</a> <a data-t="388">without your accompanying presentation.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="391">The second thing is that our visuals should be kept</a> <a data-t="393">as simple as possible.</a> <a data-t="394">And I don't mean the figures themselves,</a> <a data-t="396">because sometimes you're not going to be able</a> <a data-t="397">to have a simple figure.</a> <a data-t="398">You need a complicated one.</a> <a data-t="399">But if you find yourself trying to include a lot of figures</a> <a data-t="401">on one slide or trying to cram in more and more text,</a> <a data-t="404">you should probably be breaking this slide up</a> <a data-t="405">into multiple slides.</a> <a data-t="407">One, because you're probably not focusing and emphasizing</a> <a data-t="410">on one message at a time.</a> <a data-t="412">And two, because this just helps our audiences</a> <a data-t="413">with short attention spans pay attention.</a> <a data-t="416">Just like our presentation is kind of</a> <a data-t="418">one overarching message,</a> <a data-t="420">each slide should have one concluding thought.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="424">I remember a visiting scholar one time</a> <a data-t="426">coming to do a presentation at my old work.</a> <a data-t="428">And he was supposed to give a 20-minute presentation.</a> <a data-t="430">And he went up to the front, and he plugged in his laptop.</a> <a data-t="432">And some people noticed that he had something like</a> <a data-t="433">180 slides, and they just panicked.</a> <a data-t="436">But his presentation, his visuals were supportive,</a> <a data-t="439">and they were simple.</a> <a data-t="440">It's just he had a lot of kind of repetitions</a> <a data-t="442">where he emphasized different areas or highlighted results,</a> <a data-t="444">and he had a ton of supplementary slides.</a> <a data-t="446">And his presentation was really good.</a> <a data-t="448">Because we shouldn't be afraid of too many slides.</a> <a data-t="450">We should be afraid of really busy slides.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="453">And I think we can all know when we've,</a> <a data-t="456">our visuals have become kind of a hindrance</a> <a data-t="457">instead of helpful.</a> <a data-t="458">Because you pull up your slide, and you start talking,</a> <a data-t="460">and at some point you look at your audience,</a> <a data-t="461">and they're peering at your slide,</a> <a data-t="463">and they're kind of reading it,</a> <a data-t="464">and they're trying to understand it,</a> <a data-t="465">and they've completely stopped listening</a> <a data-t="467">to anything you're saying,</a> <a data-t="468">which might be happening right now.</a> <a data-t="469">Because you've become secondary to your visual.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="472">And even though figures are often preferred</a> <a data-t="474">over kind of big tables or big blocks of text</a> <a data-t="477">to keep your audience's attention,</a> <a data-t="478">what's more important than the type of visual you use</a> <a data-t="481">is that you're walking your audience through</a> <a data-t="483">what it is and what it means.</a> <a data-t="485">This visual, for example, is fine.</a> <a data-t="486">This is on our website.</a> <a data-t="487">You can find it, and it also has the accompanying data</a> <a data-t="489">that was used to make it.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="491">And the data table has funding type rows</a> <a data-t="493">and fiscal year columns,</a> <a data-t="494">and it's just a bunch of numbers.</a> <a data-t="495">And the figure is a much clearer way</a> <a data-t="496">to show these patterns over time.</a> <a data-t="498">But the second I bring it up,</a> <a data-t="499">I should still be kind of leading audiences</a> <a data-t="501">through my presentation.</a> <a data-t="502">I say, you know, here we have figure three, CIHR.</a> <a data-t="505">Again, that's my work,</a> <a data-t="506">Canadian Institutes of Health Research.</a> <a data-t="508">CIHR fiscal year investments by funding type.</a> <a data-t="510">And then I kind of dive right in.</a> <a data-t="512">I say, you know, on the y-axis here</a> <a data-t="513">we have investments in millions of dollars,</a> <a data-t="515">and on the x-axis we have fiscal years.</a> <a data-t="517">And then you go into the data,</a> <a data-t="518">and you tell them what the message</a> <a data-t="519">you want them to take away is.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="553">And lots of times I've been presenting</a> <a data-t="561">to audiences that I thought were very smart,</a> <a data-t="563">I assumed much smarter than I was,</a> <a data-t="565">and it made me really shrink away from leading.</a> <a data-t="568">I thought I shouldn't over-explain things</a> <a data-t="570">that I might look kind of silly</a> <a data-t="571">if I was talking down to the audience.</a> <a data-t="574">But you know your data,</a> <a data-t="575">you've done the analyses,</a> <a data-t="576">and this is your presentation</a> <a data-t="577">that you're supposed to be leading.</a> <a data-t="579">Even though it can be really tempting</a> <a data-t="581">to assume that any figure you present</a> <a data-t="582">is so obvious, that the message is so obvious,</a> <a data-t="585">they haven't been looking at it</a> <a data-t="586">for as long as you have.</a> <a data-t="588">And no matter what,</a> <a data-t="589">you should be walking them through it</a> <a data-t="590">the way I did, kind of axes to data to message.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="593">Similarly, if there's a figure</a> <a data-t="594">you're not gonna walk through,</a> <a data-t="596">don't just throw it on your slide just in case.</a> <a data-t="598">That's when our audiences start doing the peering</a> <a data-t="600">and wandering away from us,</a> <a data-t="601">and that's when our message gets lost.</a> <a data-t="604">You are there to lead,</a> <a data-t="605">and your visuals are there to support you.</a></p>

<h3>Delivering your oral presentation</h3>

<p><a data-t="608">And finally, you've done your visuals,</a> <a data-t="609">you can start working on your oral presentation.</a> <a data-t="613">And your oral presentation job is</a> <a data-t="614">that final step to really get your audience</a> <a data-t="615">to listen to and understand your message,</a> <a data-t="617">and hopefully to get that message remembered.</a> <a data-t="620">And if there's only one thing you can recommend,</a> <a data-t="621">or I can recommend,</a> <a data-t="622">about the oral aspect of your presentation,</a> <a data-t="624">it's to say it out loud at least once,</a> <a data-t="626">ideally many, many times.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="628">We already spoke about articulation.</a> <a data-t="629">They really force you to say it out loud</a> <a data-t="630">before you come here.</a> <a data-t="633">And as you're making your slides,</a> <a data-t="634">you're probably already kind of starting crafting</a> <a data-t="636">what you're gonna say in your head.</a> <a data-t="637">Maybe you're making some slide notes.</a> <a data-t="638">And I know that when I start making slide notes,</a> <a data-t="641">I just tend to write in this weirdly formal way.</a> <a data-t="643">It's how I write, it's how I email.</a> <a data-t="644">I don't know if it's me or if it was academia.</a> <a data-t="647">But the second I start saying it out loud,</a> <a data-t="649">I'm able to identify what doesn't actually sound natural</a> <a data-t="652">or feel natural for me,</a> <a data-t="653">and the areas that I trip over.</a> <a data-t="655">And also if I have an area without kind of a main message,</a> <a data-t="658">I kind of trail off without a concluding thought</a> <a data-t="660">and it helps me to highlight those.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="663">They say you shouldn't write a script for your presentation,</a> <a data-t="665">and I agree,</a> <a data-t="666">but the more times you're able to practice</a> <a data-t="667">saying it out loud,</a> <a data-t="668">the more natural it'll become,</a> <a data-t="669">both in the way you present it,</a> <a data-t="670">but also if there's little hiccups</a> <a data-t="671">like technical difficulties or a phone call,</a> <a data-t="673">or a random question that comes up.</a> <a data-t="675">Practicing it out loud is so important.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="680">Another important aspect of your oral presentation,</a> <a data-t="682">in addition to kind of getting your message across,</a> <a data-t="684">is to really create an environment</a> <a data-t="685">that encourages audience to interact with what you're saying.</a> <a data-t="688">And again, I touched on how questions are terrifying,</a> <a data-t="690">and I'm often really nervous about them</a> <a data-t="691">in my data presentations.</a> <a data-t="693">But an old supervisor of mine used to say</a> <a data-t="694">that questions are a compliment,</a> <a data-t="696">and this is because you want your audience</a> <a data-t="698">to both have understood,</a> <a data-t="699">but also care enough about what you're saying</a> <a data-t="700">to bother asking a question about it.</a> <a data-t="702">And even if they ask those really hard questions,</a> <a data-t="704">that can help maybe identify areas</a> <a data-t="706">that we've missed before it's too late,</a> <a data-t="708">or even kind of indicate</a> <a data-t="709">where we should be heading to next.</a> <a data-t="711">Questions are a good thing.</a></p>

<blockquote><a data-t="693">But an old supervisor of mine used to say</a> <a data-t="694">that questions are a compliment,</a> <a data-t="696">and this is because you want your audience</a> <a data-t="698">to both have understood,</a> <a data-t="699">but also care enough about what you're saying</a> <a data-t="700">to bother asking a question about it.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="713">And in order for an audience to ask questions,</a> <a data-t="715">you of course,</a> <a data-t="716">the most important thing</a> <a data-t="717">is that they understood what you're saying.</a> <a data-t="718">But equally,</a> <a data-t="719">providing this kind of encouraging environment</a> <a data-t="721">where they're comfortable interacting with you</a> <a data-t="723">is just as important.</a> <a data-t="725">And that's kind of done in ways</a> <a data-t="726">that we've already touched on,</a> <a data-t="727">walking through your findings,</a> <a data-t="728">language like,</a> <a data-t="729">see here,</a> <a data-t="730">what this is showing,</a> <a data-t="731">how to get your message across,</a> <a data-t="732">versus, you know,</a> <a data-t="733">obviously this,</a> <a data-t="734">assuming they can interpret everything,</a> <a data-t="735">flipping through slides,</a> <a data-t="736">that can make your audience</a> <a data-t="737">a little too lost,</a> <a data-t="738">or even just too intimidated</a> <a data-t="739">to ask any questions at the end.</a></p>
