<h3>Optimization modeling for supply chain design</h3>

<p><a data-t="549">So how do we do this? How can we answer these questions?</a> <a data-t="553">We can answer them with optimization modeling.</a> <a data-t="553">Optimization modeling is a mathematical technique that allows a practitioner to optimize a set of interconnected decisions where the decisions are constrained by real world limitations.</a> <a data-t="563">Optimization solvers take the decision options formulated as a mathematical model and return the set of decisions that optimizes, minimizes, or maximizes an equation that includes the decisions as inputs.</a> <a data-t="573">This equation is formally known as an objective function.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="578">So in our case, the decisions we need to make are one, how many distribution centers should open and where should we locate them? And two, what customers should align to each distribution center?</a> <a data-t="591">You can see that these decisions, they're interconnected because I can't align a customer to a distribution center in a certain city unless I also open a distribution center in that city.</a> <a data-t="604">And the limitations on our decisions or our constraints, they're one, that all customer demand must be met.</a> <a data-t="609">We may have a minimum or maximum number of distribution centers.</a> <a data-t="615">We may really only realistically open distribution centers in certain locations.</a> <a data-t="619">We may have a maximum volume per distribution center.</a> <a data-t="625">And for certain reasons, we may put a maximum distance between a customer and its servicing distribution center.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="630">So we'll shortly transition to the </a><a href="/software/shiny-r/">Shiny</a><a data-t="630"> app I built for the webinar.</a> <a data-t="634">But first I'll introduce this scenario.</a> <a data-t="638">You are a supply chain data scientist at Heartland Widgets Incorporated.</a> <a data-t="649">Your company is the market leader in widget sales across eight state area of the American Midwest.</a> <a data-t="654">The pandemic-induced shift in consumer demand from services to goods, as well as stay-at-home orders, has led to a boom in demand for your company's flagship product, the at-home widget.</a> <a data-t="663">Based with record demand, the Director of Supply Chain Network Planning has asked you to, quote, reimagine the company's distribution network.</a> <a data-t="673">You are to recommend the where to open Heartland's distribution centers, as well as the customer to distribution center alignment, to achieve a minimal cost.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="684">Heartland Widgets has customers in 117 Midwestern cities, spread from the Dakotas in the west, all the way to my home state of Indiana in the east.</a> <a data-t="690">Each city's demand is proportional to its population.</a> <a data-t="694">So, for example, Chicago, Illinois, which is a big city, you may have heard of it, has more demand than Fargo, North Dakota.</a> <a data-t="705">Heartland is considering 20 cities for potential distribution center sites.</a> <a data-t="710">Because each distribution center will cost $1 million to open, it may not be cost effective to open distribution centers in all 20 cities, but we're keeping our options open.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="720">So, let's get a little bit more technical here.</a> <a data-t="720">The decisions we need to make for Heartland Widgets can be represented by variables.</a> <a data-t="725">When we ask, what customer should we align to which distribution center, this is equivalent to asking a set of yes-no decisions that must be made in concert.</a> <a data-t="734">For each customer city I, should I service it by a distribution center in city J, yes or no?</a> <a data-t="740">In making our decisions, we're limited to having exactly one yes per customer city.</a> <a data-t="746">It has to be serviced by exactly one distribution center.</a> <a data-t="750">Relatedly, we need to make decisions about how many distribution centers to open and where to locate them.</a> <a data-t="755">This is a series of if-then decisions related to our alignment decisions.</a> <a data-t="760">If at least one customer is aligned to a distribution center in, for example, Omaha, Nebraska, then we must open a distribution center in Omaha.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="776">Mathematical optimization and, more specifically, the techniques of linear and integer programming are ideal for making these kinds of interrelated decisions.</a> <a data-t="783">When we use mathematical optimization, including for supply chain design, we're trying to minimize or maximize some equation that is the function of our decisions.</a> <a data-t="792">In this example, Heartland Widgets wants to design the supply chain with the lowest total cost.</a> <a data-t="797">The supply chain costs in this example are, one, the opening costs for distribution centers, which are priced at $1 million each, two, the handling costs of the distribution centers, which is one cent per unit per widget, and three, the transportation costs from distribution centers to customers, which are five cents per unit mile.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="818">Now, a unit mile is moving one unit one mile.</a> <a data-t="818">So, for example, if you have two widgets, two units, and you move them 100 miles, that's two times 100, 200 unit miles.</a> <a data-t="828">At five cents each, that's $10 to move two units 100 miles.</a> <a data-t="833">You add up these three components, cost components, and that leads to the total supply chain costs that we're trying to minimize.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="836">Now, one of the tenets of supply chain design is that these cost components can be inversely related to each other.</a> <a data-t="842">For example, the more distribution centers you have, the closer you are, on average, to your customers.</a> <a data-t="849">And if you're closer to your customers, on average, you're going to spend less on getting the product to them on that transportation cost because it's just closer.</a> <a data-t="861">However, the more distribution centers you have, the more you spend to open them in the first place.</a> <a data-t="866">So, there's an inverse relationship between the distribution center opening cost and the transportation cost to get the product to your customer.</a> <a data-t="877">Optimization modeling helps us figure out these relationships and find the optimal solution that takes into account the relationships between the costs.</a></p>

<h3>Understanding constraints and trade-offs</h3>

<p><a data-t="1324">Now, I'm going to make a small detour before we get into another scenario to explain more what we mean by constraints in supply chain network design.</a> <a data-t="1333">In optimization modeling, constraints are mathematical limitations on what set of decisions you can make that correspond to real life limitations.</a> <a data-t="1342">The more constraints you have in your model and in real life, the worse your objective function becomes.</a> <a data-t="1352">Optimization modeling, including supply chain network design, is an exercise in understanding the trade-offs involved in making constraints more or less restrictive or tighter, which is the term of art.</a> <a data-t="1362">In designing a supply chain, the tighter the constraints, the higher the total cost becomes.</a> <a data-t="1372">Supply chain design is partly an art around understanding the additional expenses associated with tighter constraints.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1381">Next, we'll talk about what a couple of those constraints may look like.</a> <a data-t="1381">So, first, we see that the lowest total supply chain cost occurs when we have four distribution centers in these cities up here.</a> <a data-t="1396">So, what would happen if we actually force our model to have exactly three distribution centers?</a> <a data-t="1406">How will that change our cost? How will that affect what our network looks like?</a> <a data-t="1406">This is actually quite straightforward to see.</a> <a data-t="1406">All we need to do is go back to our minimum and maximum number of distribution center constraints.</a> <a data-t="1416">So, I'm going to change the minimum to three and the maximum to three.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1463">And now we see that instead of four distribution centers, we have the required three.</a> <a data-t="1463">And it's the same thing, except now Indianapolis is gone.</a> <a data-t="1483">So, let's look at these outputs.</a> <a data-t="1483">Our DC opening costs are now $3 million because we only have three distribution centers and they're $1 million a piece.</a> <a data-t="1491">Our handling cost is the same because it's the same for all distribution centers and we have the same amount of volume.</a> <a data-t="1499">But our transportation cost, which was $3.5 million before is now $4.6 million.</a> <a data-t="1511">So, while our distribution center opening costs went down $1 million, our transportation cost went up $1,100,000.</a> <a data-t="1511">And so, our total cost went from $7.5 million to $7.6 million.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1561">Next, we'll see what happens when we enforce a maximum distance constraint between our distribution centers and our customers.</a> <a data-t="1565">This is often the case with food supply chains because food is perishable and you don't want your food outside of a climate controlled warehouse for too long before it gets to a store.</a> <a data-t="1585">And so, oftentimes when designing food supply chains, you would put in a distance constraint.</a></p>
