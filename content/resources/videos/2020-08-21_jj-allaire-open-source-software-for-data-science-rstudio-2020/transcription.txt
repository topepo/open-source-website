<h3>Why free and open source software matters for science</h3>

<p><a data-t="602">So I want to now say, you know, why is free and open source software for science and data science so important? <blockquote><a data-t="659">We sometimes with free software focus too much on the fact that the software has no cost. But the most important thing is that it comes without restrictions.</a></blockquote>

<p><a data-t="665">And they actually the new summarizes kind of the four essential freedoms of free and</a> <a data-t="671">open source software. <h3>RStudio's model: open source sustained by commercial products</h3>

<p><a data-t="1341">So, if you look a little bit at the history of the company,</a> <a data-t="1347">2008, late 2008, I believe, when we started, it was, as I said before, just one or two</a> <a data-t="1357">or three people working on open source software. So we did that, and you can see over the last seven</a> <a data-t="1392">years or so, we've produced a huge amount of open source software. We have, I think,</a> <a data-t="1396">over 250 open source projects that we are active developers on. <p><a data-t="1424">So I think we've figured out a model by which we can still produce open source software, but do it in this kind</a> <a data-t="1439">of sustainable, well-funded way. <h3>Corporations as stewards: the problem of shareholder primacy</h3>

<p><a data-t="1604">So we're pleased with this, but it begs the question, again, I said about</a> <a data-t="1611">corporations, and are we trustworthy? And I would say we're not at face value trustworthy</a> <a data-t="1615">because in today's world, corporations, and I'm going to get into this in a little more</a> <a data-t="1621">detail in a minute, they are pure profit maximizers. So we need to build, we need</a> <a data-t="1660">to make our motivation as clear and transparent as possible, and we need to try to build more</a> <a data-t="1665">real long-term trust, and I think some of that has to do with the nature of corporations,</a> <a data-t="1670">how corporations work, and how maybe corporations actually need to evolve.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1676">So I'm going to go</a> <a data-t="1681">back, this is a little bit elemental, but I think it's not something that I had thought</a> <a data-t="1686">really carefully and critically about until a few years ago. What actually is a corporation?</a> <a data-t="1692">Why do they even exist? And what a corporation is, is actually, it's a virtual person. So</a> <a data-t="1700">it's acting as a person, legally a person, but it's actually, it's constituted by a group</a> <a data-t="1707">of people. So why do we even have corporations? Well, what did we do before corporations?</a> <a data-t="1713">Before corporations, any business that existed was either undertaken by an individual or</a> <a data-t="1719">maybe a partnership of individuals. And so the individuals were actually personally liable</a> <a data-t="1724">for everything the enterprise did. And when a contract was made between a business and</a> <a data-t="1728">another party, it was actually a contract with the individual or the group of individuals.</a> <a data-t="1732">So when someone left a company, the contracts actually had to be renegotiated. Business</a> <a data-t="1736">was really fundamentally person to person. And that meant that anything that a business</a> <a data-t="1741">could accomplish was sort of bounded by what one person's assets could accomplish and what</a> <a data-t="1746">liability one person was willing to accrue.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1748">So as the industrial age progressed, this</a> <a data-t="1753">model sort of didn't seem adequate, and the first corporations were actually formed by</a> <a data-t="1759">Royal Act, the East India Company being one of the most significant examples of that.</a> <a data-t="1765">And then as governments realized that we needed things like bridges, railways, banks, utilities,</a> <a data-t="1771">we need to operate at a different scale than individuals have been able to operate at.</a> <a data-t="1775">So they created this instrument called a corporation that they believed needed this sort of absolving</a> <a data-t="1782">of liability and additional capital that the corporate model provided for.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1787">So if you think about then the essential nature of a corporation, it's an institution that</a> <a data-t="1793">is actually created by government in order to benefit the societies that govern. That</a> <a data-t="1798">was the original purpose of corporations. And this worked very well and served the public</a> <a data-t="1803">benefit in many regards. But if you look at that second bullet, you know, about without</a> <a data-t="1814">fear of personal liability, that actually is a recipe for bad behavior, and we've seen</a> <a data-t="1818">that play out as well. So what we have today with corporations has worked well, but I think</a> <a data-t="1824">we're seeing it crack a little bit in the contemporary world.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1827">So if you think about the legal theories of what the primary purpose of a corporation</a> <a data-t="1832">is, there's actually two competing theories. One is stakeholder. This is kind of the original</a> <a data-t="1838">idea and sort of the original idea that I've been sort of advocating for, which is that</a> <a data-t="1844">the corporation is created by the government and therefore has a social function. And the</a> <a data-t="1848">directors and officers of the corporation should consider all the stakeholders affected</a> <a data-t="1852">by the corporation, employees, the environment, the community, shareholders, kind of looking</a> <a data-t="1857">at everybody's interests when they make decisions.</a></p>

<p><a data-t="1860">There's another theory, which is shareholder primacy, and it's a pretty raw notion, which</a> <a data-t="1867">is the purpose of a corporation is to maximize value for shareholders within the bounds of</a> <a data-t="1872">law. <p><a data-t="1887">And there's been a couple significant cases that establish that, or many cases that have</a> <a data-t="1893">sort of built that up. A couple of the more significant ones was in 1919. <p><a data-t="1922">Another case, a little more contemporary,</a> <a data-t="1922">Revlon was faced with an acquisition proposal that actually the shareholders, it appealed</a> <a data-t="1927">to the shareholders, it was a good deal for them, but the board felt that it was going</a> <a data-t="1931">to not be a good deal for the employees or the bondholders, people who actually held</a> <a data-t="1935">debt. <p><a data-t="1959">And many people find this lacking. It</a> <a data-t="1980">leads back to the question is that we've given these corporations a special legal status.</a> <a data-t="1985">Does that carry any reciprocal obligation to the public good? <p><a data-t="2005">So in response to this, a bunch of states have actually adopted legislation that permit,</a> <a data-t="2011">note permit not require, permit directors to consider things other than shareholder</a> <a data-t="2016">value. <h3>The benefit corporation model</h3>

<p><a data-t="2050">So what can be done to change the system? So</a> <a data-t="2054">I don't know how many of you have heard of the company AND1. Have people heard of AND1?</a> <a data-t="2059">It's a basketball shoe company. They had great, they treated their employees really well, had great</a> <a data-t="2082">benefits. They allocated 5% of their profits to local charities, and significantly they,</a> <a data-t="2089">they're a shoe company so they had overseas factories. They worked to implement a supplier</a> <a data-t="2094">code of conduct to make sure that workers in overseas factories were treated well, safely,</a> <a data-t="2099">had good wages, et cetera. <p><a data-t="2108">So what happened to AND1? Well,</a> <a data-t="2112">they were pretty small in the mid-90s. They took on external investors in 1999, and it</a> <a data-t="2119">turned out that they grew quite a bit over, over that span from 95 to 2001. <p><a data-t="2169">So they got together with a friend of theirs to start a nonprofit called</a> <a data-t="2174">B-Lab, and the idea behind B-Lab was to create a new form of corporate governance. <p><a data-t="2217">I won't read this in detail, but you can see</a> <a data-t="2222">a little bit of the actual legislation behind Delaware Public Benefit Corporations, and</a> <a data-t="2226">it puts a stringent legal requirement on the directors to act in a different way, to not</a> <a data-t="2236">use the shareholder primacy regime.
