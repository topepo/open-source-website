<p><a data-t="1016">So let's talk a little bit about what abstractions are for</a> <a data-t="1019">and what they are not for.</a> <a data-t="1021">So one thing I want to point out that they're not for</a> <a data-t="1024">is they're not for gatekeeping,</a> <a data-t="1026">and I see this being done a lot, right?</a> <a data-t="1028">You're not a real data scientist unless you, you know,</a> <a data-t="1032">PyTorch or deep learning or whatever.</a> <a data-t="1037">Those are all different abstractions, different tools,</a> <a data-t="1041">that are used in certain places to solve certain problems.</a> <a data-t="1043">Those may not be your problems.</a> <a data-t="1046">Then you don't need to know that abstraction.</a> <a data-t="1048">You don't need to be a master of that abstraction.</a></p>

<h3>Floating-point math: a leaky abstraction</h3>

<p><a data-t="1435">So, let's do a fun example</a> <a data-t="1437">about thinking about abstractions.</a> <a data-t="1454">Electricity is a fundamental abstraction for computing,</a> <a data-t="1457">obviously, right?</a> <a data-t="1458">We know this is all powered by electricity.</a> <a data-t="1462">We know, like,</a> <a data-t="1462">we've got gigabit switches in our office,</a> <a data-t="1465">so that's a billion bits of information a second.</a></p>
